A few specific details that make Herculaneum unforgettable.

The boat sheds skeletons. At the southwestern edge of the site, near where the ancient coastline ran. About three hundred skeletons of people who had fled to the waterfront hoping for rescue by sea. They include men, women, children, an entire family huddled together, a Roman soldier with his sword. The bodies were caught by the pyroclastic surge and instantly killed by heat. Most are still in their final positions. Some are in cases for protection. Walking past these sheds is the most direct contact you'll have with the human reality of the eruption.

The carbonized wooden doors at the Suburban Baths. Original Roman wooden doors, blackened but intact, hanging in their original frames. The bronze fittings — hinges, latches, ornamental nails — are all visible. The handles are worn from use. This is direct material evidence of how a Roman bath complex actually functioned. The doors are not protected behind glass. You walk past them on your way through the rooms.

The wooden bed in the House of Galba. A bedroom preserved with a wooden bed frame in its original position, surrounded by wall frescoes. The bed has its slats, its wooden side rails, even traces of the original bedding material. This is what a Roman bedroom looked like — not the schematic version in textbooks. The bed is small, low to the floor, with a slightly raised headrest.

The carbonized food. In the bakeries, on counters, in cooking pots. Loaves of bread bearing the bakers' stamps. Bowls of fruit. The remains of a fish meal — fish bones, a small spoon. The shop signs above doorways. Some still carry the original painted lettering: "Marcus pater" — Marcus's place. "Bonum vinum" — good wine. The town goes on speaking, slightly.

The wall frescoes. Less dramatic than Pompeii's most famous painted rooms, but extensive and well-preserved. Look in the House of the Black Hall and the House of the Charred Furniture. The walls are decorated with intricate fantasy architecture, garden scenes, mythological figures. The colors are still bright. The plaster is still original.

The mosaic floor of the dining room in the House of Neptune and Amphitrite. Vivid blue and gold sea creatures around a central scene of Neptune and his wife. Walk around the room slowly. The mosaic was made by laying individual stone tesserae about a quarter-inch square. The whole thing is about four square meters. Months of work, two thousand years intact.

The Villa of the Papyri site itself — even though you can't enter — is visible from a viewing platform near the entrance. You can see the modern roof structure protecting the partial excavation. Beneath it, the villa's plan is visible: a long row of rooms, a peristyle garden, a library. The papyrus scrolls that once filled its library are now elsewhere; the rooms remain.

A quiet street that's worth walking down, even if there's nothing famous on it: Cardo IV, in the eastern part of the site. The street is preserved with stepping stones, drainage gutters, and shop fronts. Tourists tend to cluster around the famous houses; the side streets are emptier. You can stand here, looking at a Roman street as it was, with the cars and modern Ercolano just out of sight.
