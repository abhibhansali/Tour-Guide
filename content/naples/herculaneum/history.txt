The history of Herculaneum's destruction and rediscovery is intertwined with Pompeii's, but with some distinct features.

The town was founded, by Roman tradition, by Hercules — hence the name Herculaneum. More historically, the original settlement was probably Oscan or Etruscan, dating from at least the sixth century B.C. The Romans absorbed it in the third century B.C. Like Pompeii, it became a small but prosperous coastal town in the Roman Empire — favored by wealthy Romans who built villas here for the sea views.

When Vesuvius erupted on October twenty-fourth, seventy-nine A.D. (recent scholarship has revised this date from the traditional August), Herculaneum was directly downwind from the volcano. The eruption's first phase — the column of ash and pumice that fell on Pompeii — passed mostly to the east. Herculaneum got light pumice fall but not the catastrophic accumulation that crushed Pompeii's roofs. For a few hours, the people of Herculaneum may have thought they would survive.

Then came the pyroclastic surge. Sometime between midnight and dawn on October twenty-fifth, a wave of superheated gas, ash, and rock fragments came down the mountain at over a hundred miles per hour. It hit Herculaneum first because the town was closer to the volcano. The temperature was approximately five hundred degrees Celsius. No one survived. The bodies of those who'd fled to the boat sheds at the seafront were preserved exactly where they fell.

The pyroclastic flow continued past the town and into the sea. Behind it, more ash and mud poured down from Vesuvius. Over the next several days, Herculaneum was buried under approximately sixty feet of pyroclastic rock and ash. The town's exact location was forgotten.

The site was rediscovered in seventeen hundred, when a peasant digging a well struck a Roman marble column. He didn't know what he'd hit. In seventeen thirty-eight, the Bourbon king Charles the Third initiated systematic excavation. Charles sent tunneling teams down through the volcanic rock to find Roman ruins. The tunneling approach — digging horizontal shafts through hardened mud rather than excavating from above — was destructive but produced spectacular finds, particularly the bronzes from the Villa of the Papyri.

Open-air excavation began in the eighteen hundreds. Progress has been slow because Herculaneum lies under the modern town of Ercolano, an active Italian municipality. Excavating means buying and demolishing modern buildings. The Italian state has done this in stages over a century and a half. The current excavated area is what is publicly accessible. Several adjacent blocks of the ancient town are still under modern Ercolano and may never be excavated.

The most recent major work has been the Herculaneum Conservation Project — a decade-long restoration funded largely by the Packard Humanities Institute — which has stabilized many of the most fragile structures. The project has also revealed entire rooms previously unsuspected, including the so-called "Imperial Cult Building" where a small library of papyrus scrolls was found.
