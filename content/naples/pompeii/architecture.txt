Let me walk you through how a Roman city was built, using Pompeii as the example.

Start with the street grid. Roman cities were laid out on a regular grid, with two main streets crossing at the center — the cardo running north-south and the decumanus running east-west. The forum sat at the intersection. The grid was scaled to the city: smaller towns had simpler patterns, larger ones had more elaborate ones. Pompeii's grid is irregular at the edges where the city expanded over centuries, but the central core is rectangular.

The streets themselves are paved with large blocks of basalt — black volcanic stone quarried locally. The blocks fit together so tightly that little mortar was needed. Roman streets were designed to drain. They had high curbs and a slightly crowned profile in the center to shed water. The streets also doubled as the city's drainage system: when it rained, water flowed down the streets to the lower parts of the city and out the gates.

Look for the stepping stones. Every few blocks, a row of high stones crosses the street at intersections. Pedestrians used them to walk across without stepping in the dirty water and waste that ran through the gutters. The spacing of the stepping stones matched the gauge of Roman cart wheels — the carts would clatter through with their wheels straddling the stones.

Now the houses. Most Pompeiian houses follow the standard Roman atrium pattern. You enter through a narrow doorway, walk down a short corridor, and emerge into an atrium — a rectangular roofed courtyard with a square opening in the ceiling called the compluvium. Below it, set into the floor, is a shallow pool called the impluvium, which caught rainwater. The atrium was the formal reception space for visitors. Around its edges were the small bedrooms and the household shrine. At the back of the atrium, opposite the entrance, was the tablinum — a small office where the head of household received clients.

Beyond the tablinum, in larger houses, you'd find a peristyle garden — a colonnaded outdoor courtyard surrounded by more rooms, often more luxurious. The garden was the family's private space. Dining rooms, libraries, and slave quarters opened off it.

The walls of all these rooms were almost always frescoed. Pompeiian frescoes are organized into four major stylistic periods, which art historians call the First through Fourth Styles, evolving from imitation marble panels in the second century B.C. to elaborate trompe-l'oeil architectural scenes by the first century A.D. Many of the best surviving examples in the world are in this site.

Shops fronted onto the main streets. They typically had a wide opening, a counter with built-in jars for olives or wine or fish sauce, and living quarters in a small room behind. You can still see the jars in the counters — they're set into the marble in perfect rows, exactly where the shopkeeper put them.

The public buildings — the basilica, the temples, the baths, the amphitheater, the theaters — all clustered around the forum and the south end of town. The forum itself was the rectangular plaza of civic life, surrounded by colonnades on three sides and the temple of Jupiter on the fourth, with Vesuvius visible in the background. It still is.
