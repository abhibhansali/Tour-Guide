Here's something worth thinking about as you walk through Pompeii.

Most of what we know about how ordinary Romans actually lived comes from this city, and the smaller buried towns nearby — Herculaneum, Stabiae, Oplontis. Roman literature was written by and for the upper class — senators, generals, poets, philosophers. The lives of bakers, slaves, prostitutes, shopkeepers, foreign merchants, the dock workers and laundresses and street performers, are almost entirely missing from the official record. We have generalizations about them from elite writers who looked down on them. We have almost no direct evidence of how they lived.

Pompeii is where that absence stops. The graffiti on the walls — the political slogans, the love declarations, the obscene jokes — comes from exactly the people whose voices we don't otherwise hear. The shop layouts, the cheap household frescoes, the food still in the cooking pots, the toys in the children's rooms, the chained dogs and the makeshift repairs after the earthquake — all of it shows you what daily life actually looked like.

The result is that Pompeii is more democratically important than its destruction made it. It's not a temple of the gods or a palace of an emperor. It's a city of ordinary people who happened to be living next to a volcano they didn't understand. The catastrophe that killed them preserved them in a way no other Roman population has been preserved.

There's a second thing to think about. The eruption of seventy-nine A.D. is also the most-documented natural disaster in the ancient world, because Pliny the Younger wrote about it twice, in letters to the historian Tacitus, decades after the event. Pliny was eighteen years old at the time and his uncle had just died trying to save survivors. He wrote both letters as eyewitness testimony — calmly, clearly, with details about the timing and the appearance of the eruption cloud and what people did when the ash began to fall. Geologists in the nineteen-eighties realized Pliny's description was so accurate that they could reconstruct the entire eruption from his text. Every Plinian eruption ever studied is measured against Vesuvius. The young Roman writing letters about his uncle's death became, accidentally, the founder of modern volcanology.

A final thought. The mountain that did this is still there. You can see it from anywhere in Pompeii. Mount Vesuvius is still an active volcano — the most-watched volcano on Earth, in fact, because more than half a million people live in its immediate danger zone. The last eruption was in nineteen forty-four. Geologists agree it will erupt again, on a scale comparable to seventy-nine A.D., sometime in the next few centuries. The plan, in theory, is to evacuate the half million people in the red zone before that happens. Whether the plan will work in practice — given that warning signs may be subtle and people may not want to leave their homes — is another matter.

The Romans didn't know they were living next to a volcano. We do. Whether that turns out to make any difference in the end, we'll find out.
