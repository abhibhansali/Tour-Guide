Let me give you a quick history of the city and the eruption that ended it.

Pompeii had been around for a long time before the year seventy-nine. The site was originally settled by the Oscans, an Italic people, around the eighth century B.C. Greeks colonized the area shortly after, and the Etruscans controlled it briefly. The Romans took it over in the early third century B.C. By the first century, it was a comfortable provincial port city of about fifteen thousand people, with a mixed population of Romans, Greeks, Egyptians, Jews, and various traders from around the Mediterranean. It was prosperous and unremarkable.

A serious earthquake hit the region in sixty-two A.D., severely damaging buildings throughout Pompeii. Reconstruction was still underway seventeen years later when the volcano blew. Many of the buildings you'll see have hasty patch repairs from those seventeen years — fixed and re-painted, but never quite finished.

Now the eruption itself. The traditional date is August twenty-fourth, seventy-nine A.D., based on a copy of a letter by Pliny the Younger. Recent archaeological work suggests it was actually in October — fruit and vegetable evidence at the site doesn't match an August harvest. Either way, in the late summer or early autumn of that year, Mount Vesuvius began to rumble. The locals, who didn't really know they were living under a volcano — Vesuvius hadn't erupted in at least seven hundred years and the Romans called the surrounding region the "Phlegraean Fields" without understanding why it was fertile — didn't take the rumbles seriously.

Around one in the afternoon, the volcano exploded. A column of ash, gas, and pumice shot twenty-one miles up into the atmosphere. The eyewitness account is by Pliny the Younger, who watched the eruption from across the Bay of Naples. He compared the column's shape to an umbrella pine. We still call this kind of eruption a "Plinian" eruption, named after him.

Pumice rained down on Pompeii for hours. The roofs of houses collapsed under the weight. Many people fled; some tried to ride it out indoors and were trapped. By the next morning, after about twelve hours of falling ash, the city was buried under several meters of pumice.

The truly lethal phase came next. A series of pyroclastic surges — superheated gas, ash, and rock moving at hurricane speed — swept down the mountain. They hit Herculaneum first, killing everyone there instantly. Then they reached Pompeii. People who'd survived the night were killed by surge after surge of three-hundred-degree gas. Their bodies were entombed in the soft ash, and as the bodies decomposed over centuries, they left perfectly shaped cavities.

Pliny the Younger's uncle, Pliny the Elder — the famous naturalist who'd written the great encyclopedia of Roman knowledge — was the commander of the Roman fleet at Misenum. He sailed across the bay to evacuate survivors and died on the beach, probably of a heart attack worsened by the gas. Pliny the Younger lived to write the account that gives us almost everything we know about the eruption.
