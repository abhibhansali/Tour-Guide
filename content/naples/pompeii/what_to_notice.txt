Pompeii is so big that you can spend a full day and not see everything. Let me point you toward the things I'd particularly notice.

The plaster casts. When archaeologists in the eighteen sixties realized that the cavities left by decomposed bodies were perfectly body-shaped, they began pouring plaster into them. The technique was developed by Giuseppe Fiorelli. You can see casts at various locations in the site. The most concentrated group is at the Garden of the Fugitives, near the southern edge — thirteen people who tried to flee through a side garden and were caught by the pyroclastic surge. They're displayed where they fell. A mother holding a baby. A man trying to cover his face with his cloak. A teenager with his hand over his head. The casts are not human remains, exactly — the bones have been preserved but the soft tissue is gone — but they show the exact body positions of people in their last moments.

The House of the Faun. One of the largest and grandest houses in Pompeii, named for a small bronze statue of a dancing satyr that stood in the atrium. The bronze is now in the Naples museum; a copy stands in its place. The same house contained one of the most famous mosaics in the world: the Alexander Mosaic, depicting Alexander the Great defeating Darius the Third of Persia at the battle of Issus. The original is also in Naples. A copy is set into the floor where it was originally found. The mosaic is made of about a million and a half tesserae, each one no bigger than a grain of rice. Look closely at the horse's eye in the foreground. The detail is extraordinary.

The Stabian Baths. The largest of Pompeii's bath complexes, still mostly intact. You can walk through the original sequence of rooms: undressing room, cold room, warm room, hot room. The stucco ceiling decorations are preserved in places. The pools are dry, but the architecture is exactly as it was.

The amphitheater, in the southeast corner of the site. The oldest surviving permanent Roman amphitheater anywhere — built in seventy B.C., a hundred and fifty years before the Colosseum in Rome. It seated twenty thousand. Climb to the top row of seats. The view down into the arena is almost unchanged from what a Pompeiian would have seen. Imagine the place full.

The bread oven at the bakery of Modestus. Loaves of bread, stamped and ready for delivery, were found inside it. They're now in the museum, but the oven is here, in the original spot, exactly where the baker left it that morning.

In a wealthy house called the House of the Tragic Poet, near the central forum, look at the floor mosaic in the entranceway. It depicts a chained dog with the inscription "Cave Canem" — "Beware of the Dog." Roman doormats. The phrase is so familiar that we forget it has a specific Pompeiian origin. This mosaic is the original.

And finally, take a moment somewhere quiet — the forum at sunset is a good choice, with Vesuvius framed on one side and the temple of Jupiter on the other. The volcano is still active. It will erupt again, eventually. The mountain is closer than it looks.
