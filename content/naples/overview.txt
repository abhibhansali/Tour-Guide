Naples is the part of Italy most resistant to easy summary. Where Rome and Florence and Milan all have their own brand — imperial, Renaissance, industrial — Naples has roughly twelve simultaneous identities and refuses to choose. It is the third-largest Italian city. It is the headquarters of the country's most notorious organized-crime syndicate. It is the home of pizza and of opera and of one of Europe's oldest universities. It produced an emperor's most loyal scientist who died on its volcano, and it produced a center forward whose name is still chanted in its stadiums forty years after he stopped playing. None of these descriptions contradicts the others, and Naples wears them all without apology.

The name is Greek. Around six hundred B.C., Greek colonists from the nearby town of Cumae founded a settlement on the bay and called it Nea Polis — "the new city." Cumae itself was an older Greek colony from about the eighth century B.C. Nea Polis was the satellite, the expansion. The Latin Roman authorities later anglicized this to Neapolis, and the Italian Napoli is just the worn-down survival. The name has stayed nearly intact for twenty-six centuries. Almost no other major European city can claim that.

The Greeks built the city's permanent grid pattern. If you walk through Spaccanapoli today, you are walking on the original Greek decumanus — the east-west axis the colonists laid out twenty-six centuries ago. The Roman archaeologists confirmed this in the nineteenth century, when they dug down and found the Greek foundations still under the modern streets. Naples is the most archaeologically continuous European city. It is built on top of itself.

The Romans conquered Naples in the fourth century B.C. but never tried to romanize it. They allowed it to keep its Greek language and laws for centuries. Wealthy Romans built villas along the bay. Cicero owned a house here. The poet Virgil lived and died here in nineteen B.C. — his tomb is still maintained, on a hill above the modern port. The bay became Rome's resort coast. The Emperor Tiberius retired to Capri, just offshore.

A volcano was always part of the deal. Vesuvius had erupted before, but the eruption of August or October twenty-fourth, seventy-nine A.D., was the catastrophe that destroyed Pompeii and Herculaneum and a string of other towns along the coast. The Roman naval commander at the time, Pliny the Elder, was stationed across the bay at Misenum. He had spent his career writing the first comprehensive encyclopedia of natural history, a work of about thirty-seven books in Latin. When he saw the eruption column rising from Vesuvius, he sailed his fleet toward the eruption to try to evacuate survivors. He died on the beach at Stabiae, probably of a heart attack triggered by the volcanic gas. He is, by some accounts, the first recorded scientific casualty in history. The Plinian eruption — the term modern volcanology uses for that specific type of column-and-collapse event — is named after him.

After Rome fell, Naples was Byzantine, then Lombard, then briefly independent under its own dukes. The Normans took it in the eleven hundreds. The Hohenstaufen line ruled it next. The Aragonese arrived from Spain in the late twelve hundreds and built the Castel Nuovo as their seat. Spanish Habsburgs ruled directly from fifteen-oh-three to seventeen-oh-seven. Then Austrian Habsburgs. Then the Bourbons took over in seventeen thirty-four and ruled — with a brief Napoleonic interlude — until Italian unification in eighteen sixty. The historic city center of Naples is, by UNESCO designation, one of Europe's largest continuous historic districts, layered with all of these regimes.

The most consequential figure of Bourbon-era Naples was probably the king himself. Charles the Third, who took the Neapolitan throne in seventeen thirty-four, initiated the systematic excavation of Pompeii and Herculaneum in seventeen forty-eight. This single decision created modern archaeology. The techniques his engineers developed — careful stratigraphic digging, cataloging, on-site preservation — became the methodology of every later excavation. The objects they recovered, displayed in the Naples Archaeological Museum, are the largest collection of Roman everyday material culture in existence.

Science in Naples has a long heritage but tends to be overlooked. The University of Naples Federico the Second, founded in twelve hundred and twenty-four, is the oldest state-chartered university in continuous operation in the world. Giambattista della Porta did important early optical work in late-sixteenth-century Naples — including the first published description of the camera obscura with a lens. Giordano Bruno, the philosopher executed by the Inquisition in sixteen hundred for arguing the universe was infinite, was born just east of Naples. Giambattista Vico — the eighteenth-century philosopher of history, who first articulated the idea that all human societies pass through analogous cycles of development — was a Neapolitan native and is buried in the old city.

The single most important scientific institution in modern Naples is the Stazione Zoologica Anton Dohrn, on the seafront just below the city center. Founded in eighteen seventy-two by a German biologist named Anton Dohrn, it was the first marine biology research station in the world. Almost every major nineteenth-century European biologist passed through it. Modern marine ecology, embryology, and developmental biology have research traditions that trace directly back to Dohrn's lab. The aquarium attached to it — open to the public — is the oldest functioning aquarium in Europe.

Vesuvius is still active. The volcano has erupted thirty-some times since seventy-nine A.D.; the most recent eruption was in nineteen forty-four, during the Allied occupation. It is now the most continuously-monitored volcano on Earth. About six hundred thousand people live in the red zone — the area that would be devastated by a major eruption. Geologists know that another major eruption is statistically certain within the next several centuries; the evacuation plan exists but has never been tested at the required scale.

Then there is the food. The pizza margherita — tomato, mozzarella, basil — was invented in Naples in eighteen eighty-nine, when a pizzaiolo named Raffaele Esposito made it for visiting Queen Margherita of Savoy. The three colors mirrored the new Italian flag. Pizza had existed in Naples for centuries before that, of course — flat baked breads with tomato and oil have been Neapolitan street food since the seventeen hundreds. But the modern recognizable pizza, with its specific dough chemistry, double-zero Caputo flour, and wood-oven thermodynamics, was codified here. The Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana certifies pizzerias worldwide that meet the original technique. Most of the modern American pizza tradition descends from Neapolitan immigrants who arrived in the late nineteenth century.

Modern Naples has about a million inhabitants in the city proper, three million in the metropolitan area. The economy mixes legitimate maritime trade, tourism, manufacturing, and a substantial underground economy controlled in many neighborhoods by the Camorra — the local organized crime syndicate, distinct from the Sicilian Mafia and the Calabrian Ndrangheta. The Camorra is real, and Naples deals with it daily, but tourists generally do not see it. The city's civil society — its bookshops, neighborhood committees, anti-mafia magistrates, Catholic parishes, and academic institutions — coexists in tension with the criminal economy.

Two cultural icons hold the modern city together. The first is San Gennaro, the city's patron saint, whose blood — kept in a sealed glass vial in the cathedral — liquefies miraculously three times a year. Whether this is a miracle, a long-running chemical curiosity, or a combination of both is a matter of permanent Neapolitan debate. Skeptics have offered explanations involving thixotropic gels — substances that behave like solids when undisturbed but flow when shaken. The cathedral has refused to permit independent testing. The liquefaction has happened almost continuously since the fourteenth century.

The second icon is Diego Armando Maradona, the Argentine soccer player who joined the local football club Napoli in nineteen eighty-four and led them to two Italian championships and a UEFA Cup. He effectively transferred the city's psychic energy from religion to sport for a generation. When he died in twenty twenty, Naples renamed its stadium after him within forty-eight hours. Murals of his face are still painted on walls in the old city. He is, in the Neapolitan sensibility, almost interchangeable with San Gennaro.

Naples is a city you have to surrender to. It is loud, it is dense, it is fond of its own contradictions. Walk slowly. Eat constantly. Don't try to make it make sense.
