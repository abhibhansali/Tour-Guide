A few small details inside the basilica that most people walk past.

The bronze statue of Saint Peter, on a marble pedestal halfway down the right side of the nave. He's seated, holding keys, his right foot extended slightly. The foot has been worn down to a smooth blunt nub by the kisses and touches of pilgrims over nearly seven hundred years. The statue is from around twelve-eighty, and the toes were once detailed — you can see the original shape in old prints. Now they're partly gone. It's one of the most visceral pieces of evidence of continuous devotion you'll see anywhere.

In the floor of the basilica, look for inlaid red porphyry stones at the entrance — circular discs about a meter across. The largest one, just inside the main door, is the spot where every Holy Roman Emperor was crowned, from Charlemagne in the year eight hundred onward. The actual stone is older than the current basilica — it was salvaged from Constantine's church. Charlemagne, Otto the Great, every Holy Roman Emperor stood on this exact disc.

The four giant piers that hold up the dome each contain a relic — set behind statues and balconies. The relics are: the lance of Longinus, said to have pierced Christ's side; a piece of the True Cross; the head of Saint Andrew (which was returned to the Orthodox Church in nineteen-sixty-four); and Veronica's veil. Whether any of them are what they're said to be is a separate question. They've been in the building for centuries.

If you stand under the dome and look straight up, you'll notice the dome interior is decorated with mosaic, not painted. Almost every "painting" you see in Saint Peter's is in fact a mosaic — the originals are oil paintings, and copies in stone tessera were made in the seventeen hundreds because oil paint doesn't survive the humidity. The mosaicists are so skilled that the surfaces look painted at any normal viewing distance.

In the far right transept, near the back, find the tomb of Pope Alexander the Seventh — Bernini's last major work, finished when he was eighty. A bronze skeleton emerges from under a heavy marble drapery, holding up an hourglass. The pope kneels above, eyes closed. Bernini did this when he himself was dying. It's a serious meditation on death, made by an artist who was looking at his own.

And on the way out, on the floor near the main door, there's a small bronze marker of a sundial line — a meridian inlaid into the marble. At true solar noon, the sunbeam through a small hole in the wall touches it. The system still works, though almost nobody notices.
