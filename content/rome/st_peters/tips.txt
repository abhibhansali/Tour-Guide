Entry to the basilica itself is free, but you have to go through security. The line moves faster than it looks — it's metal detectors, not ticketing.
Early morning, before nine, or late afternoon, after five, are the best times to avoid the worst crowds.
Strict dress code is enforced: knees covered, shoulders covered, hats off indoors. Bring a scarf or sarong in summer; they will turn you away.
Photography is allowed inside, no flash. The Pietà is behind glass — anti-glare photos are difficult; just look at it.
If you came from the Sistine Chapel through the tour-group exit, you skipped the security line — congratulations. Otherwise, expect to queue.
Climb the dome if your knees allow it. Two options: walk all five hundred and fifty-one steps for around eight euros, or take the elevator partway and walk the last three hundred and twenty steps for about ten. The final stretch is narrow and tilted — claustrophobic, not difficult.
The view from the top is the best free vista in Rome — the whole city laid out, and the Bernini colonnade directly below.
Free bathrooms are outside on the left of the basilica as you exit, with a long but fast-moving queue.
Free guided tours in English are sometimes available — ask at the information desk on the right after entering.
The Vatican Grottoes, below the basilica, contain papal tombs and the burial of Peter. Free, often quieter than the church above. The entrance is near the pier of Saint Longinus.
Mass is celebrated several times daily — anyone is welcome, but the basilica restricts tourist traffic during services.
