The Capitoline has been the center of Roman public life since the founding of the city.

In the early kings period — the seventh and sixth centuries B.C. — the hill held the most important temples of Rome's religion. The Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus, on the southern peak, was the largest and most sacred temple of Roman state religion. Roman triumphs ended here: a victorious general would parade up from the Forum to the Capitoline, sacrifice a white ox at the temple, and offer his trophies to Jupiter. The temple was destroyed and rebuilt three times. Its foundations still survive under the modern Palazzo dei Conservatori.

On the northern peak stood the Temple of Juno Moneta — Juno the Adviser — which housed the Roman mint. Our word "money" comes from this temple, by way of the Latin moneta. Romans were minting coins on this hill from at least the third century B.C. through the fall of the empire.

After the empire fell, the Capitoline kept its political function in a degraded form. Medieval Roman municipal government met on the hill. The local barons used the ruins as quarries. By the fourteen hundreds, the piazza on top was an overgrown field, with the Marcus Aurelius statue standing in the middle of weeds.

The major redesign came under Pope Paul the Third in the fifteen thirties. Paul wanted to give Rome a unified civic center that could rival the new public spaces being built in Florence and Venice. He commissioned Michelangelo, then in his late sixties, to redesign the piazza and the surrounding palaces. Michelangelo's plan was radical: he reoriented the piazza, designed three palace facades, laid out the geometric pavement pattern, and placed the Marcus Aurelius bronze at the center as the focal point. The work took decades, mostly executed after Michelangelo's death. The piazza was substantially complete by the early seventeenth century.

The museums grew alongside. Sixtus the Fourth's fourteen seventy-one donation — including the bronze Capitoline Wolf, the bronze head of Constantine, and a few other pieces — was the founding collection. The popes continued adding works through the fifteen hundreds. The Conservatori palace was opened to the public for limited viewing in fifteen forty-two. In seventeen thirty-four, Pope Clement the Twelfth formally established the museums as a public institution. They're considered the world's first public art museum, predating the British Museum, the Louvre, and the Uffizi as a publicly-organized institution.

The Capitoline collection has been added to over centuries. The Palazzo Nuovo, on the opposite side of the piazza, was built in the seventeenth century specifically to display additional sculpture. The collection now numbers thousands of works.

After Italian unification in eighteen seventy, the Palazzo Senatorio at the center became the seat of Rome's city government. It still is. The mayor's office is on the upper floor, just past the museum entrances. When you visit the piazza, you're walking through Rome's civic heart.

The Marcus Aurelius equestrian bronze that you see in the center of the piazza is a copy. The original — the only large bronze equestrian statue to survive intact from antiquity — was moved indoors to the Palazzo dei Conservatori in nineteen ninety-seven, after pollution damage. The copy was cast from the original. You'll see the actual Marcus Aurelius inside the museum, in a glass-enclosed room.
