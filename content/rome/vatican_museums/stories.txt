A few specific moments and people inside this collection.

When Raphael was twenty-five years old, Julius the Second gave him four rooms of the papal apartments to decorate. The plan had been for the older painter Perugino to do them — Raphael's teacher. Julius saw a sketch by Raphael, fired the older masters, and turned the entire commission over to the young man. The frescoes that followed, especially the School of Athens, are arguably the high point of Renaissance painting. Plato and Aristotle in the center are walking out of the canvas. Raphael painted himself into a corner of the composition — the young man in dark clothing looking out at the viewer. He died at thirty-seven, in fifteen-twenty, with the rooms not quite finished.

There's a strange story about the Belvedere Apollo, which used to be the most admired sculpture in Europe. For four hundred years, every art student copied it. Goethe wrote rhapsodically about it. Then, in the nineteenth century, archaeologists started finding actual Greek originals — instead of Roman copies — and the Apollo's reputation collapsed. It turned out to be a Roman knockoff, with idealized proportions but lifeless detail. The Greeks had been better. The Vatican still displays it, but it's no longer the star. Taste, even in great art, has fashions.

There's the story of the painter Caravaggio, whose Deposition hangs in the Pinacoteca. Caravaggio was a violent man — he killed someone in a brawl in sixteen-oh-six and fled Rome. His paintings are unsettling: real-looking, dark, with peasant faces and dirty feet on saints. The church alternately bought his work and rejected it. The Deposition was painted in sixteen-oh-three, before his exile, and is one of the most physically convincing depictions of grief in religious art. He paints the weight of Christ's body. You can feel it in the men holding him.

And there's a stranger story, about the Etruscan tomb finds. The Vatican's Etruscan museum has the contents of a single intact tomb, called the Regolini-Galassi tomb, found in eighteen-thirty-six near the town of Cerveteri. Inside it were the bodies of an Etruscan princess and her servants, surrounded by gold jewelry, bronze vessels, and an entire bronze-plated funeral bed. The bed is on display. The gold is in display cases that fill an entire room. The Etruscans, who came before the Romans and were eventually conquered by them, are mostly mysterious to us. Their language is barely deciphered. But the contents of that tomb are extraordinary, and they're in the Vatican because the popes happened to own the land where it was found.
