The Vatican Museums are housed inside the Vatican Palace — a sprawling architectural compound built across six centuries. Let me walk you through what you're seeing structurally.

The oldest part of the palace dates to the eleventh century, when popes occasionally lived here. But the heart of the museums is the Belvedere — a summer villa built by Pope Innocent the Eighth on the highest part of the Vatican hill in the fourteen eighties. Innocent wanted somewhere cooler and more private than the main palace, so he built a small pavilion on the ridge.

Julius the Second commissioned the architect Donato Bramante to connect Innocent's Belvedere back to the main palace with two long parallel corridors and a sequence of courtyards. Those corridors are the spine of the museum route today. The view down them, even now, is one of the longest interior perspectives in the world. Bramante was the same architect who'd just started Saint Peter's Basilica next door. He treated the whole Vatican as one grand architectural project.

Inside, the styles change every few rooms because every pope wanted his contribution to be visible. The Gallery of Maps, painted under Pope Gregory the Thirteenth in the fifteen eighties, is a single ceiling-vaulted hallway covered in detailed topographic maps of all the regions of Italy. The Gallery of Tapestries hangs vast Flemish tapestries from the fifteen hundreds, designed by students of Raphael. The Raphael Rooms — four rooms decorated by Raphael himself between fifteen-oh-eight and his death in fifteen-twenty — are the artistic peak of the whole museum, with the possible exception of the Sistine Chapel.

The spiral staircase you'll exit on at the end of the visit is, surprisingly, modern. It was designed by the architect Giuseppe Momo in nineteen-thirty-two — two interlocking spirals, one for ascending and one for descending, like a double helix. It looks Renaissance because Momo wanted it to. It's twentieth-century engineering inside a deliberately old style.

A note about the building's politics. The Vatican is its own sovereign state — the smallest country in the world. It minted its own coins until the euro. The Swiss Guards you'll see at the doors take their orders from the pope, not from Italy. When you step into the museum, you're technically crossing an international border. Your phone will tell you so.
