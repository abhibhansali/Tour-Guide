You're walking into one of the most remarkable small collections in the world. The Galleria Borghese has, room for room, more masterpieces than any museum its size anywhere — including six of Bernini's greatest sculptures and six of Caravaggio's most important paintings, all under one roof, all in their original setting.

The setting matters. This isn't a converted palace or a purpose-built museum. The gallery is in the Villa Borghese, a country house built between sixteen-twelve and sixteen-fifteen by Cardinal Scipione Borghese, the nephew of Pope Paul the Fifth. Scipione was the original art collector of the family. He used his Vatican connections to acquire — sometimes legitimately, sometimes through aggressive seizures — the best work he could find. He filled the villa with classical sculpture, Renaissance paintings, and contemporary works by his young protégés. Gian Lorenzo Bernini was one of those protégés. Caravaggio was another.

The collection is still in the building Scipione built. The rooms, the niches, the placement of individual works — much of it is exactly as Scipione arranged it four hundred years ago. The painted ceilings, designed to complement specific paintings, are still in place. Bernini's sculpture group of Pluto and Proserpina is in the room it was sculpted for. You're walking through Scipione Borghese's personal taste, preserved.

The visit is structured around a strict time slot. You buy a ticket for a specific two-hour window. Everyone in that window enters together; everyone exits together. The gallery does this because the rooms are small and the works are fragile, and they want crowd control. The two hours is generous for the size of the collection — you can see everything thoroughly. But you can't linger past your window.

The villa has two floors. The ground floor — the rooms you enter — hold most of the sculpture, including all of Bernini's major works. The upper floor is the painting gallery, with the Caravaggios, Raphael's Deposition, Titian's Sacred and Profane Love, and others. Visit the ground floor first if possible; the upper floor benefits from light, which fades later.

Around the villa, the Borghese Gardens extend for several hundred acres. They're free, beautiful, and one of the best places in central Rome for a quiet walk. The Pincian Hill, in the northern part of the gardens, has a panoramic view of the city — and is one of the great viewpoints for sunset. Plan to walk the gardens before or after the gallery visit.

Book the gallery weeks in advance. Same-day tickets exist but are rarely available.
