Cardinal Scipione Borghese was the most aggressive art collector of his generation. He used the resources of the papacy — his uncle Paul the Fifth's office — to acquire art ruthlessly. The collection now in the Galleria Borghese is what he assembled between roughly sixteen-oh-five and his death in sixteen thirty-three.

Some of his acquisitions were straightforward purchases. He paid handsomely for works by his contemporary Caravaggio, even when Caravaggio's reputation was unsettled by his criminal life. He commissioned new works from young Bernini, becoming Bernini's first major patron when the sculptor was still in his twenties.

Other acquisitions were less ethical. Scipione had Caravaggio's painting Madonna of the Palafrenieri seized from a Roman confraternity that had rejected it for not meeting their requirements. He extorted a Raphael Deposition from the city of Perugia by threatening to revoke their religious privileges. He confiscated the contents of the painter Cavalier d'Arpino's studio after charging d'Arpino with possessing unregistered firearms. Several Caravaggios in the gallery came from that single seizure. Scipione's nickname in Rome was "the cardinal-nephew" — code for the corrupt papal relative who used family power for personal gain.

The villa was built between sixteen-twelve and sixteen-fifteen by the architect Flaminio Ponzio, with Scipione personally directing the design of the major rooms. The building was specifically designed as a display case for art. The largest room — the Salone del Imperatori — had niches around the walls for the largest sculptures. The painting galleries upstairs had picture-hanging frames built into the walls. Each major work had a specific intended spot.

After Scipione's death, the collection passed to his cousins. For the next two centuries it stayed in the Borghese family, hung exactly as Scipione had arranged it. The collection was occasionally added to but never substantially altered. The villa was their private gallery, open occasionally to visiting scholars and dignitaries.

In eighteen-oh-seven, Camillo Borghese — Scipione's descendant, married to Napoleon's sister Pauline — sold a major portion of the classical sculpture collection to Napoleon. The pieces went to Paris. About two hundred ancient Roman sculptures left the villa, and most of them are still in the Louvre. The remaining classical sculpture in the gallery is what wasn't sold.

In nineteen-oh-three, the Italian state purchased the entire villa and remaining collection from the Borghese family. The villa became a public museum. It opened for general public visits in the nineteen tens. The Bernini sculptures, the Caravaggio paintings, and most of the remaining classical pieces have stayed where Scipione placed them.

The most recent significant change was the addition of climate control and security systems in the late twentieth century. The strict two-hour visit policy was introduced in nineteen ninety-seven, after a study determined that the building's small rooms couldn't safely accommodate uncontrolled crowds.

What you're seeing is essentially a seventeenth-century cardinal's private collection, preserved with unusual fidelity, and still hanging on the same walls he hung them on.
