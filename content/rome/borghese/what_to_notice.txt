A few works in the gallery that justify the whole visit.

The four major Bernini sculptures, all on the ground floor.

Apollo and Daphne. Walk around it. The transformation of Daphne — fingers becoming leaves, hair becoming bark — is the technical center of the work. From one angle you see Apollo pursuing; from another you see Daphne already partly tree. The piece works as a sequence of views, designed to be circled. Linger.

The Rape of Proserpina. Pluto, the god of the underworld, grabs the young Proserpina to carry her away. Look at Pluto's fingers pressing into Proserpina's thigh — the marble is carved so that her flesh appears to yield under his grip. The detail is unbelievable. Bernini was twenty-three when he finished this. The face of Proserpina is contorted in fear and tears.

Bernini's David. Caught in the moment of slinging the stone. His brow is furrowed; his teeth are clenched. The marble armor at his feet is the discarded gear from Saul, who'd offered it to David and been declined. The face is Bernini's self-portrait. The whole sculpture turns on a single arrested gesture.

Aeneas, Anchises, and Ascanius. An earlier Bernini work, from when he was nineteen — Aeneas escaping Troy with his elderly father on his shoulders and his small son alongside. The three figures form a vertical line. The grandfather's wrinkled flesh, the father's muscular body, the child's softer features — Bernini was already mastering the differentiation of skin textures at different ages.

The Caravaggio room, upstairs. Six paintings by Caravaggio in a single room — the densest concentration of his work outside of Naples. Sick Bacchus (or Self-Portrait as Bacchus) is the youngest of them, painted around fifteen ninety-three when Caravaggio was twenty. He looks ill — the painting may have been done after a hospital stay. Madonna of the Palafrenieri shows the Madonna stepping on a serpent with Saint Anne nearby; the painting was rejected by the Roman confraternity that commissioned it and ended up in Scipione's collection through bullying. Boy with a Basket of Fruit — a sensual painting of a young Roman holding a basket of grapes and peaches, painted around fifteen ninety-three. Look at how the fruit is depicted: each grape is detailed, slightly bruised, decaying. The corruption of the natural world is part of Caravaggio's theme.

Raphael's Deposition. Painted in fifteen-oh-seven for the Baglioni family in Perugia. Christ is being carried to the tomb by mourners — the painting is full of movement, drama, sorrow. Raphael was twenty-four when he finished it. Scipione confiscated the painting from Perugia in sixteen-oh-eight. The original frame is preserved.

Titian's Sacred and Profane Love. An early Titian, painted around fifteen-fourteen. Two women sit at the edge of a fountain — one clothed, one nude. The traditional interpretation is that the clothed woman represents sacred love and the nude represents profane, but modern scholars argue the opposite reading. Either way, the painting is one of the most beautiful Titian landscapes anywhere. The light, the colors, the relaxed poses of the figures — all are masterful.

In the same room, look for Correggio's Danaë, painted around fifteen thirty-one. Danaë reclining as Zeus, in the form of a golden shower, descends on her. The eroticism is undisguised. Cupids tend to her. The painting is one of the great mythological-erotic works of the Italian Renaissance.
