You're standing in front of one of the most theatrical pieces of public sculpture in Europe. The Trevi Fountain takes up an entire side of a small piazza, three stories tall, carved out of the back wall of a private palace. Most fountains decorate a square. This one effectively becomes the square.

The fountain was designed by an architect named Nicola Salvi, who began it in seventeen thirty-two for Pope Clement the Twelfth. The pope wanted a monumental terminus for one of Rome's oldest aqueducts — the Aqua Virgo, built by Marcus Agrippa in nineteen B.C. to bring fresh water in from springs about twenty kilometers east of the city. That aqueduct, almost two thousand years old, is still working. Every drop of water you see pouring out of these fountains arrived through pipes that Augustus's engineers laid out.

There's a legend about the aqueduct's name. Roman soldiers, exhausted and thirsty after a march, were shown the spring by a young virgin — vergine in Italian. They followed her, drank, and the spring became the source of the aqueduct. The legend is almost certainly a later invention, but the name stuck. The water you see is still called Acqua Vergine, virgin water, and it's piped to a few fountains around the city. Romans still drink from public spouts of it.

Look at the central figure. Most visitors assume it's Neptune. It's actually Oceanus — a more abstract sea deity, the personification of all rivers and oceans. He's standing on a shell-shaped chariot pulled by two seahorses. The seahorses are guided by Tritons — one of them blowing a conch shell, the other restraining a rearing seahorse. The composition is theatrical, exaggerated, deeply baroque. The whole back wall is the Palazzo Poli, and Salvi designed the fountain to look as though Oceanus had just burst out of the palace itself.

Salvi spent almost twenty years on the design before dying in seventeen fifty-one. The fountain was finished eleven years later by Giuseppe Pannini in seventeen sixty-two. Pope Clement the Thirteenth blessed it that year. Romans have been throwing coins into it ever since.

The coin tradition is more recent than it looks. It comes from a nineteen fifty-four Hollywood film called Three Coins in the Fountain. The premise: throw one coin in over your left shoulder with your right hand, and you'll return to Rome. The film popularized a vague existing tradition into a precise ritual. Throw two coins, the modern version goes, for a romance. Three for marriage. About three thousand euros are tossed in every day. The city collects them weekly and donates the money to Caritas, the Catholic charity. So even if your wish doesn't come true, the coin probably bought groceries for someone.

A few last things to notice. The water has a particular blue-green tint, slightly turquoise. That's natural — it's the minerals in the spring water reflecting through the white travertine of the basin. The bottom of the fountain is paved with the original eighteenth-century stone, and you can see the algae stains where the water level usually sits.

The fountain was famously the location for Anita Ekberg wading through it in Fellini's nineteen-sixty film La Dolce Vita. You can't wade. The carabinieri are watching from across the piazza, and they will fine you about five hundred euros. The film made the fountain world-famous, but it also made it the most surveilled public space in Rome.

Stand back, throw your coin if you want to come back, and try to be here once at night. The lights come on at sunset. The crowd thins after eleven. After midnight, with the water still running and almost nobody around, it is one of the strangest and quietest beautiful places in the city.
