Let me give you a few specific things to find while you wander.

The Temple of Caesar. It's small — just a low platform with a curved alcove protected by a metal roof. This is the spot where Caesar's body was burned in forty-four B.C., after Mark Antony's funeral speech turned the mourners into a riot. Augustus built a temple here later, deifying his adoptive father. Inside the alcove, you will almost always find fresh flowers. Modern Romans still leave them. Two thousand years on, somebody still cares enough to bring flowers to Julius Caesar.

The House of the Vestal Virgins. Six women, chosen as girls, served the goddess Vesta for thirty years. They kept a sacred fire burning continuously. If the fire went out, the priestess responsible was flogged. If a Vestal broke her vow of chastity, she was buried alive — there is genuinely no record of any Roman matter taken more seriously than this. Their compound, with its long central garden and rows of statues of senior Vestals, is one of the most peaceful corners of the Forum. The statues are damaged, but you can still read names on some of the bases.

The Curia. The brick rectangle near the Arch of Septimius Severus. This is where the Senate met. The bronze doors are copies; the originals were moved to the Lateran in the seventeenth century. Inside, the marble floor is the original Roman opus sectile pattern, surviving because the building was converted to a church in the seventh century. You can step inside. It's worth it just to stand on the same floor where the Senate stood.

The Lapis Niger — the Black Stone. It's a small fenced-off area of dark paving on the Comitium, the open assembly space in front of the Curia. Beneath it is one of the oldest surviving Latin inscriptions, dating from around five-seventy B.C. The Romans themselves, even in Cicero's time, didn't know what it said. They knew only that it was old and probably cursed, so they paved over it.

The columns of the Temple of Saturn. Eight Ionic columns, all that's left. Look closely at the base inscription: it's the dedication of a re-restoration, from around the year four hundred A.D., when the empire was already crumbling. The text is calmly formal, as though the empire would last forever. Within a generation, the Goths would be inside the city.
