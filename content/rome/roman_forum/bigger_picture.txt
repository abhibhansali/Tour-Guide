Here's something to think about as you walk through the Forum.

For most of European history, this place defined what "civilized" meant. The idea of a republic, with elected officials and a deliberative body. The idea of public space designed for citizens. The idea of laws debated openly and inscribed on bronze. The idea of an emperor as a public figure rather than a private monarch. All of that was Roman, and all of it was, for centuries, transacted in the few hundred square meters you're standing in.

When the Forum ended, those ideas didn't end with it. They went into a long sleep. Medieval Europe was governed very differently — by hereditary rulers, mostly, with the church as the only common framework. The classical model survived only in fragments: in monastery libraries, in Byzantine law codes, in the memory of what Cicero had been.

It came back in stages. The Italian republics of the eleven and twelve hundreds — Venice, Florence, Siena — reinvented some of the institutions. The Renaissance, beginning a couple of hundred miles north of here in Florence, dug up the old texts and made them respectable again. The Enlightenment took it further. By the seventeen hundreds, when Americans and French were writing constitutions, they were consciously echoing the Roman Republic. Capitol Hill in Washington is named for this hill. The U.S. Senate is named for the Roman Senate. Madison and Jefferson were arguing in private letters about what Cicero would have done.

This is the thing about standing in the Forum. The classical world is not just back there in time. It comes forward into the present in ways that aren't obvious until you walk through the place where it happened.

There's a second thing too, less optimistic. The Forum is also a lesson in how long-lived institutions die. Rome didn't fall in a day. It got rich, it got complacent, it stopped renewing its political class, it outsourced its army, it tolerated corruption. The buildings around you were here, mostly intact, while the political life inside them was hollowing out. The temples kept getting dedicated to longer-reigning emperors. The Senate kept meeting. The forms persisted long after the substance was gone.

Walking through, it's hard not to wonder how it would have looked from the inside, year by year, as it slipped away. Probably it looked fine. That's the unsettling part.
