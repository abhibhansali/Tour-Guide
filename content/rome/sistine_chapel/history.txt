Let me tell you the story of how this ceiling got painted.

In fifteen-oh-eight, Michelangelo was thirty-three years old, already famous, and primarily a sculptor. He'd carved the Pietà in Saint Peter's at twenty-four. He'd carved the David in Florence at twenty-eight. He saw himself as a marble man — fresco was an inferior medium, painters were a lower class than sculptors. Julius the Second wanted him to paint the chapel ceiling. Michelangelo wanted to keep carving the pope's tomb. He refused several times. Julius eventually issued what amounted to a direct order.

Michelangelo took the job sullenly. He hired a Florentine painter to teach him fresco technique, then fired the man within months and finished learning by himself. He fired all his assistants. He worked almost entirely alone, on scaffolding he designed himself, sixty feet above the floor.

The scaffolding question is interesting. Popular image: Michelangelo on his back, paint dripping in his face. Actual scaffolding: free-standing wooden platforms attached to the walls, and Michelangelo stood up on them, neck bent backwards, looking up. He wrote a letter to a friend describing the physical strain — his back hurt, paint dripped into his eyes, his face was getting sunburned by the candle smoke. He attached a sonnet, complaining in verse about his job. The original letter survives. So does the sonnet.

He worked from October fifteen-oh-eight to October fifteen-twelve. Four and a half years. The ceiling has more than three hundred figures and tells the story of Genesis from the creation of the world to the drunkenness of Noah. The whole composition is integrated — every figure relates to every other figure compositionally — and it was painted in the wet plaster method called buon fresco, which gives the painter only one chance to get each section right before the plaster dries.

Twenty-three years later, in fifteen-thirty-five, Pope Paul the Third brought him back. The original altar wall had frescoes by Perugino and a small altarpiece. Paul wanted the whole wall replaced with a vast Last Judgment. Michelangelo was sixty-one. The painting took five more years. The figures were larger, the style darker, the bodies more contorted. By the time it was unveiled, the Catholic Counter-Reformation was underway, and the new church found Michelangelo's frank nudity scandalous. After his death, Daniele da Volterra was paid to paint clothing over the most explicit figures. The nickname they gave Daniele was "Il Braghettone" — the breeches-maker. Most of his additions survive.
