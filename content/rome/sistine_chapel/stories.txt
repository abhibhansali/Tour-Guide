The Sistine Chapel has been argued about for five hundred years. Here are a few of the better arguments.

The cleaning controversy. Between nineteen-eighty and nineteen-ninety-four, a team of Italian restorers cleaned five centuries of candle smoke, soot, and varnish off the ceiling and the Last Judgment. When they finished, the colors were vivid — pink, lemon yellow, mint green, bright blue. The world had been used to a darker, more solemn Michelangelo. Many art historians were outraged. James Beck of Columbia University led the charge: he argued the restorers had stripped away Michelangelo's own final glazes, the dark touches he added on top of the wet fresco, and that what we were now seeing was an underpainting Michelangelo never meant to be visible. The restorers replied that there were no glazes, that the dirt had been mistaken for art, that they had simply cleaned. The argument was never resolved. You're looking at what came out of the cleaning. Whether it's what Michelangelo actually painted is a question we can't fully answer.

The conclave. When a pope dies or resigns, the College of Cardinals — men over the age of eighty are excluded — gathers in this room to elect a successor. They sleep nearby, in spartan quarters. They vote in the morning and the afternoon. Each ballot is burned afterward in a small stove that vents through the chapel roof. Black smoke means no decision. White smoke means a new pope. The whole process can last hours or weeks. The room is sealed during voting — no phones, no recording devices, no contact with the outside world. The last conclave, in twenty-thirteen, lasted two days.

The smoking gun letter. There's a letter from Michelangelo, surviving in his own handwriting, complaining to a friend in Florence about how miserable the ceiling work was making him. He wrote it about halfway through. "My beard turns up to heaven; my brain falls in like rags upon my back. I am not in a good place to be a painter." The next line is the one art historians love: "I am not in the right place, and I am not a painter." Michelangelo never quite accepted that he was a painter, even as he was painting the ceiling that would change Western art.

And one stranger story. In the nineteenth century, a Vatican archivist found, in a tomb on the Aventine Hill, a casket of letters from a woman named Vittoria Colonna. She had been Michelangelo's closest friend in the last decade of his life. The letters are tender. Whether they were lovers, in any sense, is unclear; she was a widow, a poet, a deeply religious woman, fifteen years younger than him. He drew portraits of her, gave her sketches, sent her poems. When she died in fifteen-forty-seven, he wrote that he had been left to "wander in the dark." The Last Judgment, painted in those years, has a melancholy that some readers connect to her.
