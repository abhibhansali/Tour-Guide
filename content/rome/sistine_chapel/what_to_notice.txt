Look up first. The ceiling reads from the altar end toward the entrance, which means you should look back over your shoulder once you've walked into the room.

Find the Creation of Adam — God and Adam reaching toward each other, fingers almost touching. It's the third panel from the entrance, near the middle of the ceiling. Two specific things to notice: First, the gap between their fingers is the whole point of the painting. Michelangelo paints the moment before life is given, not the moment of contact. Adam is reclining, languid; God leans toward him with effort. Second, look at the cloak behind God. Many viewers have pointed out that the shape of the cloak, with God and the figures inside it, resembles the cross-section of a human brain. Michelangelo dissected cadavers as a young man to understand anatomy. Whether the resemblance is deliberate has been argued about for forty years. Decide for yourself.

Now look at the figures along the edges of the ceiling — the seven prophets and five sibyls, sitting on thrones. They are massive, monumental, painted at twice the scale of the central scenes. The Libyan Sibyl, near the entrance, is twisted dramatically as she lifts a book — her pose is one of the most studied figures in Western art, copied for centuries. The prophet Jeremiah, on the opposite side, slumps forward, hand to his face, despairing.

Now turn to the Last Judgment on the wall behind the altar. Christ is in the center, raising his hand, judging the dead. Saints are clustered around him. The damned are falling on the right; the saved are rising on the left. There are roughly four hundred figures in this single wall.

Find Saint Bartholomew, holding his own flayed skin — he's just below Christ, slightly to the right. The face on the flayed skin is Michelangelo's self-portrait. He painted himself as a wreck, a tortured face on an empty hide. Most artists who put themselves into their own work do so flatteringly. Michelangelo painted himself as something Saint Bartholomew was holding by the corner.

Look at the lower right corner of the Last Judgment. There's a man with donkey ears, being bitten by a snake. That's Biagio da Cesena, the papal master of ceremonies who complained that the fresco was indecent. Michelangelo painted him into hell. Biagio complained to the pope. The pope reportedly said: "I have authority in heaven and on earth, but my authority does not extend into hell." Biagio is still there.
