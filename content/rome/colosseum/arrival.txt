You're approaching one of the most recognized buildings on Earth — and one that was, for most of its life, used for things we'd struggle to attend today. The Colosseum is not a stadium. It's a Roman amphitheater, an oval purpose-built for a specific kind of public spectacle, and the largest one ever constructed.

A few orienting numbers. It seats about fifty thousand people. It's a hundred and sixty feet tall — that's a sixteen-story building. The outer ring you see today is only part of what once stood. Two thirds of the original travertine façade has been carried off over the centuries to build other parts of Rome, including Saint Peter's and the Palazzo Venezia. The half that's missing is the side that faced the Roman Forum. The half that survives is the side that faced the city.

The official name is the Flavian Amphitheater — Amphitheatrum Flavium — named for the dynasty that built it: Vespasian, his son Titus, and after them Domitian. They came to power right after the chaos of Nero's reign and the civil wars of sixty-nine A.D., and they wanted a populist gesture, something to give back to Rome. Nero had built himself a private palace on this very spot, with an artificial lake in the middle. The Flavians drained the lake and built a public amphitheater on top of it. The political message was not subtle.

The name "Colosseum" comes from a giant bronze statue — the Colossus of Nero — that used to stand right beside it. The statue is long gone, but the nickname stuck.

You're going to walk through one of around eighty original entrances. The Romans had numbers carved over them so spectators could find their seats — an early version of the section signs at a stadium. They could empty fifty thousand people from this building in about fifteen minutes. We still can't do that with modern arenas.

Once you're inside, you'll be looking down into the arena floor — except the floor isn't there. What you'll see is the network of tunnels and chambers underneath, called the hypogeum. We'll spend time on it. For now, just take in the scale of the thing.
