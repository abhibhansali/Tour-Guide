The building is a feat of engineering even by modern standards. Let me give you the parts to look for.

Start with the outer wall — the section that survived. It's built almost entirely of travertine, a creamy local limestone quarried at Tivoli, twenty miles east. The blocks were originally held together not with mortar but with iron clamps. When the iron was extracted during the Middle Ages — iron was valuable — the holes were left behind. Walk close to the outer wall and you'll see a pattern of small square cavities all over the surface. Those are the clamp holes. They're a kind of bullet scar from medieval recycling.

The wall has four stories. The bottom three are arcades — eighty arches each, repeating around the oval. Each story uses a different Greek architectural order, getting lighter as it climbs: Doric at the bottom, then Ionic, then Corinthian. The top floor — the attic — has rectangular windows and a series of decorative brackets. Those brackets used to support wooden masts, and the masts used to support the velarium.

The velarium was a giant retractable awning that shaded the upper rows. It was operated by a detachment of sailors from the imperial fleet at Misenum, near Naples — because nobody else in Rome had the rope-rigging skills to handle that much canvas. Picture a fabric roof, larger than the building's footprint, being unfurled and tensioned by sailors. That's what shaded the audience on hot afternoons.

Inside, the seating was rigorously segregated by class. The emperor and senators sat closest to the arena, on marble. Behind them were the equestrian class, then ordinary citizens, then — at the very top, standing only — women and the poor. Your seat number, carved on a ticket made of bone or pottery, told you exactly which entrance to use, which staircase, which row. The whole system was designed for speed and order.

The arena floor was wooden, covered with sand. The sand absorbed blood. The Latin word for sand is harena — that's where our word "arena" comes from.

Below the wooden floor sat the hypogeum, on two levels. It held cages for animals, holding cells for gladiators, machinery for hoisting scenery, and trapdoors that could deliver a leopard or a small forest into the middle of the arena without warning. The whole performance space had elevators. Engineers have rebuilt one of them, partway across the arena, and you can sometimes see it operating.
