A few things easy to walk past.

The clamp holes I mentioned earlier — they're everywhere in the outer wall. Look at a single travertine block up close and you'll see two small square cavities per joint, where iron clamps once held the building together. The masonry is technically loose. It stands because the geometry is right, not because the mortar is strong.

Look for the line on the outside where the original façade ends and the brick reinforcement begins. The popes in the early eighteen hundreds, finally worried that the south wall might collapse, built two enormous brick buttresses on the broken edges. They're meant to be visible — they're not pretending to be Roman. They're a frank admission of patching, and they've held for two hundred years.

When you walk into the seating area, look down into the hypogeum. The clearest views are from the upper tiers. You're looking at the underground level where the animals and gladiators waited. You can see the slots in the brick walls where wooden floors and stairs once attached. In some sections, you can see the curved chambers where animal cages were stored. On one side, the modern reconstruction of an elevator stands — a wooden frame, ropes, a winch. It's a working replica.

In the floor of the arena, look for the partial wooden platform near one end. It's a modern restoration, built so visitors can stand at the level of the original floor and look across the oval at eye level. Standing on it gives you a very different sense of the building than looking down does.

Up in the seating rings, find the numbered entry arches — those Roman section markers I mentioned. Roman numerals are still legible above some of them. You're seeing the same numbers a ticket-holder would have read in eighty A.D.

Look for the cross in the arena. It marks where one of the medieval stations of the cross used to be — part of the Catholic reinvention of the space after Pope Benedict's consecration.

And on your way out, glance back at the eastern face of the building from a distance. The four-story height there is intact. That's what the entire ring once looked like, all the way around. The Colosseum you see today is roughly two-thirds of the original. Imagine the rest, and you start to understand what a Roman would have felt walking up to it.
