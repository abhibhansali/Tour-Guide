A few specific people and moments from this place.

There's a famous gladiator named Flamma — the Flame. He was a Syrian, fought in the second century, won so many bouts that he earned his freedom four separate times. He turned it down each time and kept fighting. He died at thirty, after thirty-four bouts. His tombstone, found in Sicily, lists his record: twenty-one wins, nine draws, four losses, where a loss meant he had survived but been defeated. He chose this life and stayed in it. That tombstone is one of the only sources we have for the world of an individual gladiator.

There's the inauguration in eighty A.D., when the Emperor Titus oversaw a hundred days of games. Cassius Dio describes the building being partially flooded for a mock sea battle — a naumachia — though scholars now think the larger naval battles were probably staged elsewhere. Either way, the building's drainage and water supply was extraordinary for any era.

There's the strange medieval afterlife. After the empire fell, the Colosseum was lived in. Families built houses inside it. A church was attached to it. Goats grazed in the arena. The Roman family called the Frangipani turned the whole thing into a fortress in the eleven hundreds. They walled off the upper arches and used the towers for defense in their feuds with other noble families. The Colosseum, for several centuries, was just a very strangely shaped neighborhood.

There's the moment in the eighteenth century when the building became an unlikely subject of botanical study. Botanists noticed that the Colosseum hosted plants found nowhere else in Italy — seeds carried from across the empire on the fur of imported animals, germinating in the cracks of the masonry. By eighteen-fifty-five, a botanist named Richard Deakin had cataloged four hundred and twenty species. When the Italians cleared the vegetation later, in an attempt to "restore" the building, many of those plants died out locally. The cleanup was the kind of progress that erases something.

And there's Lord Byron, who came up here at night in eighteen-seventeen, climbed the upper tiers in the moonlight, and wrote some of the most famous lines in English about ruin: "While stands the Coliseum, Rome shall stand; when falls the Coliseum, Rome shall fall; and when Rome falls — the world." He probably overstated the case. But standing here, you can see why he'd think so.
