You're walking into Trastevere, one of the most beloved neighborhoods in Rome and one of the most resistant to gentrification. The name comes from the Latin trans Tiberim — "across the Tiber" — because the neighborhood sits on the western bank of the river, separated from the historic center by the water. For most of Roman history, Trastevere was where the working classes lived: dockworkers, freed slaves, foreign merchants from Greece and Syria and beyond. It still has that character. The cobblestones are uneven. The buildings are weathered. The laundry hangs from balconies. Tourists come here for dinner; locals never left.

The neighborhood developed as Rome's port district. The Tiber, just south of here, was navigable from the Mediterranean, and Trastevere was where ships unloaded grain, oil, wine, and slaves. The river dock — Ripa Grande — is gone now, replaced in the late nineteenth century by the modern Lungotevere embankments. But the neighborhood's mercantile character persisted into the twentieth century. As late as the nineteen fifties, Trastevere was significantly poorer than the rest of Rome. Bohemian artists, students, and tourists discovered it in the sixties. Rents climbed. Restaurants and bars proliferated. But the basic structure — narrow medieval streets, working-class housing stacked on top of small shops — stayed.

A few things to find while you wander.

Santa Maria in Trastevere — the largest church in the neighborhood — is one of the oldest churches in Rome. The current building dates from the eleven hundreds, but the site has been used for Christian worship since the third century A.D. The mosaics inside, especially the apse mosaic by Pietro Cavallini from around twelve ninety, are some of the finest medieval mosaics in Italy. Cavallini was a contemporary of Giotto and worked in a similar early-realist style. The mosaics depict scenes from the life of the Virgin and are the closest you can get to the visual world of the medieval Roman church. The fountain in the piazza outside is the oldest continuously-operating fountain in Rome.

The Tempietto del Bramante. A small circular building from fifteen-oh-two on the western side of Trastevere, behind the church of San Pietro in Montorio. Donato Bramante designed it as a memorial to the spot where, by tradition, Saint Peter was crucified upside-down. The Tempietto is a perfectly proportioned classical temple — sixteen Doric columns, a hemispherical dome — built at miniature scale. Bramante was directly studying ancient Roman ruins and translating them into Renaissance language. The building is tiny but it inspired three centuries of architecture. The dome of Saint Peter's Basilica, the U.S. Capitol, every domed neoclassical building in Europe and America owes something to this small tempietto. It's worth the climb up the small hill to see it.

The Villa Farnesina. Across the river from central Rome but technically in Trastevere. A small Renaissance villa with extraordinary frescoes by Raphael, Sebastiano del Piombo, and Sodoma. The cycle of the Triumph of Galatea, by Raphael, is one of his most beautiful mythological works. The villa is open most days for a small fee. Often empty.

Eating in Trastevere. The piazza in front of Santa Maria in Trastevere is full of tourist-priced restaurants. The genuinely good ones are a block away on side streets — Da Augusto, Cesare al Casaletto a bit further south, La Tana de Noantri. Roman classics: carbonara, cacio e pepe, amatriciana, saltimbocca. Most of these are simpler than they sound. Wine: a small carafe of house white or red is the way to drink with dinner in Trastevere — never bottles in tourist restaurants.

At night, Trastevere becomes a single outdoor party. The medieval streets fill with locals and tourists. Music from bars overlaps in the alleys. The pizzas come hot until midnight. It's one of the best nighttime experiences in any European capital.
