A few specific details worth pausing for.

The original Roman opus reticulatum on the walls of the spiral ramp. The diagonal grid pattern of small stones is one of Hadrian's signature construction techniques. He used it extensively at his villa at Tivoli and in his rebuilding projects in Rome. The pattern is unchanged from the year one thirty A.D. You're walking past walls that have been here for nineteen hundred years.

The vestibule where Hadrian's ashes were originally interred. About a third of the way up the spiral ramp, you'll pass a small chamber to the right. This is the funerary vestibule that led to the burial chamber proper. The room was opened and emptied in the early Middle Ages. Whatever was inside was lost. The bare walls are all that remain.

The Stradone — the wide upper passageway, originally lined with marble. The shape of the columns and architectural details is still visible in the bare wall mortar.

The Sala Paolina frescoes, in the papal audience hall. The trompe l'oeil architecture is convincing enough that, in some places, you can't tell whether you're looking at a painted column or a real one. The classical figures in the painted niches are designed to look like real bronze statues. Look at the way the painted shadow falls. It's calculated for a viewer standing in the center of the room.

Cellini's prison cell. Marked with a plaque on the wall outside. Look at the dimensions of the space. Then read his Autobiography, where he describes the room in detail — the iron grating, the smell, the dust, the dim light. Cellini was held here for several months on a charge of murder. He claimed in his memoir that he saw visions of Christ in the cell. He also recorded designing his escape attempt by detail — measuring the height of the window, calculating the length of bedsheets needed to lower himself. His escape was real, his subsequent broken leg was real, and he made it back into the Italian court within a year.

The view from the top terrace. Look along the line that runs from the castle westward toward Saint Peter's. The view down that axis — past the Tiber, across the Borgo neighborhood, and onto Saint Peter's Square — was created in the seventeenth century, when Pope Sixtus the Fifth ordered the demolition of buildings along the route to create a clear processional path. Walk to the north corner of the terrace for a view of the Tiber's bend. Walk south for the Pantheon and the historic center.

The Passetto di Borgo. If you can see it — the elevated walkway running along the top of an old Roman wall, heading toward Saint Peter's — that's the escape corridor. Sections of it are sometimes open for guided tours; check at the entrance. It's where the popes ran when they were in trouble.

The Tosca rampart. The point on the upper terrace from which Tosca leaps in Puccini's opera. There's a small placard. If you've seen the opera, this is the moment to stand exactly where the soprano hits her last note.
