You're walking up to one of the strangest buildings in Rome — a structure that has been, across its eighteen hundred years of existence, an imperial mausoleum, a Roman fortress, a medieval prison, a papal stronghold, a Renaissance treasury, an art repository, and now a public museum. The Castel Sant'Angelo has gone through more identities than any other building in the city, and it's still standing.

The basic shape is a tall cylindrical drum sitting on a square base, with a circular ring of fortifications around it and a small statue on top. From a distance, it looks vaguely like a wedding cake. Close up, the scale is intimidating. The drum is about two hundred feet wide. The walls of the cylinder are massive — at the base, more than twenty-five feet thick. The whole structure was originally clad in gleaming travertine marble, with bronze fittings and golden statues. Today, the marble is mostly gone, stripped over centuries for other Roman buildings, and the bronze fittings disappeared sometime in the Middle Ages. What remains is the brick core.

The building was originally the Mausoleum of the Emperor Hadrian — the same Hadrian who rebuilt the Pantheon. Hadrian commissioned it around the year one thirty-four A.D., as a burial place for himself and his family. The cylinder was the tomb proper, with the imperial ashes interred inside. The original design called for a small temple on top, with a quadriga — a four-horse chariot — driven by a statue of Hadrian. Over the next several hundred years, his successors continued to be buried here. The last emperor interred was Caracalla in two seventeen A.D.

The building's transformation into a fortress began under the Emperor Aurelian in the two seventies A.D., when he incorporated it into Rome's new defensive walls. By that point, the empire was contracting, and even the imperial tomb had to do double duty as military infrastructure. Later emperors fortified it further. Medieval popes adapted it as a refuge — and then, in fifteen-twenty-seven, used it as one.

You're going to walk up a long spiral ramp inside the original Hadrian-era tomb, then through a series of medieval-and-Renaissance papal apartments, and emerge on a terrace overlooking the whole of Rome. The view from the top is one of the finest in the city. The Bernini bridge — Ponte Sant'Angelo, in front of the castle — is decorated with ten angel statues by Bernini and his pupils, completed in sixteen sixty-nine. The bridge alone is worth a separate look.

Plan two hours. The route is more interesting than you'd expect.
