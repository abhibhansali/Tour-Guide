You're walking into Piazza della Rotonda, and the building in front of you — the Pantheon — has been here, almost exactly as you see it, for nearly nineteen hundred years. That's not a small claim. Most Roman buildings you see today are skeletons. This one still has its skin.

Sixteen granite columns hold up the porch. Each one is a single piece of stone — no joints, no segments — quarried in eastern Egypt and floated down the Nile and then across the Mediterranean. They're forty feet tall and weigh about sixty tons each. The Romans cut them, shipped them, and stood them up. We don't entirely know how.

Look up at the inscription. It says Marcus Agrippa built it, in his third consulship. Agrippa was Augustus's right-hand man and son-in-law, and he did build a temple here, around twenty-seven B.C. But it burned down. Twice. The building you're about to walk into was rebuilt from scratch by the Emperor Hadrian about a hundred and forty years later, around one hundred and twenty-five A.D. Hadrian kept Agrippa's name on the front anyway. Roman humility, of a kind.

The bronze doors are almost certainly original. Twenty-three feet tall, several tons each, swinging on their ancient hinges. They have been doing so since Hadrian's masons hung them.

When you step inside, give yourself a second before you look up. The interior is a single, perfect sphere — if you imagined a ball that exactly fills the room, it would touch the floor and the very top of the dome. Width and height are the same: a hundred and forty-two feet. Hadrian's architects built a hemisphere on top of a cylinder of the same height. That's the geometry of the place.

The other thing you'll notice almost immediately is that the room is not dark. There is a single round opening at the top of the dome — the oculus — twenty-seven feet across, completely open to the sky. Light pours through it in a giant tilted beam. Rain falls through it, too. For now, just stand still and look up. Then we'll talk about how this building has stayed standing for two thousand years.
