Now that you're inside, look down before you look up again. The marble pavement under your feet follows Hadrian's original pattern — restored, but the same design. Notice that it's not flat. It bulges very slightly upward in the middle. That's deliberate. Find the small drain holes set into the marble — there are twenty-two of them. When it rains through the oculus, the convex floor channels the water away. It works exactly as well today as it did under Hadrian.

Now look up. The coffers — those recessed squares on the inside of the dome — are not just decoration. Each square is a cube of weight the Romans removed from the structure. There are five rings of twenty-eight, a hundred and forty in total. Originally, each held a bronze rosette. Those were looted long ago, but you can still see the fixing holes if you look carefully.

The oculus has no glass. It never has. When it rains in Rome, it rains inside the Pantheon. There's an old story that air pressure from sacrificial fires used to push the rain back out — that one is a myth. The drains do the work. If you visit on a stormy day, you'll see the rain falling in a perfect column from sky to floor.

There's a real moment of magic on April twenty-first — Rome's traditional birthday. At noon on that day, the beam of sunlight through the oculus falls exactly on the entrance doorway, lighting up whoever stands there. Hadrian's architects designed this. The emperor walked into a temple lit by the sun itself. Even if you're not here on the twenty-first, watch the beam move across the wall as you stand there. It moves surprisingly fast.

Two things you might walk past. On the right-hand side of the rotunda, about a third of the way around, there's a simple plaque marking the tomb of Raphael. The painter chose to be buried here in fifteen-twenty. His epitaph, in Latin, reads: "Here lies Raphael, by whom Nature feared to be conquered while he lived, and feared to die when he died."

A few steps further around, you'll see the tombs of two kings — Vittorio Emanuele the Second, the first king of unified Italy, and his son Umberto. They are guarded, by tradition, by volunteers from the old royal family.

And when you walk out, glance up at the underside of the porch ceiling. The rough patch is where Pope Urban's workers cut away the bronze. The scar is still there.
