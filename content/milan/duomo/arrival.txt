You're walking into Piazza del Duomo, and the building in front of you was under construction for five hundred and seventy-nine years. The first stones were laid in thirteen eighty-six. The last details — small bronze doors, a few final sculptural pieces — were completed in nineteen sixty-five. That makes Milan Cathedral one of the longest construction projects in human history. Many of the workers who started it could not have imagined the buildings standing across the piazza when it finally finished. They were working for a future they would never see.

The cathedral is huge. Roughly five hundred and thirty feet long, sixty-six thousand square feet of interior space, capacity for forty thousand worshippers. It's the largest church in Italy that isn't Saint Peter's — and depending on how you measure, it might be the largest by volume even including Saint Peter's. The Italians argue about this. So do the Romans.

What you're really looking at, though, is the surface. The exterior is encrusted with about thirty-four hundred statues and one hundred and thirty-five spires. No two are exactly alike. The Madonnina — the gilded statue of the Virgin Mary on the highest spire — stands four meters tall and watches over the city from three hundred and fifty-six feet up. By tradition, no building in Milan was supposed to rise above her. That tradition lasted into the twentieth century. Newer skyscrapers have surpassed her, but most of them now carry a small replica of the Madonnina on top, as a kind of polite acknowledgment.

The marble is pink-and-white Candoglia marble, quarried from a single mountain northwest of Milan. The dukes of Milan endowed the cathedral foundation with the entire quarry in the fourteenth century. The quarry still belongs to the cathedral. Every replacement stone for repairs, every restoration, every new piece, comes from the same source it has come from for six hundred years. The marble blocks float down the Ticino river to the cathedral workshop, where stonemasons cut and finish them on site. The job has been continuous since thirteen eighty-six. It never really stops.

A note about the front you're looking at. The Italianate Baroque doorways were started in the late fifteen hundreds. The Gothic-style upper façade, with its swirling tracery and pinnacles, was completed by Napoleon's order between eighteen-oh-five and eighteen thirteen. Napoleon was crowning himself King of Italy inside the cathedral and wanted the front finished for the occasion. The pink-and-white pattern, the slender Gothic windows, the upward thrust of the spires — much of that is essentially Napoleonic.

Step inside. The interior is calmer, darker, more vertical than the exterior promises.
