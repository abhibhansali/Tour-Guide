A few things to look for, inside and out.

The statue of Saint Bartholomew, in the right transept of the nave. He's depicted, gruesomely, holding his own flayed skin draped over his shoulder like a coat. The saint was traditionally martyred by being skinned alive, and Renaissance sculptors loved the anatomical opportunity. This particular Bartholomew, by Marco d'Agrate from fifteen sixty-two, is famously accurate — every muscle, every vein, every tendon is carved as though the figure were a flayed cadaver. The sculptor was so proud of it that he carved an inscription on the base: "I was not made by Praxiteles, but by Marco d'Agrate." Praxiteles was the great classical Greek sculptor. It's a Renaissance brag.

The brass meridian line embedded in the cathedral floor, near the entrance. Look for a thin metal strip running across the marble pattern, marked with zodiac symbols. A small hole in the south wall, near the ceiling, lets in a sunbeam that crosses the meridian at noon. The system was installed by Brera Observatory in seventeen eighty-six, and it's been calibrating Milanese time for two hundred and fifty years.

The pillars supporting the nave. Look at the column capitals — the carved tops where the columns meet the vaulting. Each capital is different. Some are simple acanthus leaves. Others are full sculptural compositions with niches containing statues of saints. The intent was to create a stone forest with each tree being unique. Walk slowly down the central aisle and look up. Most visitors miss this entirely.

On the roof terraces, look for the row of gargoyles along the lower edge. Each one is different. Some are dragons; some are monkeys; some are animals from medieval bestiaries that nobody can identify; some are clearly portraits of specific medieval Milanese. The sculptors had license to add personal touches. The roof was, after all, far from official inspection.

In the south transept, find the statue of Pope Pius the Fourth, which is positioned next to a small Madonna that appears to wink. The Madonna's eye, due to a flaw in the marble, has a small dark spot that gives the impression of a closed lid. Generations of Milanese children have been told the Madonna winked at one of them.

And finally, on the way out, look for the small plaque marking the spot where, in eighteen seventy-six, the body of Manzoni — the great novelist who wrote The Betrothed, the founding novel of modern Italian literature — was laid in state for his funeral. Verdi composed his Requiem for the occasion. The Requiem premiered in this cathedral, with seven hundred singers and a hundred instrumentalists. It is one of the great moments in Italian cultural history, and the plaque commemorating it is easy to walk past.
