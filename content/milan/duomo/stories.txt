A few stories that put faces on this building.

The Holy Nail. Above the altar, embedded high in the apse, there's a small wooden tabernacle. Inside it, by tradition, is a nail from the True Cross — one of the three nails used in the crucifixion of Christ, believed to have been brought to Milan by Saint Helena, the mother of the Emperor Constantine, in the fourth century. It's been considered the cathedral's most sacred relic since the building was a building. Once a year, on September fourteenth — the feast of the Exaltation of the Cross — the archbishop is hoisted up to the tabernacle on a wooden device called the Nivola, a kind of cloud-shaped lift. He retrieves the nail, brings it down, and shows it to the congregation for forty hours, then returns it. The Nivola was designed by Leonardo da Vinci. It still works.

Carlo Borromeo and the plague. In fifteen seventy-six, plague hit Milan. The city panicked; the wealthy fled. Archbishop Carlo Borromeo refused to leave. He organized care for the dying, distributed food, walked through the streets blessing the sick. He led processions in which he personally carried a giant crucifix while barefoot. He bankrupted himself emptying the church treasury to buy bread for the poor. The plague killed seventeen thousand Milanese. Borromeo survived. He was canonized within thirty years of his death. The crystal coffin in the crypt holds his body, dressed in archbishop's vestments, with a silver mask over his face. You can see him today.

Napoleon's coronation. In the late spring of eighteen-oh-five, Napoleon arrived in Milan with the iron crown of Lombardy. He placed it on his own head, refusing the help of the archbishop. The crown is a real medieval object — a circle of gold with an inner ring said to be forged from a nail of the True Cross. (Possibly the same nail. Possibly a different one. The Catholic Church is generous about how many nails the cross had.) Napoleon kept it. After his fall, it was returned to Italy. It now lives in the cathedral at Monza, just outside Milan.

The cathedral master. From the fourteen hundreds onward, the position of capo maestro of the cathedral — the master craftsman in charge of construction — was the highest professional appointment in Milan. The job was passed down within families for generations. The same family of stonemasons, the Brentani, worked on the cathedral for more than four hundred years. There are still working stonemasons on the cathedral who learned the trade from fathers who learned from grandfathers. The building has trained roughly twenty generations of craftsmen.

A final story. In nineteen forty-three, during the Allied bombing of Milan, an incendiary bomb hit the cathedral roof. It bounced off the marble, slid down between two spires, and lodged itself harmlessly in a drainpipe. The bomb was defused the next morning. Locals at the time said the Madonnina had pushed it aside.
