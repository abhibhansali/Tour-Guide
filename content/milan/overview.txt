Milan doesn't look like an Italian city at first. Where Rome is ancient and Florence is Renaissance and Naples is chaotic, Milan is industrial — gray, businesslike, oriented around fashion and finance and design rather than around the past. This appearance is misleading. The city is two thousand three hundred years old, and almost every important Italian story has passed through it.

The name comes from the Celts. Around four hundred B.C., a Celtic tribe called the Insubres founded a settlement here on the Lombard plain, halfway between the Alps and the Apennines. They called it Medhe-lan, which roughly meant "the middle of the plain" — the place at the center of the lowland. The Romans, when they conquered it in two hundred and twenty-two B.C., latinized this to Mediolanum. The modern name Milano is just the worn-down survival of that.

The Romans took Mediolanum seriously. It was the gateway between Italy and Gaul, the natural staging ground for any army moving north or south across the Alps. By the third century A.D., as Rome itself was declining, Mediolanum became one of the empire's effective capitals. The emperors based themselves here for stretches. In three hundred and thirteen A.D., the Roman emperor Constantine and his colleague Licinius met in Mediolanum and issued the Edict of Milan — the decree that legalized Christianity across the Roman Empire. That single legal document changed the trajectory of Western civilization. It happened in this city.

The middle ages took Milan in different directions. After the Western Roman Empire collapsed, the city was successively ruled by the Goths, the Lombards, the Franks, and a series of Holy Roman emperors. In the eleven hundreds, Milan became a self-governing commune, then the center of a regional power. The Visconti family took control in the late twelve hundreds and ruled for about a hundred and fifty years, building most of the city's medieval infrastructure including the first version of its great Gothic cathedral. The Sforza family succeeded them in the fourteen hundreds. Under the Sforza dukes, Milan became one of the major Renaissance courts of northern Italy — wealthier and more politically powerful than the better-known republics of Florence and Venice.

The most consequential figure of Sforza Milan was a Florentine engineer they hired: Leonardo da Vinci. Leonardo lived in Milan from fourteen eighty-two to fifteen-oh-three — roughly half his working life. He was not, primarily, a painter here. He was Ludovico Sforza's military engineer, hydraulic engineer, civic architect, and party planner. He designed lock gates for the Milanese canals — the same canal system whose surviving remnants you can see today at the Navigli. He designed fortifications, weapons, theatrical machinery for ducal weddings. The Last Supper, on the wall of Santa Maria delle Grazie, was a side project. The Codex Atlanticus — Leonardo's vast personal notebook of sketches and inventions, twelve hundred pages of working drawings — is still kept in Milan, at the Ambrosiana library. You can see selected pages on display.

Milan slid into Spanish, then Austrian, then briefly French control over the next several centuries. Napoleon crowned himself king of Italy in Milan's cathedral in eighteen-oh-five, wearing the Iron Crown of Lombardy. After Italian unification in eighteen sixty-one, Milan became the country's industrial engine.

The science and the industry of nineteenth-century Milan are easy to overlook but genuinely important. The Brera astronomical observatory, founded in seventeen sixty-four, made and still makes serious contributions to astronomy — its astronomers including Giovanni Schiaparelli, who in the eighteen seventies coined the word "canali" for the linear markings he thought he saw on Mars. The English translation of his word as "canals" rather than "channels" launched a hundred years of speculation about Martian civilizations. The features turned out not to exist. Alessandro Volta, who invented the electric battery, worked at the nearby University of Pavia. The chemist Amedeo Avogadro — whose number underlies all of modern chemistry — was born in Turin but lectured across northern Italy including Milan.

In the eighteen seventies, the Pirelli company started here making rubber products — eventually tires for the entire European auto industry. Alfa Romeo was founded in Milan in nineteen-ten. Pirelli, Alfa, and a dozen other Milanese firms drove the second industrial revolution in Italy. The city's population doubled. The current ring-road layout of central Milan was laid out in this era, with the railroad lines fanning out from a single grand central station built in the nineteen thirties.

The Second World War damaged Milan badly. Allied bombing in nineteen forty-three destroyed parts of the cathedral roof, the La Scala opera house, the refectory housing the Last Supper, and large parts of the working-class districts. The Last Supper survived because Italians had sandbagged its wall before the war. La Scala was rebuilt within three years and reopened with a concert conducted by Toscanini in nineteen forty-six — an event that mattered to Milan more than almost any other postwar gesture.

After the war, Milan invented Italian fashion. The first organized fashion shows here date to the nineteen fifties. By the nineteen seventies, the Quadrilatero della Moda — the four streets around Via Montenapoleone — was the global headquarters of luxury fashion. Armani, Versace, Prada, Dolce and Gabbana, Missoni all run from Milan. The fashion industry brought design with it. The Salone del Mobile, Milan's annual design fair, became the most important industrial-design event in the world. The Milanese aesthetic — minimal, refined, oriented toward materials and function — is now a global language.

A few specific scientific contributions worth knowing. The University of Milan's hospital network has produced major work on cancer treatment and cardiology. The Mario Negri Institute, founded in the nineteen sixties, is one of Europe's leading pharmaceutical research centers — and a deliberately independent one, refusing pharmaceutical industry funding to preserve credibility. Pirelli still runs an applied-research lab here on rubber chemistry and tire mechanics. The Politecnico di Milano, the city's main technical university, is consistently ranked among Europe's strongest engineering schools.

Modern Milan is the financial capital of Italy. The Borsa Italiana — the country's main stock exchange — operates from a palazzo just north of the Duomo. The headquarters of most major Italian banks and corporations are here. The city's population is about a million and a half within municipal limits, around three and a half million in the broader metropolitan area. It is the fourth-largest urban economy in the European Union, after London, Paris, and Madrid.

Two things make modern Milan strange in Italian terms. First, it is genuinely diverse. Twenty percent of the population are first or second-generation immigrants — Egyptian, Filipino, Sri Lankan, Latin American, Eastern European communities all present in substantial numbers. The rest of Italy is not like this. Second, it doesn't really care about its tourists. Rome, Florence, Venice — those cities are organized around the people who visit them. Milan is organized around the people who work here. You can spend a week and never visit the Duomo.

Milan is also a transport hub. The high-speed rail network — the Frecciarossa lines — uses Milan as its northern terminus. Trains from here reach Rome in two and a half hours, Naples in four, Paris in seven. The two main airports, Malpensa and Linate, handle most of northern Italy's international air traffic.

The city will host the Winter Olympics in twenty twenty-six, jointly with Cortina d'Ampezzo in the Dolomites. The infrastructure work for that — new metro lines, renovated stadiums, environmental remediation of the Lambro river — is reshaping the eastern districts. By the time you visit, much of that work will be visible.

Milan, in the end, is the part of Italy that decided to stop looking backward. The other Italian cities are museums to themselves. Milan is a working capital, with a Gothic cathedral and a Last Supper somewhere in the middle of it, which it visits when it has time.
