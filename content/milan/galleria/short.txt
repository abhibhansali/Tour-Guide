You're standing in what is, by a generous definition, one of the world's oldest shopping malls — and one of the most beautiful interior spaces in Milan. The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele the Second is a glass-roofed shopping arcade connecting Piazza del Duomo with Piazza della Scala, just down the street. It was completed in eighteen seventy-seven and named for the first king of unified Italy.

The architect was Giuseppe Mengoni, a man from Bologna who won the city's design competition with a plan for a soaring cross-shaped arcade roofed entirely in glass and iron. The technology was new at the time. Glass roofs of this scale had only become possible in the previous generation — the Crystal Palace in London, finished in eighteen fifty-one, had set the template. Mengoni built his version with deliberate civic grandeur: marble walls, mosaic floors, ornate cast-iron tracery in the roof framework, and an octagonal central atrium that's still one of the most photographed spaces in Milan.

He didn't live to see it. The night before the official opening — a ceremony to be presided over by King Vittorio Emanuele himself — Mengoni was inspecting the roof when he fell from the scaffolding and died. He was forty-seven. The opening went on without him. There's a small plaque inside commemorating the fact.

Look at the floor as you walk in. The mosaic patterns include the coats of arms of the four cities that were major Italian centers at the time of unification: Milan (with its red cross on white), Rome (with the she-wolf nursing Romulus and Remus), Florence (with its red lily), and Turin (with a bull rampant). Find the Turin bull — it's set in the floor near the south entrance, the one facing the Duomo. The bull has been worn down to a smooth depression on a specific spot: its testicles.

The Milanese have a tradition. Place your right heel on the bull's testicles, spin around three times, and you'll have good luck — usually in love or in business, depending on who's telling the story. The custom is at least a hundred years old. The local government periodically restores the bull, only to have visitors wear it down again within months. It's the most accidentally tactile mosaic in Italy.

The Galleria has been a luxury shopping address since the day it opened. The original tenants included Prada — the leather-goods firm started by Mario Prada in eighteen-thirteen — and Borsalino, the hat-maker, plus several long-defunct old-fashioned cafes. Today the shops are dense and expensive: Louis Vuitton, Gucci, Versace, Prada (still here), Armani. The prices are theatrical. Window shopping is the more sensible activity for most visitors.

Walk through to the north end and you'll exit onto Piazza della Scala — a smaller, quieter piazza with the famous La Scala opera house on one side, a statue of Leonardo da Vinci in the middle, and the Galleria d'Italia museum on the right. La Scala doesn't look like much from outside — its plain neoclassical façade hides one of the most acoustically perfect opera houses in the world. If you can get into a performance, do.

The Galleria itself is busiest at midday and during the early evening passeggiata, the traditional Italian after-work stroll. Locals come here to walk, see, and be seen. The cafes have outdoor seating tucked under the glass roof — exorbitant prices but a fair trade for the view. The Camparino bar, on the Duomo side, has been serving Campari since eighteen sixty-seven; it's one of the original bars in the whole arcade and a piece of Milanese history all by itself.

At night the Galleria is lit from inside and looks particularly striking. The crowds thin around ten. Walk through it slowly, look up at the iron and glass, look down at the mosaic, and remember that someone built this entire thing — and died for it — to be a single piece of urban beauty.
