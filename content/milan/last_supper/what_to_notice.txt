You have fifteen minutes. Here are the things I'd point at specifically.

Judas. He's the fourth from the left, leaning back, in shadow. He's the only apostle whose face is in darkness. He's the only one who's pulled back from the table. He's the only one clutching something — a small bag, almost certainly the thirty pieces of silver. And he has just knocked over a salt cellar with his elbow. The spilled salt on the white tablecloth is a small, brilliant detail. In medieval European folklore, spilling salt was a sign of betrayal or bad faith. Leonardo is telling you, by way of a small painted accident, which of the twelve has done what.

The hand at Christ's throat. Look just to the left of Christ — at Peter, leaning forward. Peter is holding a knife in his right hand, gesturing forward, blade angled almost upward. Centuries of Christians have argued about whether the knife is meant for Christ's defender or his betrayer. Leonardo gives no answer. The knife is just there, pointed in the general direction of Judas.

John. Sitting to Christ's immediate right, leaning toward Peter, hands clasped, eyes downcast. John has historically caused trouble for art historians and conspiracy theorists. The face is feminine. The hair is long. Some critics (most famously the writer of a recent thriller you may have heard of) have argued that this is actually Mary Magdalene, painted as part of a secret message. The art historical consensus is otherwise: Leonardo painted John as the youngest apostle, traditionally beardless and youthful, and Renaissance painters routinely gave young men softened, idealized faces. The "Mary" theory is more fun than true.

The expressions. Slow down on this. Each apostle has a distinct emotional reaction. Bartholomew, far left, is leaning forward in shock. Andrew, beside him, has his palms up in a "this can't be true" gesture. James the Greater spreads his arms in horror. Thomas raises one finger — the same finger he'll later use to doubt the resurrection. Matthew turns away, asking Jude beside him. Look at three or four faces in detail. Each one is a complete psychological portrait in a few brushstrokes.

The damage. Don't ignore it. Large portions of the painting are essentially gone — you can see bare plaster in several places, especially around Christ's feet and parts of the table. Other areas are clearly later restorers' work in slightly different palettes. The painting is a palimpsest. Leonardo's hand is in there, somewhere, but most of what survives is fragments and reconstruction. That fact is part of what you came to see.

And the door. There's a doorway cut directly through the bottom center of the painting — added in seventeen sixty-eight by a careless monastery cook who wanted easier access between the kitchen and the dining hall. The cut removed Christ's feet. It's still there. The cook is anonymous.
