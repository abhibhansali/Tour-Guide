You're walking up to the Castello Sforzesco — Sforza Castle — a brick fortress that has, depending on the century, served as a defensive position, a ducal palace, an artist's workshop, a barracks, a prison, and now a complex of museums. It's one of the largest medieval castles in Europe by ground area, and it has been a Milanese landmark for almost seven centuries.

The castle was first built in the thirteen hundreds by the Visconti family, the dukes of Milan, as a defensive bulwark on the city's western edge. The original fortress was mostly destroyed in a popular uprising in fourteen forty-seven. When Francesco Sforza — a former mercenary general who'd married into the Visconti family — became duke a few years later, he rebuilt the castle as his official residence. He hired the best architects in Italy. The square plan, the four corner towers, the central courtyard system you see today are essentially his fifteenth-century design.

Two of Sforza's hires are names you'll recognize. Donato Bramante — the man who would later begin Saint Peter's in Rome — designed parts of the interior. And Leonardo da Vinci, who lived in Milan from fourteen eighty-two to fifteen-oh-three on the duke's payroll, decorated one of the castle's most striking rooms.

That room is called the Sala delle Asse — the Hall of the Planks — in the northeast tower. Leonardo painted the entire ceiling with intertwined branches of mulberry trees, knotted ropes, and naturalistic vegetation. The result is a kind of painted forest canopy, completely covering the ceiling and merging with the walls. It was painted around fourteen ninety-eight and was Leonardo's gift to the duke. The room was extensively damaged over the centuries — it was used as a barracks for a period, and the frescoes were whitewashed — but they were rediscovered in the eighteen nineties and have been undergoing careful restoration since. You can visit; the restoration is ongoing.

After the Sforza dukes lost Milan to the French in fifteen-oh-five, the castle had a long second life as a military installation. The Austrians used it. Napoleon used it. The Italian army used it after unification. By the late eighteen hundreds, it was in such bad shape that Milan was seriously considering demolishing it entirely.

It was saved by an architect named Luca Beltrami, who led a major restoration starting in the eighteen nineties. Beltrami rebuilt the central tower — the Torre del Filarete — from old drawings and prints. He restored the brickwork. He converted the interior into a series of museum galleries. The work was politically contentious; some called it imaginative reconstruction more than restoration. The result, however, is the castle Milan still has today.

Inside, the castle houses a remarkable collection. The Museum of Ancient Art, on the ground floor, contains Michelangelo's Rondanini Pietà — his last sculpture, left unfinished at his death in fifteen sixty-four. It's not the polished Pietà of his youth in Saint Peter's. This one is rough, abstract, almost expressionist. Michelangelo was eighty-eight when he died working on it. The figures of Mary and Christ are barely separated. You can see the chisel marks where he was still working two weeks before he died.

The Pinacoteca on an upper floor has paintings by Bellini, Mantegna, Filippo Lippi, and others. The Egyptian and Prehistoric collections are tucked into smaller rooms. The total visit takes about two hours if you want to see everything; one hour if you want only the Pietà.

When you exit at the back of the castle, you'll find yourself in Parco Sempione, a large public park laid out in the late nineteenth century. The park stretches for almost a kilometer to the Arco della Pace, a neoclassical triumphal arch that Napoleon had begun. The walk through the park is one of the best free experiences in central Milan. Stop by the small lake. Watch the Milanese walking their dogs. The castle's brick towers rise behind you, looking exactly as they have for five hundred years.
