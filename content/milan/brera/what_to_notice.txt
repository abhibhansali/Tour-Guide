A handful of works that justify the entire visit.

Raphael's Marriage of the Virgin, fifteen-oh-four. Painted when Raphael was twenty-one, before he moved to Rome. It depicts the marriage of Mary and Joseph in front of a perfectly symmetric temple, with a high priest blessing them. The composition is built on a geometric clarity that Raphael had taken from his teacher Perugino — but Raphael's version is sharper, more confident, more refined. Notice the broken stick in the foreground: by tradition, Mary's other suitors had presented sticks to the priest, and the one whose stick miraculously bloomed was chosen as her husband. The blooming stick is in Joseph's hand. The losers' broken sticks are at the bottom of the frame. The painting is a masterpiece of compositional balance.

Mantegna's Dead Christ, fourteen-eighty. One of the most radically foreshortened paintings of the Renaissance. Christ lies on a marble slab, feet toward the viewer, body receding into the picture plane. Mantegna deliberately made the feet smaller than the head, against the natural perspective — to keep the focus on the face. Look at the wounds. Look at the weeping figures on the left. The painting is almost unbearable to stand in front of. Mantegna kept it in his own house until he died.

Piero della Francesca's Brera Madonna, fourteen seventy-two. Mary on a throne, sleeping infant Christ in her lap, saints flanking her, a kneeling armored figure on the right (Federico da Montefeltro, the duke who commissioned it), and overhead a single suspended ostrich egg. The egg is the famous detail — it's hanging by a thread from the apse above, and its meaning has been debated for five centuries. Theories include the immaculate conception, the perfect creation, the duke's coat of arms. Piero's perspective and palette are the real reasons to look. The architectural shell behind the Madonna is a study in proportion.

Caravaggio's Supper at Emmaus, sixteen-oh-six. Christ has just been recognized by two of his disciples — a moment of revelation. The figures are caught mid-gesture. The light is harsh and theatrical. This is the Milan Supper at Emmaus; the more famous London version is in the National Gallery. Caravaggio painted the Milan version while on the run from a murder charge, and the painting reflects that — darker, less idealized, more haunted than the London piece.

Francesco Hayez's The Kiss, eighteen fifty-nine. Two figures in medieval dress embrace in a stone alcove. A subtle Italian flag is hidden in their clothing — green of the man's hose, white of his shirt, red of the woman's dress. Painted at the moment of Italian unification, the work is a coded patriotic statement disguised as a love scene. Italians know it the way Americans know Whistler's Mother. Reproductions of it hang in every cafe in Italy.

In the same wing, look for Bellini's Pietà, the Carpaccio panels, Tintoretto's portrait of Jacopo Soranzo. The collection is dense enough that you can spend hours and still feel you missed things.
