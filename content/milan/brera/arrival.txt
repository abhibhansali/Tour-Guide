You're walking into the Pinacoteca di Brera, one of the great art galleries of Northern Italy, housed inside a seventeenth-century palace that also contains an active art academy, a national library, an observatory, and a botanical garden. The whole complex is called the Palazzo Brera, and it's one of the most concentrated cultural addresses in Milan.

The Pinacoteca's collection focuses on Lombard and Venetian painting from the fourteen hundreds through the eighteen hundreds, with strong holdings of Northern Italian Renaissance and a smattering of major works from Rome, Naples, and beyond. The collection was assembled, more or less, by Napoleon. When his armies took Northern Italy in seventeen ninety-six, his administrators seized art from churches and monasteries across the region — supposedly to "preserve" them for public benefit, and not coincidentally to centralize them under French control. Napoleon turned the Brera Academy of Fine Arts into a public museum in eighteen-oh-nine, intentionally modeled on his Louvre in Paris. Many of the works on display were stolen from where they were originally painted, and they have never gone back.

You enter the palazzo through a large arched gateway off Via Brera. The first thing you'll see in the central courtyard is a bronze statue of a naked man — Napoleon himself, sculpted as Mars by Antonio Canova between eighteen-oh-six and eighteen-oh-nine. Canova was the great Italian neoclassical sculptor, and he was commissioned to render Napoleon as a heroic ancient. The result is a bronze of the emperor twelve feet tall, completely nude, holding a spear and a small winged Victory in his palm. Napoleon himself hated it. He thought it was too feminine. The statue sat in Italy and was eventually placed where it is now, in the courtyard of the gallery he founded.

Climb the grand staircase to the first floor. The galleries are arranged roughly chronologically, with some thematic clustering by region. The route is one-way only in busy season; flexible otherwise. Total time for a serious visit is about two hours; you can do the highlights in an hour.

The major works are concentrated in the central wing. The Raphael, the Mantegna, the Bellini, the Hayez, the Piero della Francesca — all in the same dozen rooms. We'll point at the specific ones to make sure you see in the next chapter.

A note on context. Milan is sometimes accused of having no good museums. The Brera is the rebuttal. It is genuinely first-rank.
