You've come up to one of the great viewpoints in Italy. Piazzale Michelangelo is a large terrace on a hill above the south bank of the Arno, looking out across the whole of central Florence. The dome of the cathedral, the bell tower beside it, the red tile roofs of the medieval city, the curve of the river — it's all there in front of you in a single view.

The terrace was designed by an architect named Giuseppe Poggi in eighteen sixty-nine. Florence had just been chosen as the capital of newly unified Italy — a temporary capital, as it turned out, but at the time everyone thought it was permanent. The city was being remodeled at breakneck speed to live up to its new status: medieval walls torn down, ring roads laid out, new neighborhoods built. Poggi designed the riverbank, the new boulevards on the south side, and this hilltop viewing terrace, all as part of one coherent urban program.

The piazzale is essentially a celebration of Florence's sculptural heritage. In the middle is a bronze cast of Michelangelo's David — bronze rather than marble because bronze handles outdoor weathering better. Around the base of the David are bronze copies of four reclining figures from the Medici Chapels: Day, Night, Dawn, and Dusk. Michelangelo carved the originals in the fifteen twenties to flank the tombs of Lorenzo and Giuliano de' Medici. The copies up here let you see all five major Michelangelo sculptures in one spot, in the open air, with central Florence as their backdrop.

The view itself takes a minute to read. Let me walk you through it.

Look down to the river. The bridge directly below is the Ponte alle Grazie. Upstream from there is the Ponte Vecchio, the only old bridge in the view — you can pick it out by the shops crowded along it. Beyond Ponte Vecchio is the Santa Trinita bridge, and further upstream are the modern bridges.

Looking past the river into the historic center: the unmistakable shape is the cathedral dome. The bell tower stands separately beside it. To the right of the cathedral, the boxy crenelated tower belongs to the Palazzo Vecchio. To the left, the cluster of buildings includes the smaller churches and palaces of the wealthy quarters. The whole medieval center fits inside a roughly diamond-shaped band of streets, hemmed in by the river and the surrounding hills.

Look further. The green hill rising on the left, with a fortress-like structure on top, is the Forte di Belvedere, built in the fifteen nineties as a Medici defensive position. The dark green slopes behind everything are the Tuscan hills — Fiesole on the right, Settignano further around. The light changes the view dramatically. Early morning is sharp and clear, with everything in front-lit color. Late afternoon turns the whole city amber. Sunset is when most visitors come.

A practical note. The piazzale is easy to reach on foot if you're willing to climb. The footpath called the Costa San Giorgio takes about twenty minutes from the river and has its own merits — it climbs through a quieter part of the city, past the old Galileo house and the rose garden. Buses run up from the center if you'd rather skip the climb. The number twelve and the number thirteen both stop here. A small cafe and a couple of souvenir stands are usually open. The bathrooms are at the back, near the bus stop.

If you're here at sunset, the trick is to arrive an hour before — sunset crowds are intense, especially in summer, and the railings fill up fast. Find a spot, sit on the steps, watch the light change. The city in front of you turns from yellow to gold to pink to gray over about forty minutes. The cathedral dome is the last thing to go dark.

When the lights of the city come on, the dome glows like a lantern. It's worth the climb.
