You're walking into the Accademia Gallery — a small museum, mostly known for one specific reason: this is where Michelangelo's David lives.

The David has been here since eighteen seventy-three. Before that, he stood in Piazza della Signoria, where Michelangelo placed him in fifteen-oh-four, exposed to weather, pigeons, vandalism, and the occasional thrown chair from political riots. By the late eighteen hundreds, the statue was in trouble — surface damage, eroded marble, the left arm broken once and reattached. The Italian state moved him indoors, into a building specifically renovated to house him. He's been here ever since. The copy you saw outside the Palazzo Vecchio is good, but the real thing is in front of you.

The David is the headline. But the Accademia is more than a single sculpture. The collection also includes four of Michelangelo's unfinished "Prisoners" — male figures emerging from rough blocks of marble, abandoned mid-carving — and a powerful unfinished Saint Matthew. The unfinished works are arranged along the gallery's main corridor, leading up to the David at the end. The arrangement is theatrical: you walk past the figures struggling to escape their stone, then arrive at the David, fully realized, the question answered.

There are also painting galleries — fourteenth and fifteenth-century Florentine works by Lippi, Botticelli, Ghirlandaio, and others. They're worth a look but not the reason most people come. There's a museum of historical musical instruments on the upper floor — the Medici grand-ducal collection, including violins by Stradivarius and Amati. Few people visit it. The room is calm and unexpectedly beautiful.

But everyone comes for the David.

Plan an hour and a half. Longer if you want to slow down with the paintings or the instruments. The David alone deserves twenty minutes — most visitors give it three. Take the time.

The David stands at the end of a long corridor in a specially-designed tribune topped with a circular skylight. The light is soft and even, calculated for the white marble. Walking up to the statue, you experience him gradually getting larger — you see his back first, then his side, then his full front. Michelangelo carved him at seventeen feet tall, more than twice life-size, exaggerated proportions and all. He was meant to be seen from below, from the piazza he originally inhabited. Here, indoors, the angle is unusual. We'll talk about what to look at specifically in a minute.

For now: take a breath before you walk up. The David is one of the works of art most people travel to Italy for. He's worth a moment of attention.
