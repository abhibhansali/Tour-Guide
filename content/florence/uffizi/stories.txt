A few stories that animate the collection.

Botticelli and the bonfire. Sandro Botticelli painted the Primavera and the Birth of Venus in the early fourteen eighties for Lorenzo de' Medici's cousin Lorenzo di Pierfrancesco. They were Lorenzo's most popular works, mythological and serene. Then, in the fourteen nineties, the Dominican friar Savonarola took political control of Florence and began his fundamentalist campaign against luxury and pagan art. Savonarola's followers held "Bonfires of the Vanities" in fourteen ninety-seven and fourteen ninety-eight, burning paintings, books, and decorative goods. By contemporary accounts, Botticelli — who'd come under Savonarola's spell — burned several of his own works on those bonfires. We don't know exactly what was destroyed. The Primavera and the Birth of Venus survived because they were already in a Medici villa outside the city.

Caravaggio's Medusa. Look for it in the Caravaggio room — a circular shield depicting the severed head of Medusa, mouth open mid-scream, snake hair writhing. Caravaggio painted it in fifteen ninety-eight, but he painted it on a ceremonial shield rather than canvas. The shield was meant as a gift from Cardinal del Monte to Cosimo the Second de' Medici, a flattery sent from Rome to Florence. The painted Medusa was supposed to project the Medici grand duke's power outward — as though the duke were Perseus, holding up the head of his defeated enemy. Caravaggio gave Medusa his own face. The screaming head is, unmistakably, a self-portrait.

Leonardo and the Adoration. In one of the side rooms, you'll find an enormous unfinished panel by Leonardo da Vinci called the Adoration of the Magi. Leonardo started it in fourteen eighty-one for a monastery in San Donato, was paid in advance, and then left Florence for Milan in fourteen eighty-two without finishing it. The panel is mostly underdrawing — figures, architecture, horses, all blocked in but never properly painted. It's one of the most beautiful unfinished works in art history. You can see Leonardo's mind working: he wasn't a procrastinator so much as a man who could not stop revising. He kept the painting in his studio for years before eventually walking away from it. The Medici acquired it in the early sixteen hundreds.

Titian and the Venus of Urbino. Titian painted his Venus of Urbino in fifteen thirty-eight for the Duke of Urbino. It's a lounging nude on a bed in a private bedroom — the most explicit major painting of the Renaissance, with frank eye contact between the viewer and the model. The painting is often described as a wedding gift, possibly representing marital domestic love. Modern scholars are skeptical: the pose is more suggestive than ceremonial, and the painting was likely commissioned simply because the Duke wanted a nude. The work shocked some of its contemporaries and inspired generations of painters — Manet's Olympia, three centuries later, is a direct response.

The Medici lions. In several rooms, look for small painted lions — the Medici family heraldic emblem. Cosimo the First decorated his administrative offices with the family crest everywhere, embedded in ceiling decorations, on door frames, in marble inlays. The family had run Florence for two hundred and fifty years by Cosimo's time, and the lion was the casual reminder of that.
