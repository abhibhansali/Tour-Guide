Let me explain what Brunelleschi actually did, because it's still genuinely strange.

The problem he faced: how to span a hundred-and-forty-three-foot opening with masonry, without any internal scaffolding. The traditional Roman method — pour concrete onto a wooden hemisphere — wouldn't work because the hole was too large and Tuscany didn't have enough timber. The traditional Gothic method — flying buttresses to absorb the dome's outward thrust — would have looked ugly on the existing cathedral.

His answer was elegant. Build two domes, one inside the other, and let them stabilize each other.

The outer dome is what you see from the city — the red-tiled curve. The inner dome is what you see from inside the cathedral — the frescoed hemisphere. The two are connected by stairs and corridors. You can climb between them. The structural job is done by the inner dome, which is heavier and built of brick. The outer dome is mainly for protection from weather.

Inside the inner dome, the bricks are laid in a herringbone pattern — zigzagging diagonally upward. The pattern interlocks each new layer with the one below, so the bricks support each other while the mortar is still wet. The dome can rise without falling inward, layer by layer, no scaffolding required from underneath. The technique was probably borrowed from Persian and Roman precedents that Brunelleschi had studied, but he applied it at a scale no one had attempted.

The shape isn't a hemisphere. It's pointed — what's called a "pointed fifth," after a Tuscan geometric proportion. The pointed shape made the dome more stable under its own weight: the line of force runs more vertically down the walls instead of pushing outward.

Inside the cathedral, look up at the inside of the dome. Brunelleschi had intended it to be left plain — the structure was the art. But a century and a half later, in fifteen seventy-two, Cosimo the First de' Medici commissioned Giorgio Vasari to fresco the interior with a vast Last Judgment. Vasari started it; the painter Federico Zuccari finished it after Vasari's death. The result, finished in fifteen seventy-nine, is impressive in its own right but covered up Brunelleschi's bare brick.

The cathedral floor is itself a Renaissance composition — a vast pattern of colored marble laid in geometric designs. Look down as you walk around. The colors are pink Maremma marble, green Prato marble, and white Carrara — the same three colors as the façade. Florence had a thing about those three stones.

Climb the dome if you can. The four hundred and sixty-three steps run between the two shells. You see Brunelleschi's herringbone brick at close range. You emerge on a small balcony at the base of the lantern, six hundred years above the city.
