A few specific things to look for inside and out.

The east-facing doors of the Baptistery — Ghiberti's "Gates of Paradise." The originals are now in the Museo dell'Opera del Duomo, behind glass; the panels on the Baptistery itself are exact bronze replicas. Either way, look closely. Each of the ten panels tells a complete Old Testament story in low relief — the Creation, Cain and Abel, Joseph and his brothers, Moses, David and Goliath. Ghiberti uses perspective that was new to sculpture at the time. The figures in the foreground are deeply carved; the architecture in the background is almost flat. The illusion of depth was so striking when first installed in fourteen-fifty-two that it made Ghiberti famous across Europe.

On the north side of the cathedral exterior, find the small statue of an architect holding a model of a dome. That's Brunelleschi. He's sitting in a stone niche, looking upward at his own dome. It was placed there in eighteen-thirty.

Inside the cathedral, near the back, find Giovanni Michelino's painting of Dante. It's the only contemporary likeness of Dante Alighieri inside the cathedral — Dante, who was Florentine by birth, was exiled from the city and never returned. The painting, from fourteen sixty-five, shows him holding a copy of the Divine Comedy, looking out at the viewer, with Florence on one side and the gates of purgatory on the other. Florence took six hundred years to officially revoke the exile order. They finally did, in twenty-oh-eight.

The two clocks in the nave. Both work, both keep time, but one is the older Italian system and one is the modern hour system. The original cathedral clock, from fourteen forty-three by Paolo Uccello, is the round one near the entrance, and it runs counterclockwise — Italian hours started at sunset, and the clock displays a twenty-four-hour day that resets each evening. It's the oldest functioning clock face in the world.

In the floor near the altar, look for the marble inlay of a bronze ring — that's where a solar marker is set. A small hole in the dome's lantern lets a beam of sunlight in. On the summer solstice, near noon, the beam falls exactly on the bronze ring. Toscanelli — the same astronomer who advised Columbus on his Atlantic voyage — set up this instrument in the fourteen seventies to track the seasons. It still works.

Outside, in the corner near the Baptistery, find the small ornamental column with a flat top. According to tradition, Florentines used it as a public meeting and gossip point for centuries. It's a literal town square inside the town square — the place where Florence checked in on itself.
