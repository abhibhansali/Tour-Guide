Florence is the city that decided history could be remade. For about three hundred years, between roughly thirteen hundred and sixteen hundred, this small Tuscan town produced more important paintings, sculptures, buildings, books, and ideas per square mile than any city has produced before or since. Whatever your view of the Renaissance — whether it really was a clean break with the Middle Ages or just the medieval world taking credit for itself — it is a fact that an enormous number of the people who shaped European art and thought were either born here, worked here, or died here. The cumulative output is still visible in every direction.

The name comes from the Romans. Around fifty-nine B.C., the Roman dictator Julius Caesar settled a colony for retired soldiers on a bend in the Arno River — flat ground, defensible bridge crossing, useful trading position. They called it Florentia, "the flourishing one." The Latin word survived centuries of Lombard occupation and medieval renaming, and Florence is essentially still that name. The Roman street grid is still partially traceable in the modern city center, particularly around Piazza della Repubblica, which sits exactly where the Roman forum once was.

Florence's classical history is unremarkable. It was a minor provincial town in Roman times. Through the early Middle Ages it was sometimes Byzantine, sometimes Lombard, then Frankish, then part of various imperial holdings. The city only became important in its own right around the year ten hundred, when it became a self-governing commune — like Venice and Milan, an Italian city-state in the European feudal landscape.

The thing that made Florence rich and ultimately revolutionary was banking. By the twelve hundreds, Florentine merchants were running the most sophisticated financial network in Europe. They traded wool — bringing cheap English wool to Italy, dyeing and weaving it in Florence, selling it back to Northern Europe at three times the price. The profits flowed through Florentine banks, which became Europe's effective central financial system. The florin, the city's gold coin, became the international currency of medieval Europe.

The most important Florentine banking family, beginning in the late thirteen hundreds, was the Medici. The Medici started as wool merchants. By the early fourteen hundreds they had become bankers to the papacy itself, holding the accounts of multiple popes. They invested their banking profits in art and architecture and political influence. Cosimo de' Medici — the elder Cosimo — effectively ran Florence from behind the scenes from fourteen thirty-four until his death in fourteen sixty-four. His grandson Lorenzo the Magnificent ran it openly from fourteen sixty-nine until his death in fourteen ninety-two.

The Medici did something extraordinary. They didn't just collect art. They patronized scholarship — bringing classical Greek and Roman texts back into circulation. They subsidized artists for years at a stretch, freeing them to experiment. They commissioned major civic buildings. Cosimo the Elder funded Brunelleschi's dome for the cathedral. Lorenzo the Magnificent patronized a teenage Michelangelo, who lived in Lorenzo's household sculpture garden. The same family also produced two popes (Leo the Tenth and Clement the Seventh), two queens of France (Catherine and Marie de' Medici), and a grand-ducal dynasty that ruled Florence until seventeen thirty-seven.

The art and architecture of fifteenth-century Florence is the part everyone knows. Brunelleschi's dome. Donatello's bronze David. Masaccio's frescoes in the Brancacci Chapel, where perspective was first painted with mathematical precision. Botticelli's Primavera and Birth of Venus. Michelangelo's David. Leonardo da Vinci, born in a village fifteen miles west of Florence in fourteen fifty-two. Raphael, who spent his crucial early years here. The list goes on long enough that historians have stopped trying to count.

What is less famous is that Florence also reinvented science. Galileo Galilei was Tuscan — born in Pisa, but he spent his later working years in Florence as court mathematician to the Medici grand dukes. His telescope observations confirming the Copernican model were done from a villa just outside the city. Galileo's pendulum experiments, the foundation for modern timekeeping, came partly from sitting in Florence's cathedral and watching a bronze lamp swing on a long chain. The Galileo Museum, near the Uffizi, displays the actual telescopes he used. His severed middle finger, removed during a body transfer in seventeen thirty-seven, is also on display.

Bartolomeo Cristofori, the Paduan instrument-maker who served the Medici court in the early seventeen hundreds, invented the piano in Florence. The earliest known piano — labeled "gravicembalo col piano e forte," harpsichord with soft and loud — was built here in seventeen-twenty. The first one ever made still exists, in the musical instrument collection at the Accademia Gallery upstairs from the David. Almost nobody visits that room.

Luca Pacioli, a Franciscan friar working in Florence in the late fourteen hundreds, wrote the first European treatise on double-entry bookkeeping. His book Summa de Arithmetica, published in fourteen ninety-four, codified the accounting techniques the Medici banks had been using. Modern finance — the auditing of accounts, the separation of business assets from personal assets, the recording of every transaction in two parallel columns — descends directly from Pacioli's Florentine treatise. Pacioli was also a close friend of Leonardo da Vinci. The two men collaborated on a book on geometric proportions.

The eighteenth century saw Florence's strangest scientific institution: La Specola. Founded in seventeen seventy-five as the Imperial and Royal Museum of Physics and Natural History, La Specola housed the world's largest collection of anatomical wax models — life-sized depictions of human anatomy in extraordinary detail, made for medical students who couldn't access enough cadavers. The models, made by a workshop attached to the museum, are still on display. They are simultaneously gorgeous and unsettling. The museum is just south of the Arno and not crowded.

The Medici line ended in seventeen thirty-seven with the death of Gian Gastone, the last grand duke. Florence passed to the Habsburg-Lorraine dynasty by treaty. The very last Medici, Anna Maria Luisa, signed the Family Pact that bound the new rulers to keep the entire Medici art collection in Florence permanently. That pact is why the Uffizi exists as a public museum and not as a dispersed collection in Vienna and Paris. Anna Maria Luisa died in seventeen forty-three. Without her, you would be visiting half of these works in other countries.

After Italian unification in eighteen sixty-one, Florence served briefly as the capital of the new Italian state — from eighteen sixty-five until eighteen seventy-one. The Italian government renovated the city in a hurry, demolishing medieval districts and laying down ring boulevards in the Parisian style. Many Florentines resented the changes. The capital eventually moved to Rome.

The twentieth century brought two catastrophes. The Germans, retreating from Florence in August nineteen forty-four, blew up every bridge across the Arno except the Ponte Vecchio. They destroyed the medieval districts at both ends of that bridge instead, to make it impassable. The buildings were rebuilt after the war. The bridge survived.

The second catastrophe was the great flood of November fourth, nineteen sixty-six. The Arno overflowed after weeks of rain. A wall of water twenty feet high hit Florence overnight. Hundreds of paintings, thousands of books, and entire libraries were submerged in mud. The international response — thousands of volunteers, dubbed the "mud angels," who came to save the collections — became the founding moment of modern art conservation science. The techniques developed in Florence in the years after the flood are now standard worldwide.

Modern Florence is small. About three hundred and seventy thousand people live in the historic center and surrounding districts. The economy runs on tourism (around sixteen million visitors a year), high-end leather goods, textiles, and food production. Tuscan cuisine — the bistecca, the ribollita, the pici, the cantucci — is one of Italy's most distinctive regional traditions, and most of it codified here.

Florence is the most concentrated dose of European cultural history available in a single walkable footprint. Almost every important name in Renaissance painting, sculpture, science, banking, or political philosophy passed through this city. You can walk past their tombs in Santa Croce, see their work in the Uffizi, climb the buildings they built. There is nowhere else like it.
