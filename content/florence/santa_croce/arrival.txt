You're walking into Basilica di Santa Croce — the Holy Cross Basilica — and what looks at first like a beautiful Franciscan church is actually the closest thing Italy has to a national pantheon. Italy's poets, scientists, artists, and political thinkers are buried here. Michelangelo. Galileo. Machiavelli. Rossini. The painter Foscolo. The inventor Marconi. Walk through the nave and you'll pass their tombs in succession, every few steps, against the right and left walls. The Italian writer Ugo Foscolo called this place the "temple of Italian glories." It is.

The piazza outside is huge — one of the largest open squares in Florence — and was originally used for jousting and public ceremonies. The white-and-green marble facade of the church is, like the Florence Cathedral's, a nineteenth-century addition. The church behind it dates from twelve ninety-four. The facade was completed in eighteen sixty-three, in a style imitating the gothic original. The mismatch in age between facade and church is unusual but typical of Florence.

The church belongs to the Franciscan order, founded by Saint Francis of Assisi in the early thirteen hundreds. The Franciscans built it as a teaching church — large nave, simple plan, walls suitable for preaching frescoes. Santa Croce is one of the largest Franciscan churches in the world. Inside, the architecture is austere by Florentine standards: a long basilica plan, slim columns, modest decoration of the stone surfaces. The drama is in the frescoes and the tombs, not in the building itself.

Plan two hours for a careful visit. The church is large, the side chapels deep, and several of them are major frescoed spaces by Giotto and his school.

A few orienting points. The main nave runs east-west. As you walk in, the tombs along the right wall include Michelangelo's (first), Dante's cenotaph (a memorial — Dante is actually buried in Ravenna), Vittorio Alfieri's, Niccolò Machiavelli's. Along the left wall: Galileo's, Foscolo's, and others. The high altar at the far end. The major frescoed chapels are off the transept — Bardi Chapel on the right, Peruzzi Chapel adjacent.

Behind the church, through a side door, is the Pazzi Chapel — a small Renaissance chapel by Brunelleschi from the fourteen forties, considered one of his architectural masterpieces. It's reachable from the cloister behind the church and is included in your ticket. Don't skip it.

The whole complex tells a story about how Florence has chosen to remember its dead. A monastery that was supposed to be humble and Franciscan slowly became the national mausoleum of a country that didn't exist yet.
