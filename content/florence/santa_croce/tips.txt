Entry is about eight euros, including the church and the Pazzi Chapel and the small museum.
Open most days; closed Sundays in the morning when masses are happening.
The church is a working religious space. Modest dress required: shoulders covered, knees covered.
Plan ninety minutes to two hours.
The Pazzi Chapel is reached through the cloister behind the church. Don't leave without seeing it — many visitors do.
The frescoes in the Bardi and Peruzzi chapels are lit on a timer that requires inserting a coin (usually one euro). Lights last about two minutes. Bring small change.
Photography is allowed, no flash.
The piazza outside is large and full of cafes. Most are tourist-priced. Walk a few streets east for better.
The leather school behind the church — Scuola del Cuoio — was founded after the war to train war orphans. Real leather goods, made on site, at fair prices. Worth a visit even if you're not buying.
Free entry on the first Sunday of each month for residents (and sometimes for foreigners — check at the door).
The Galileo Museum is a five-minute walk west, near the Uffizi. If Galileo's tomb intrigues you, his actual scientific instruments — and his middle finger — are there.
