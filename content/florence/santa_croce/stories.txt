A few stories about the people in this church.

Galileo's wandering body. After Galileo died in sixteen forty-two, the Inquisition refused to permit a public church burial. He had been convicted of heresy nine years earlier for advocating that the Earth orbits the Sun, and the church wanted no monument that might dignify his ideas. He was secretly buried in a closet at the back of Santa Croce. There he stayed for ninety-five years. In seventeen thirty-seven, when the Habsburg-Lorraine took over Tuscany from the Medici, the new grand duke quietly arranged for Galileo's remains to be moved to a proper tomb along the nave. During the transfer, three fingers, a tooth, and a vertebra were removed by enthusiastic supporters who wanted relics. Most of those bits are now in the Galileo Museum down the street, near the Uffizi. You can visit them. Galileo's middle finger is on display in a glass case.

The Pazzi family and their chapel. The Pazzi were one of the wealthiest banking families in fourteen seventies Florence. They conspired with Pope Sixtus the Fourth to assassinate the Medici brothers, Lorenzo and Giuliano, in the cathedral during Sunday Mass on April twenty-sixth, fourteen seventy-eight. The plot succeeded against Giuliano, who was stabbed to death in front of the altar. Lorenzo survived. The reprisal was brutal: most of the conspirators were caught the same week and executed by hanging from the windows of the Palazzo Vecchio. The family's chapel here at Santa Croce — the elegant Brunelleschi-designed Pazzi Chapel — was completed during the plot's planning. The patriarch, Andrea Pazzi, had died before the conspiracy. His chapel survived, even as his descendants' fortunes were obliterated.

Niccolò Machiavelli's exile. Machiavelli served as a senior official in the Florentine Republic from fifteen-oh-one to fifteen twelve. When the Medici returned to power in fifteen twelve, he was dismissed, imprisoned briefly, tortured, and exiled to a small farm outside the city. There he wrote The Prince, his cynical guide to political power, in an effort to win his way back into Medici favor. The book did not have the intended effect. Machiavelli died in fifteen twenty-seven without ever returning to political life. He was buried in Santa Croce. The Florentines, slowly, decided he was a national hero. His monument here is the political afterthought.

Foscolo's prayer. The Italian poet Ugo Foscolo, writing during the Napoleonic occupation in eighteen-oh-six, wrote a long poem called Dei Sepolcri, "On Sepulchres." The poem describes Santa Croce specifically as the place where the "great souls of Italy" rest. The poem was a kind of nationalist prayer — written for an Italy that didn't yet exist as a state. Foscolo's poem became enormously influential in the nineteenth-century Italian nationalist movement. When the Italian state eventually did unify in eighteen sixty-one, Santa Croce was already understood as its spiritual founding document. Foscolo himself is buried in the church. The literary establishment moved his remains here from England in eighteen seventy-one.

The flood. November fourth, nineteen sixty-six. The Arno had been swollen by weeks of rain. The dam upstream of Florence released its load to prevent its own collapse, sending an enormous surge of water into the city. The flood hit Santa Croce hardest of all major Florentine churches, because the piazza outside was a low point. Water rose twenty feet inside the church. Cimabue's giant Crucifix, which had hung above the high altar for seven hundred years, was partly destroyed. Italian volunteers and international experts — the so-called "mud angels" — worked for years to salvage what they could. The Cimabue Crucifix is now back in Santa Croce, partly restored but unmistakably damaged. The water line on the church walls is still visible if you look for it.
