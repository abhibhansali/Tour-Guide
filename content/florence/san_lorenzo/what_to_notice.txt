Here's what to look at in each part of the complex.

In the basilica's main nave. Brunelleschi's space is the headline, but two other things deserve attention. Donatello's bronze pulpits, near the high altar — two square boxes covered in low-relief panels depicting the Passion and Resurrection of Christ. Donatello carved them in his last years, in the fourteen sixties; they're his final major work. The figures are deliberately rough, hurried, raw — the work of an old man not interested in polish anymore. They're some of the most emotionally direct Renaissance sculptures anywhere. Most visitors walk past without looking.

The high altar inscription. Below the altar, a simple Latin inscription marks the tomb of Cosimo de' Medici, the family patriarch who funded the church. The inscription calls him Pater Patriae — father of the country — a title voted to him posthumously by the Florentine senate. The simplicity of the burial spot is deliberate. Cosimo wanted to be remembered as a citizen, not a prince.

In the Old Sacristy. The Donatello bronze doors, low to either side of the altar alcove. Eight panels of paired apostles in animated debate. Look at the gestures and the foreshortening. Donatello had recently come back from Rome where he'd been studying classical relief sculpture. His work here is the first Renaissance bronze relief in Italy.

In the New Sacristy. The four allegorical figures — Day, Night, Dawn, Dusk — are the central works. Look at the figure of Night, on Giuliano de' Medici's tomb. Female, reclining, twisted, with closed eyes. Her body is contorted in restless sleep. Beneath her foot is an owl, sacred to night. In her arms is a mask, suggesting that night is a kind of theatrical performance the body puts on. The figure has been quoted, copied, and inspired sculptors for five hundred years.

Above each tomb sits a heroic statue of one of the dukes. Lorenzo de' Medici (the duke, not the Magnificent — a different Lorenzo) is depicted in armor with a chin resting on his hand, brooding. Giuliano is depicted in idealized classical armor, more active. Neither idealized portrait actually resembles the historical man. Michelangelo, asked why, reportedly said: "Who in five hundred years will care what they actually looked like?"

In the Chapel of the Princes. The walls are inlaid with semi-precious stones — lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, mother-of-pearl from the Persian Gulf, agates from various Mediterranean sources, jasper from Sicily. The work is called pietra dura, "hard stone," and it's a Florentine specialty. The mausoleum was supposed to also hold the Holy Sepulcher itself — the Medici grand dukes negotiated for it but were denied. The chapel was built with that ambition in mind. It feels, in person, like a slightly over-sized monument.

In the Laurentian Library. Walk slowly up the vestibule staircase. Note that the central flight has rounded steps that bulge outward, like a sculptural object. The side flights are simpler and flat. The pilasters on the walls have no obvious structural function. The columns sit on tiny brackets. Everything is calculated to make you uncomfortable, in a way that's hard to articulate. The reading room itself, at the top, is calmer — long, narrow, with reading desks running its length. The manuscripts are still in the original cabinets. Most visits to the library focus on the staircase. Walk into the reading room, sit on one of the benches, look at the wooden manuscript cases. Cosimo de' Medici's books are still in there.
