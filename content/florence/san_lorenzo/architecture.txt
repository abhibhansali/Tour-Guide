The architectural story here is genuinely important. Brunelleschi was inventing Renaissance architecture as he built this church.

In the basilica's main nave, look down the central aisle. The space is governed by clear geometric proportions: the height of the columns equals one bay of the nave, two bays equals the width of the side aisles, the whole nave is exactly four side-aisles long. The classical orders — Corinthian columns, smooth pilasters, gray pietra serena stone framing white plastered surfaces — are deployed with precise repetition. The space repeats, calmly, predictably. Nothing surprises you. Everything is in proportion.

This was new. Medieval Italian churches were built additively, with mixed styles and irregular proportions. Brunelleschi imposed mathematical regularity. He had spent years in Rome measuring classical ruins. He brought those measurements back to Florence and built modern churches that imitated the proportions, but adapted to Christian worship. The result here — and at his other church, Santo Spirito, across the river — set the template for Italian church design for two hundred years.

The Old Sacristy, off the left transept, was Brunelleschi's first completed Renaissance interior. It's a perfect cube: thirty-three feet by thirty-three feet by thirty-three feet, with a hemispherical dome on pendentives, and a smaller altar alcove at one end. The walls are bare white plaster framed in gray pietra serena. The ceiling is decorated with Donatello reliefs — figures of the four Evangelists, and bronze doors with low-relief scenes of the apostles. Every element is integrated. The space was the first time anyone had used pure classical architecture in a religious interior. Brunelleschi was effectively inventing a new visual language.

Cross over to the New Sacristy, designed a century later by Michelangelo. The space is the same size as the Old Sacristy. The proportions, on paper, are similar. But the feeling is utterly different. Michelangelo's Sacristy is anxious, tense, full of architectural contradictions. The classical orders are bent, broken, recombined into uneasy shapes. Columns sit on shallow brackets. Niches are blank where you expect statues. The whole room feels like it might be slightly unstable. Michelangelo was working at the very edge of Renaissance discipline, anticipating what would later be called Mannerism — the deliberate violation of classical rules for emotional effect.

The Michelangelo tombs in this room are also unusual. They show recumbent figures of Day, Night, Dawn, and Dusk — allegorical bodies, twisted, restless, half-finished, lying on curved sarcophagi. Above each tomb sits a heroic seated figure of one of the Medici dukes being commemorated. The dukes look idealized; the times of day look tormented. Michelangelo himself once said the figures of Day and Night wished they could sleep forever, given what they had seen.

The Laurentian Library's vestibule is Michelangelo's most radical architectural statement. The room is small and tall. Three flights of stairs converge in a sculptural composition that's been called "the most disturbing staircase in the world." Pilasters that should support load are merely decorative. Niches that should contain statues are empty. The whole vestibule violates classical rules deliberately. Architects in the centuries after Michelangelo have argued whether the room is genius or grotesque. Both views have merit.
