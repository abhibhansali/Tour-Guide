San Lorenzo's history is the history of the Medici family — and through them, of the Renaissance.

There has been a church on this site since the year three ninety-three A.D., when Saint Ambrose of Milan consecrated a small basilica here. It was the cathedral of Florence for three centuries, until that role passed to Santa Reparata, which would in turn be replaced by today's Duomo. After San Lorenzo lost its cathedral status, it became a Romanesque parish church serving the neighborhood.

In fourteen-twenty, Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici — the founder of the Medici banking dynasty — decided to rebuild the church. He had become wealthy through banking with the papal court and Florence's wool merchants. He hired Filippo Brunelleschi, the architect who'd just won the commission for the cathedral dome. Brunelleschi designed a complete rebuild in the new Renaissance style — the first major church in that style anywhere. Giovanni paid for it. The arrangement was political: the Medici were associating themselves with the cutting edge of Florentine culture, and they were doing it in their own parish church, where their household had attended Mass for generations.

Giovanni died in fourteen twenty-nine. His son Cosimo de' Medici — the elder Cosimo, who would consolidate the family's political power — took over the project. Cosimo paid for the completion of the basilica, the construction of the Old Sacristy, and most of the early decoration. The Medici family was buried in the Old Sacristy and the basilica's crypt. Cosimo himself is interred in the crypt, in a simple tomb at the center, marked by an inscription describing him as the "father of the country" — Pater Patriae — a title posthumously voted by the Florentine senate.

The New Sacristy and Michelangelo. In fifteen sixteen, Pope Leo the Tenth — born Giovanni de' Medici, the family's first pope — commissioned Michelangelo to design a marble facade for the basilica. Michelangelo spent three years on the project, including a year at Carrara selecting and shaping marble blocks. The project was canceled in fifteen-twenty for political reasons. Pope Leo died in fifteen twenty-one, and his successor Clement the Seventh redirected Michelangelo's energies toward a different Medici project: a memorial chapel for Lorenzo the Magnificent's two grandsons, who had died young. This became the New Sacristy. Michelangelo worked on it from fifteen twenty-one to fifteen thirty-four, with long interruptions caused by sieges, political upheavals, and his eventual departure for Rome.

The Chapel of the Princes was begun in sixteen-oh-four by Ferdinand the First de' Medici as a grand mausoleum for the Medici grand dukes. The chapel was designed in pure baroque opulence, using imported stones — lapis lazuli, agate, jasper, mother-of-pearl — set in elaborate inlay patterns. It took two hundred years to complete and arguably bankrupted the dynasty. Six grand dukes are buried inside.

The Laurentian Library was Pope Clement the Seventh's commission to Michelangelo, in fifteen twenty-three, to house the Medici manuscript collection. Construction began in fifteen twenty-four. Michelangelo designed the vestibule and reading room. The library opened to scholars in fifteen seventy-one, decades after his death. The collection still includes Cosimo de' Medici's original library of about eleven thousand manuscripts.
