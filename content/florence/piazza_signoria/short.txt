You're standing in the political heart of Florence — the square that has been the city's main civic gathering place for nearly eight hundred years. Piazza della Signoria isn't a beautiful square in the way Piazza Navona is. It's an asymmetric L-shape, with a fortress-like government building anchoring one corner and a sculpture loggia open on another. But everything that matters in Florentine public life — every speech, every revolt, every public execution, every public celebration — has happened here.

The big crenelated tower on your right is the Palazzo Vecchio, the Old Palace. It's been the city hall of Florence since twelve ninety-nine. The square outside it has, by tradition, been kept open as a public assembly space — the place where Florentines come together to be Florentines. The clock on the tower keeps real time. The bell at the top — the Vacca, "the cow" — used to ring for every major event in the city, from war to election results.

Now look around at the sculptures. This square is essentially an outdoor museum of Renaissance and post-Renaissance sculpture. Let me orient you.

Beside the Palazzo Vecchio entrance: a copy of Michelangelo's David, standing exactly where the original stood from fifteen-oh-four to eighteen seventy-three. Michelangelo carved the David for this spot specifically. The statue was meant as a symbol of Florentine resistance to tyranny — David, the young Israelite, defeating Goliath, the imperial bully. The original was moved indoors to the Accademia Gallery for preservation in eighteen seventy-three. The copy you're looking at is faithful, weathered, and good enough that most visitors take their photos with it without realizing.

Next to the David, an even larger figure: Hercules and Cacus, by Baccio Bandinelli. Florence asked for it as a companion piece to the David, paid Bandinelli enormous fees, and then mostly hated the result. Even contemporaries thought Bandinelli was overmatched. Critics still bring it up as a cautionary tale.

The fountain in the middle of the square is the Fountain of Neptune, by Bartolomeo Ammannati, finished in fifteen sixty-five. Florentines, who specialize in nicknaming things they don't quite respect, called the big white marble figure of Neptune "Il Biancone" — the big white one. He's stood there for almost five centuries with that name.

The bronze equestrian statue is Cosimo the First de' Medici, by Giambologna, fifteen ninety-five. Cosimo was the duke who consolidated Florence as a Medici state.

Now the small open-air gallery — the Loggia dei Lanzi — at the corner. Three giant sculptures stand under its arches. From left to right: Benvenuto Cellini's bronze Perseus, holding the severed head of Medusa, finished in fifteen fifty-four. Giambologna's Rape of the Sabines, finished in fifteen eighty-three — three figures spiraling upward, carved from a single block of marble. Hercules battling the centaur Nessus, also by Giambologna. All three are originals.

There's one more thing you should know about this square. In fifteen-ninety-eight, on May twenty-third, the friar Girolamo Savonarola was hanged and then burned at the stake on this exact spot. Savonarola had ruled Florence for four years as a fundamentalist theocrat, burning Botticelli's paintings and Florentine luxury goods in the so-called Bonfire of the Vanities right here. Then the city turned on him. A small bronze plaque set in the pavement near the fountain marks the spot where the fire was lit. Most tourists walk over it without noticing.

This is what Florence does. Every flagstone in this square has been the scene of either glory or atrocity, sometimes both, often within a few years of each other. You can take a coffee at the cafes around the edges and just absorb the layers.
