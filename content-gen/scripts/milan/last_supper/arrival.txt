You're walking up to one of the most carefully-guarded paintings on earth. The Last Supper isn't in a museum. It's still where Leonardo painted it: on the back wall of the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie, the monastery's dining hall. The Dominican monks who lived here in the fourteen nineties would have eaten their meals every day facing this painting, watching Christ announce his betrayal.

A few things to know before you go in. Your visit will be exactly fifteen minutes. The room is climate-controlled and limited to thirty people at a time. You'll pass through an airlock — a sequence of glass doors that buffer the humidity — before entering. This is because the painting is unbelievably fragile. Leonardo, characteristically, painted it using an experimental technique instead of true fresco. He didn't want to be rushed by wet plaster. He wanted to be able to layer, retouch, work slowly. So he painted with tempera and oil on dry plaster. The technique didn't survive. The painting began deteriorating during Leonardo's own lifetime. Within fifty years of being finished, it was already considered ruined. What you'll see today is, in essence, a partial trace of what Leonardo originally painted, recovered through a heroic twenty-year restoration that ended in nineteen ninety-nine.

The refectory itself is a long rectangular hall. On one end, the Last Supper. On the opposite end, a Crucifixion fresco by Donato Montorfano from fourteen ninety-five — the same year Leonardo began work next door. Most visitors barely glance at the Montorfano. Notice that the Crucifixion is in much better condition than the Leonardo, because Montorfano used real fresco technique. The contrast is a lesson in both.

The painting itself is enormous — fifteen feet tall, twenty-nine feet wide. Christ is at the center, apostles in groups of three on either side. The vanishing point of the painted architecture lines up exactly with Christ's right temple. The illusion is that the painted room is an extension of the real room. When the friars sat down to eat, they were eating with the apostles.

You'll have time to look at perhaps eight or nine specific things. Don't try to see everything. Pick a few details and look hard. We'll suggest which ones in the next chapter.

The painting has survived Napoleon's troops, who used the refectory as a stable. It has survived an Allied bombing in August nineteen forty-three that destroyed three of the room's four walls. It has survived nearly five hundred years of natural decay. It is still there, faintly. That's enough.
