Leonardo was hired by Ludovico Sforza, the Duke of Milan, around fourteen ninety-five. He'd been working in Milan for over a decade as a court engineer, architect, and occasional painter — Ludovico's all-purpose genius. The duke commissioned the Last Supper as part of a broader project to renovate the monastery of Santa Maria delle Grazie, which Ludovico had chosen as his family's mausoleum.

The subject Leonardo chose was traditional — Christ's announcement at the Last Supper that one of the twelve apostles would betray him. Painters had been depicting it for centuries. But Leonardo did something nobody had done before. He focused not on Christ at the moment of breaking bread, but on the apostles in the moment of reaction. Christ has just said the words: "One of you will betray me." The apostles recoil, gesture, accuse, ask, deny. Each face is studied. Each pose is doing emotional work. The painting is, in a real sense, the first time anyone painted twelve separate inner lives in a single image.

The work took about three years. Eyewitnesses described Leonardo's process as agonizing. He would stand in front of the wall for hours, staring at it, without picking up a brush. Then he would climb up the scaffold and add a single brushstroke and leave. The prior of the monastery complained to Ludovico that Leonardo was being lazy. Leonardo's response was that creative people work hardest when they appear to be doing nothing. He'd been searching, he said, for a face for Judas. He suggested that if the prior didn't stop nagging, he might use the prior's face. The prior never complained again.

The painting was finished around fourteen ninety-eight. By the time Vasari saw it in fifteen fifty-six, it was already badly deteriorated. The dry-plaster technique Leonardo invented was unstable, and Milan's humidity attacked the pigment from below. Within two centuries, the painting had been restored by lesser hands at least three times, each restoration adding overpainting that obscured the original further.

Then came Napoleon's army in seventeen ninety-six. They billeted troops in the refectory. The soldiers reportedly threw bricks at the painting for sport. Damage was extensive.

In August nineteen forty-three, an Allied bomb hit the monastery during the bombing of Milan. Three of the four refectory walls were destroyed. The wall with the Last Supper was protected by sandbags placed there earlier in the war by Italians who knew what they had. It survived.

The modern restoration ran from nineteen seventy-eight to nineteen ninety-nine. Twenty-one years. The restorer, Pinin Brambilla Barcilon, removed centuries of overpainting and dirt, layer by layer, with cotton swabs. What emerged is what you'll see — fragmentary, faded, partly hypothetical in places. It is the closest we can get to Leonardo's actual work.
