You're standing in front of Teatro alla Scala — La Scala — the most famous opera house in the world, and arguably the most prestigious. The building's plain neoclassical exterior tells you nothing about what it is. La Scala was deliberately designed not to advertise itself. Milan, even in seventeen seventy-eight when this building opened, believed that real culture didn't need to shout.

The theater takes its name from the church that stood here before it — Santa Maria della Scala, demolished in seventeen seventy-six to make room for a new opera house. Empress Maria Theresa of Austria, who at the time ruled Milan, commissioned the building. The architect was Giuseppe Piermarini, a leading neoclassical designer. He built fast: the theater opened just two years after the church came down, in seventeen seventy-eight, with a premiere of Antonio Salieri's opera Europa Riconosciuta.

What La Scala did, more than any single venue in opera history, was define the Italian operatic tradition. Bellini and Donizetti premiered major works here in the eighteen thirties. Verdi's career was launched at La Scala in eighteen forty-two with Nabucco. He premiered Otello and Falstaff here. Puccini followed in the eighteen nineties — Madama Butterfly premiered at La Scala in nineteen-oh-four, where it famously bombed on opening night before going on to global fame. Toscanini conducted here. Maria Callas defined Italian soprano singing on this stage in the nineteen fifties. The list goes on, and it's hard to overstate the venue's centrality. If a major Italian opera in the past two centuries opened anywhere first, it usually opened here.

The building has not had an easy life. An Allied bomb hit La Scala in August nineteen forty-three during the same raid that nearly destroyed Santa Maria delle Grazie. The roof and much of the auditorium collapsed. The theater was rebuilt by a city that, despite the war's devastation, prioritized the opera house. It reopened in nineteen forty-six with a concert conducted by Toscanini, who had been in exile for twenty years over his anti-fascist politics. The reopening was, for Milan, a more emotionally charged event than almost any other postwar gesture.

A major renovation between two thousand one and two thousand four — designed by the Swiss architect Mario Botta — modernized the stage equipment, expanded backstage facilities, and added a fly tower visible from the rear. The renovation was controversial. The result is the working theater you'll see today.

You can visit La Scala in two ways. The museum, Museo Teatrale alla Scala, occupies several rooms in the building and gives you access to a small gallery looking down into the auditorium. Cost is about ten euros, open most days. It's a quick visit but worth it just to stand in one of the loge boxes and see the famous red-and-gold horseshoe interior.

The other way is to attend a performance. Tickets range from about fifteen euros for last-row seats up to several hundred for the prime stalls. The cheap tickets are real — the upper galleries are still functional opera-loving seats. La Scala's policy is that opera should be accessible. Dress code matters: jacket and collared shirt for men in the better seats, smart attire generally. The audience is a mix of serious opera devotees and well-dressed Milanese, who treat opening night every December as the city's most important social event.

The opera season runs December through July. The opening on December seventh — the feast day of Saint Ambrose, Milan's patron saint — is one of the great social events in Italy. Tickets for opening night sell out almost a year in advance.

Across the small piazza, look for the statue of Leonardo da Vinci with four students around its base. Leonardo lived in Milan for more than half his working life, and the statue is one of the city's quiet tributes to him.
