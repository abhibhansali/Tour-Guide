You're walking into Navigli — Milan's canal district, southwest of the historic center. The neighborhood is built around two surviving canals, the Naviglio Grande and the Naviglio Pavese, both flowing in long straight lines through a district of bars, restaurants, art galleries, and small workshops. By day it's quiet. By night, especially weekend nights, it's one of the busiest social areas in Milan.

The canals are older than they look. Milan was, until the early twentieth century, a city of waterways. A medieval engineering effort beginning in the eleven seventies created a network of canals connecting Milan to the Po, the Ticino, and ultimately the Adriatic. The system was expanded across five centuries — Leonardo da Vinci worked on lock designs for it in the fourteen eighties — and at its peak there were five canals running through central Milan. The Romans had used the area for waterborne trade; the medieval engineers turned the city itself into a port.

The marble for Milan's cathedral, the Duomo, was floated down the Naviglio Grande from the Candoglia quarry in the fourteen hundreds. The canal you're standing beside is the one the cathedral was built from. Boats unloaded their cargo at a wharf near where Piazza Sant'Eustorgio now sits.

The canals served Milan as both transport network and water supply until well into the nineteen hundreds. By the early twentieth century, with railways and motorized trucks displacing barge traffic, the network was less useful. In the nineteen twenties and thirties, much of the canal system was filled in to make way for streets. Most of central Milan today sits on top of buried canals. Navigli is what survived.

What survived survived because the Navigli district was working class — industrial workshops, small factories, modest housing for the people who worked them — and the city didn't see it as worth the cost of filling in. So the canals stayed, even as the rest of Milan's water became invisible. The Navigli's modern identity was shaped by the artists and bohemians who moved in during the nineteen seventies, when rents were cheap. They opened bars and galleries. The neighborhood became a counterweight to the formal Milan of fashion and finance — looser, scruffier, more nighttime-oriented.

What to do here. The simplest visit is just to walk along one of the canal embankments — Alzaia Naviglio Grande on the south side or Ripa di Porta Ticinese on the north side. The architecture is a mix of restored nineteenth-century terraced houses and converted industrial buildings. Many of the courtyards behind the street frontages are accessible and worth a peek through their gates — most contain small artists' workshops, vintage shops, or design studios.

The bars start around six or seven in the evening with the aperitivo tradition — order a drink and you're typically given an extensive buffet of small bites for free. The Navigli is one of the best places in Milan to do this. The bars get progressively busier through the evening. By eleven, the streets along the canal are packed with mostly young locals.

On the last Sunday of each month, the Mercatone dell'Antiquariato — a large antiques and flea market — sets up along the Naviglio Grande. Hundreds of stalls of furniture, books, vintage clothing, jewelry. It's worth scheduling around if your timing works.

The metro stop Porta Genova is the main access point. From the Duomo, it's about a fifteen-minute tram ride or a forty-minute walk through the southern part of central Milan.
