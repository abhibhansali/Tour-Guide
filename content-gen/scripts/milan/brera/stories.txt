A few stories about the works and the people behind them.

The Mantegna and his obsession. Andrea Mantegna painted the Dead Christ probably in the fourteen seventies, and he kept it in his house until he died in fifteen-oh-six. He never sold it. He never showed it publicly. It was found among his personal effects, painted on a relatively modest canvas, signed quietly. The painting was an exercise in extreme foreshortening that almost no Renaissance painter would have attempted to display — too radical, too uncomfortable. Mantegna seems to have painted it for himself, as a study of his own technical mastery and of his own preoccupation with death. The painting reached the Brera through Napoleonic confiscation more than three hundred years after Mantegna painted it.

The Hayez Kiss and the secret patriot. Francesco Hayez was a Venetian painter who moved to Milan and became the dean of Italian Romantic painting in the eighteen forties and fifties. The Kiss was painted in eighteen fifty-nine, the year Italian unification began. Hayez had been quietly an Italian nationalist for decades. The Italian state was being assembled out of Austrian-occupied territories. Hayez could not openly celebrate the cause, so he encoded it: the colors of the figures' clothing match the Italian flag. The medieval setting evokes earlier moments of Italian resistance to foreign rule. The composition's tenderness undercuts any reading of the work as merely political. It was instantly recognized by his contemporaries for what it was. The painting became iconic of the new Italy. It still is.

The Brera Madonna and the egg. Piero della Francesca's painting has been argued over for centuries because of the suspended ostrich egg. Why is it there? Vasari, writing decades later, didn't know. Modern art historians have proposed: the egg as symbol of the virgin birth (popular in medieval iconography); the egg as a symbol of Federico da Montefeltro's recently-born son (the duke is kneeling in the painting and his wife had just died in childbirth); the egg as a perfect geometric form Piero was preoccupied with as a mathematician. He wrote treatises on perspective and was, by Renaissance standards, more of a mathematician than a painter. The egg may simply be Piero's signature interest in pure geometric shape, placed in the painting because it is beautiful. We don't know.

The Napoleonic confiscation question. Should the Brera return its forcibly-collected works to their original churches? This argument flares up periodically. The Italian state's position has been consistent for two centuries: no. The reasoning is that many of those churches no longer exist, others have changed configuration, and the collection's value as a unified whole exceeds the sum of its parts. The argument is contested but stable. The fact remains that you are looking at art that was, in most cases, never meant to be in a gallery and never meant to be in Milan.

The Brera Academy connection. Across the courtyard from the gallery, the Accademia di Belle Arti has been training Italian artists since seventeen seventy-six. Modigliani studied here. Boccioni studied here. Boldini studied here. The whole arrangement — academy, gallery, library, observatory — was the kind of integrated cultural project Napoleon believed in, and Milan has kept it going.
