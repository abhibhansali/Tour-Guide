The Brera as a cultural institution predates the gallery. The palace was built between fifteen ninety-one and the late sixteen hundreds for the Jesuit order. Designed by Francesco Maria Ricchini, a major Lombard architect of the period, it was originally a Jesuit college — one of the most important Catholic teaching institutions in Northern Italy.

The Jesuits ran the building for a century and a half. They added an observatory in seventeen sixty-four, which is still active and is one of the oldest continuously-operating astronomical observatories in Italy. They also began a small library and a teaching collection of art reproductions.

In seventeen seventy-three, the Pope dissolved the Jesuit order. The Brera went to the Austrian Habsburgs, who at the time ruled Milan. The Empress Maria Theresa converted the Jesuit college into an academy of fine arts in seventeen seventy-six — the institution that today is called the Accademia di Belle Arti di Brera, still active in the same building. Art students were expected to study from a working collection of original masters. That collection became the seed of the museum.

The transformation into a public art gallery was Napoleon's doing. After the French took Milan in seventeen ninety-six, Napoleon's administrators reorganized the academy and dramatically expanded its collection. They sent out commissioners across the former Republic of Venice, the Duchy of Milan, the Romagna, and elsewhere, instructed to take the best paintings from churches and monasteries — Italian works, mostly, but also early Christian icons, Flemish panels, and Byzantine pieces — and ship them to Milan. The official rationale was preservation and public education. The actual effect was a vast forced consolidation.

Many of the most important paintings in the gallery today arrived in this way. Raphael's Marriage of the Virgin came from a small church in Città di Castello. Mantegna's Dead Christ came from the painter's own house. The Piero della Francesca Brera Madonna came from the Ducal Palace at Urbino. The Bellini Pietà came from a Venetian church. The transfers were one-way and have not been reversed.

In eighteen-oh-nine, on Napoleon's birthday, the Pinacoteca opened to the public — Italy's first state-organized public art museum, three years before the Louvre formalized its own statutes. It has been growing ever since. The most recent major addition was a wing for nineteenth and twentieth-century art opened in twenty-eighteen.

The Italian state, after Napoleon's fall, kept the collection where it was. The argument was practical: removing it would have devastated Milan, and reassembling the original parish churches' altars would have been impossible. So the Brera remains a Napoleonic settlement that everyone has long stopped contesting.
