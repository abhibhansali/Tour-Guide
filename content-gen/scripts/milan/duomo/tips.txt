Entry to the cathedral is paid — about five euros for the interior, more for combined tickets with roof and crypt. Book online to skip the long ticket line.
The roof terraces are the highlight and easily worth the extra euros. Choose elevator versus stairs at booking; the elevator route is just as scenic and saves your knees.
Strict dress code: shoulders covered, knees covered, hats off. They will turn you away. The cathedral sells disposable shawls if you forget.
First entry of the day, around eight, is dramatically quieter than midday.
The piazza is heavily patrolled but also heavily preyed on. Friendship-bracelet hustlers approach tourists aggressively, then demand payment. Don't engage; walk past firmly. Pigeon-feed hustlers do the same trick. Pickpockets work the crowd around the entrance.
The cathedral is a working church; mass is celebrated several times daily and parts of the building close to tourists during services.
The crypt is a separate ticket but well worth it for Saint Charles Borromeo's tomb.
There's a cathedral museum on the south side of the piazza — usually under-visited and full of original sculptures, drawings, and architectural fragments that have been replaced on the building.
The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele is directly off the piazza on the north side. Go straight through it after the cathedral; it's the next attraction.
For a great quick coffee or panino, walk one or two blocks off the piazza in any direction. The cafes on the piazza itself are tourist-priced.
