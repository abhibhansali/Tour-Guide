Milan in thirteen eighty-six was ruled by a Visconti duke named Gian Galeazzo, an ambitious man with ambitions to match. He wanted a cathedral that would make a political statement: Milan as the new capital of northern Italy, the equal of Rome or Florence or any city north of the Alps. He chose the unusual decision to build in the French Gothic style — pointed arches, flying buttresses, vertical thrust — instead of the more conservative Italian Romanesque or Renaissance forms that other Italian cities were using.

That choice would set up problems that would take centuries to solve. French Gothic depends on specific structural techniques that Italian masons hadn't fully mastered. Northern European specialists had to be brought in, and they argued with the local craftsmen for decades. The cathedral's design was rewritten multiple times in its first century. Each generation of architects modified what their predecessors had begun.

Construction proceeded fitfully through the fourteen hundreds and fifteen hundreds. The cathedral was used for worship even while it was being built — there are records of bishops conducting mass in a partially roofed nave. When Charles the Fifth, the Holy Roman Emperor, was crowned King of Italy here in fifteen forty-one, the building was still unfinished.

A major figure in the cathedral's history is Carlo Borromeo, the archbishop of Milan from fifteen sixty-four to fifteen eighty-four. He pushed construction forward aggressively, reorganized the interior to match Counter-Reformation principles, and personally tended plague victims during the epidemic of fifteen seventy-six. He was canonized by the church in sixteen ten. His body is preserved in a crystal coffin in the crypt below the cathedral, and you can visit it.

Construction continued for the next two centuries, on and off, depending on money. When Napoleon took Milan in seventeen ninety-six, he made a deal with the cathedral foundation: he would fund the completion of the façade if he could be crowned inside. The bargain held. Napoleon was crowned King of Italy in the cathedral on May twenty-sixth, eighteen-oh-five, wearing the Iron Crown of Lombardy. The façade was rushed to completion for the event, but not actually finished. Final details on the front were still being added in the nineteen hundreds.

The very last sculptural detail — the last empty pedestal on the cathedral's exterior — was filled in nineteen sixty-five. The Italian government held a small ceremony. After almost six centuries, the project was officially declared complete.

Almost. Even now, restoration work is constant. Every facade panel, every spire, every statue needs to be cleaned, repaired, or replaced on a cycle. The Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo — the cathedral foundation, which has run the construction since thirteen eighty-six — still operates a permanent workshop. Stonemasons still come down from Candoglia. The job has not, in any meaningful sense, ever stopped.
