Walk inside and pause near the entrance. The first thing to absorb is the verticality. Italian churches, especially Renaissance ones, tend to be wide and low. Milan Cathedral is tall and narrow. Five aisles run forward to the altar. The central nave rises one hundred and fifty feet — almost as high as a fifteen-story building — and is held up by fifty-two massive marble columns. The number isn't accidental. Each column corresponds to a week of the year. Originally, each was topped with a niche containing a saint who'd be celebrated that week.

The stained glass windows are among the largest in the world. Some of them are seven hundred years old; some are nineteenth-century replacements. The windows along the right wall depict scenes from the Old Testament; those along the left depict the New. The three giant windows above the altar are mostly Counter-Reformation work, finished in the late fifteen hundreds. They're worth slowing down for. The colored light they cast across the floor is the building's main piece of natural lighting.

Look down. The cathedral floor is a vast pattern of pink, white, and black marble, laid in geometric designs across the entire interior. Look for the brass line embedded in the floor near the entrance — a meridian, installed by astronomers from the Brera Observatory in seventeen eighty-six. A small hole in the south wall lets a beam of sunlight through. The beam crosses the meridian at exactly local solar noon. Astronomers used the instrument to calibrate Milanese clocks before standard time existed. The system still works, though almost no one notices it.

Now find the way up to the roof. The cathedral roof is walkable. You climb either by stairs or by elevator, and once you're up there you can stroll along the network of terraces between the spires. From the roof, you're at eye level with hundreds of sculptures most visitors never see. There are saints, prophets, gargoyles, animals, half-human figures, allegorical scenes — three thousand five hundred and forty separate sculptures by some counts, made across six centuries. Many are signed by the workshops that produced them. Some have weather damage that makes them more affecting; others have been recently restored and look new.

From the highest terrace, look up at the Madonnina. The original statue, gilded in seventeen seventy-four, was so well-loved that Milanese still sing about her in folk songs — "Madunina del Domm." When you reach the top, you'll see her right above you.

Back inside, find the bronze doors. There are five of them at the front. The central door, by Ludovico Pogliaghi, depicts the life of Mary in dense bronze relief, finished in nineteen-oh-six. The doors on either side were added through the twentieth century. They're worth a few minutes' study before you go in. The detail is extraordinary.

Don't miss the crypt, accessible from the right transept. It contains the relics of Saint Charles Borromeo and several other Milan archbishops. The chapel is small, quiet, and a startling contrast to the vastness of the cathedral above.
