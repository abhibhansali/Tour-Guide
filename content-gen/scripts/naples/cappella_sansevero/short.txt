You're walking into the Sansevero Chapel — Cappella Sansevero — and what looks like a small private family chapel turns out to be one of the strangest, most arresting interior spaces in Italy. The chapel is the family burial site of the di Sangro princes of Sansevero. It contains arguably the most extraordinary marble sculpture in the country — Giuseppe Sanmartino's Veiled Christ — plus the most genuinely unsettling artifacts in any European church.

Some context. The chapel was built in the late fifteen hundreds and turned into a comprehensive family monument by Raimondo di Sangro, the seventh prince of Sansevero, between seventeen forty-four and his death in seventeen seventy-one. Raimondo was an unusual aristocrat — a scientist, alchemist, occultist, freemason, and engineer who was also a deeply religious Catholic. He spent his fortune on the chapel as a kind of personal statement of his theology and his scientific interests. He commissioned ten major sculptures, four of which represent his ancestors' virtues, plus the central Veiled Christ. He also commissioned the two so-called "anatomical machines" in the crypt below — and we'll get to those.

The Veiled Christ. Giuseppe Sanmartino carved it in seventeen fifty-three, when he was twenty-three years old. It depicts the dead Christ recumbent on a low marble bier, covered with a thin shroud. The shroud is the technical center of the work. It looks transparent — you can see the wounds in Christ's hands and feet through it, you can see his facial features. The "veil" is, of course, also marble. The whole sculpture is carved from a single block. The illusion that the body is covered with translucent fabric is one of the most impressive technical achievements of any single sculpture in Italy.

Sanmartino did this by carving the underlying body and the overlying "fabric" in different degrees of polish. Where the marble represents flesh, it's smoothed to a glossy finish. Where it represents shrouded body, the carving is matte. The difference in surface texture creates the illusion of transparency. The work has fooled visitors for two hundred and seventy years. Eighteenth-century travelers were so convinced the veil was real that they wrote about Sanmartino as if he were a magician.

The other sculptures in the chapel — the Allegory of Modesty, the Allegory of Disillusion, the Education of the Prince, the others — are each tributes to a particular virtue of one of Raimondo's ancestors. They are technically remarkable in different ways. The Allegory of Disillusion, by Francesco Queirolo, shows a man struggling to free himself from a net — and the net itself is also marble, with fine mesh details carved across the figure's body. Queirolo took seven years on it. He damaged his own eyes in the process and never sculpted again.

The two anatomical machines, in the crypt below the main chapel. Two preserved human skeletons, an adult man and an adult woman, with their entire circulatory systems intact — veins, arteries, capillaries — reconstructed in some kind of wax-and-metal medium that has never been fully identified. The machines were commissioned by Raimondo di Sangro from a doctor named Giuseppe Salerno around seventeen sixty. They were originally part of an attempt to demonstrate the human circulatory system for educational purposes. Some contemporary sources suggest they may have been built by injecting the actual blood vessels of corpses with a metal compound and then dissolving the surrounding tissue. The science is not entirely understood. The machines are gruesome and fascinating in equal measure.

Plan an hour. The chapel is small but the works deserve time. Photography is not allowed inside. Modest dress is expected.

Entry is about ten euros and must be booked online with timed entry slots.
