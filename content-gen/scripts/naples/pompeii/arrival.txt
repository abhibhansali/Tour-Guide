You're entering one of the strangest places in the world. Pompeii is not a ruin in the usual sense. Most ancient cities decayed slowly — buildings collapsed, were rebuilt, were quarried for stone, were buried under newer buildings. The remains, when archaeologists find them, are usually fragments of foundations and the occasional pillar.

Pompeii is different. Pompeii was a complete, functioning Roman city — somewhere between eleven and twenty thousand people, a forum, markets, theaters, brothels, bakeries, gardens, gymnasiums, an amphitheater, frescoed houses with running water — and then in a single afternoon, in the summer or autumn of seventy-nine A.D., it was buried under six meters of volcanic ash. The ash sealed everything. The roofs collapsed under the weight, but the walls stood. The objects inside the houses stayed where they were. The bodies of those who didn't escape stayed where they fell.

When excavators began digging the city out, starting in seventeen forty-eight, they were uncovering not ruins but a paused city. Bread was still in the ovens. Wine was in the jars. The graffiti was still on the walls. Election posters from the year seventy-nine. Lists of slaves. Pornographic poetry written on the walls of brothels. Daily life, freeze-framed.

You're entering through one of three main gates. The site covers roughly one hundred and sixty acres — about two-thirds the size of Central Park in New York. Only about two-thirds of the city has been excavated; the rest is still under ash and grass. The Italian government has, in recent decades, decided to leave significant portions buried because excavation accelerates decay. The Pompeii you'll walk through is the most-restored portion. The rest waits.

Almost everything you'll see is original Roman work, or precisely-restored. The frescoes on the walls are mostly the original paint. The mosaic floors are the original tessera. The streets — paved with large basalt blocks with deep ruts from cart wheels — are the original streets the Pompeiians walked. You can stand on the same stones, look at the same paint, walk into the same houses.

A note about pace. The temptation is to rush from one famous building to the next. Don't. The pleasure of Pompeii is the texture — graffiti, shop signs, ovens, fountains, mosaics — and you'll miss it if you walk too fast. Wear comfortable shoes. Bring water. Plan for at least three hours, ideally a full day.

The first thing to absorb is the silence. A city built for noise — markets, traffic, voices — is now profoundly quiet. The sound of the cicadas is sometimes the only thing you hear. The Roman empire ended a long time ago. This is what one piece of its absence sounds like.
