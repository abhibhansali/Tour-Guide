Pompeii is full of small, vivid stories — the kind that survive only when a city gets paused mid-sentence.

The graffiti. Pompeii has about ten thousand surviving inscriptions written on its walls. Some are political — endorsements for upcoming city elections, posted by trade guilds. Some are commercial — advertisements for gladiator fights, with names and weight classes. Many are personal. There are love declarations, scrawled by teenagers in chalk. There are insults aimed at specific named individuals. There are obscene jokes. There are quotations from Virgil and Ovid, copied out by schoolchildren practicing handwriting. One famous example is a complaint, scratched into a wall in the basilica, that reads roughly: "I am amazed, wall, that you have not collapsed under the weight of all this stupid writing on you." Roman irony, surviving on a Roman wall, for two thousand years.

The graffiti in a small bedroom of the brothel — the Lupanar, on the main commercial street — is especially blunt. The names of the prostitutes and their prices are written on the walls beside the doorways. The customers wrote reviews. One entry: "Here I conquered a thousand women, with no help." Another: "Hermeros loves Phileros." The frank, casual sexuality of Roman daily life is preserved on these walls as nowhere else.

The bakeries. Pompeii had thirty-three identifiable bakeries. Many of them had ovens still loaded with bread the morning of the eruption. The loaves are now in the National Archaeological Museum in Naples — about eighty-one carbonized loaves, recognizable as bread, some still bearing the bakers' stamps that marked which household had ordered them. The Romans had two main bread shapes: round flat loaves scored into eight wedges, and oval rolls. Both forms are in the museum.

The Villa of the Mysteries, just outside the city walls. This was a wealthy suburban estate with one of the most spectacular surviving fresco cycles in the ancient world — a series of larger-than-life painted figures performing what appears to be an initiation rite into the cult of Dionysus, the wine god. The figures are still vivid, the colors still saturated. We don't entirely understand what the ritual was. We know only that wealthy Roman women, in private rooms in their country houses, undertook some kind of secret religious experience involving wine, dance, and a flagellation scene. The Villa of the Mysteries is a fifteen-minute walk from the main site. It's almost always less crowded than the central city. It is one of the most haunting rooms you'll see anywhere.

And the dogs. There's one specific cast — a plaster cast of a chained dog, head thrown back, twisted in its death throes — that has become one of the iconic images of Pompeii. It was a guard dog at a house called the House of Orpheus, chained near the front door. When the ash came, no one freed it. You can see the cast in a glass case near where it was found. It's still wearing its collar.
