Allow at least four hours. Six is more realistic if you want to see major sites without rushing. A full day is what the site deserves.
Wear shoes with grip. The original Roman paving is uneven, slippery when wet, and many surfaces are bare basalt or rough stone. Sandals are a bad idea.
There is essentially no shade. In summer, do this early morning or late afternoon. The site closes around seven in summer.
Bring water — at least a liter per person, more in summer. There are a few drinking fountains scattered through the site, identifiable by their continuous flow.
A printed map or downloaded offline map is essential. The signage is improving but still inconsistent. Many of the most interesting houses are unmarked.
Buy your ticket online to skip the line. Combined tickets are sold for Pompeii alone or for Pompeii plus Herculaneum, Oplontis, and other smaller sites. The combined ticket is excellent value if you have a second day.
Many houses are locked behind gates on a rotating schedule — only a fraction are open on any given day. Don't be disappointed; come back another day to see different ones if you can.
The National Archaeological Museum in Naples is essential context for any Pompeii visit. The best frescoes, mosaics, and artifacts from the site are there. Visit it after your Pompeii trip, while everything is fresh.
The site can be reached by train from Naples in under an hour. The Circumvesuviana railway has a stop called Pompei Scavi-Villa dei Misteri — that's the one you want, not the modern town's stop.
Eating options inside the site are limited and overpriced. Pack a small lunch, or plan to eat in the modern town after.
Pickpockets work the train into Pompeii. Keep valuables close.
