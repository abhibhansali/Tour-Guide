You're looking at, or standing on, one of the most famous and most dangerous mountains in the world. Mount Vesuvius is a stratovolcano on the eastern edge of the Bay of Naples, just over four thousand feet high, currently quiet, currently being watched more closely than any volcano on Earth. About six hundred thousand people live in its immediate red zone — the area that would be devastated by a major eruption — and the population is still growing.

The mountain you see today is actually the smaller, younger cone inside the much larger remains of an older volcano called Monte Somma. Monte Somma was the original mountain. In seventy-nine A.D., the eruption that destroyed Pompeii blew the top off it. The current cone — the one with the smoking crater on top — grew up inside the broken-off caldera over the subsequent two thousand years. You can see the original Somma ridge on the north side; it's the half-circle of older rock that nearly encloses the modern cone.

Vesuvius has erupted dozens of times since the year seventy-nine. The two biggest were in fifteen-oh-five and sixteen thirty-one — the sixteen thirty-one event killed about three thousand people in the surrounding villages. There were major eruptions in seventeen-oh-seven, in seventeen seventy-nine, in eighteen seventy-two, and most recently in March nineteen forty-four, during the last weeks of the Allied occupation of Naples. The forty-four eruption destroyed several towns and damaged a squadron of American B-Twenty-Five bombers parked at a nearby airfield. There are photographs of GIs watching lava flow toward their tents.

Since nineteen forty-four, the volcano has been quiet. Dormant, in volcanological terms, but not extinct — actively monitored, with seismometers, gas sensors, and ground-deformation instruments throughout the surrounding mountains. The Italian Civil Protection runs a permanent observatory on a nearby hill, the Osservatorio Vesuviano, founded in eighteen forty-one. It's one of the oldest volcano observatories in the world.

Visiting the volcano is straightforward. From Naples or Pompeii, you can take a bus or a tour up the mountain road. The road ends at a parking lot about a thousand feet below the summit. From there, a footpath climbs the rest of the way — twenty to thirty minutes of walking, depending on your pace, on a wide gravel track that switches back up the cone. The slope is moderate but the surface is loose volcanic gravel; you'll feel like you're walking through dry beach sand at times. Wear shoes with grip.

At the top, you walk along a path around the rim of the crater. The crater is about a quarter-mile across and several hundred feet deep. You can usually see steam coming up from fumaroles — vents in the floor of the crater where geothermal gas escapes. The smell of sulfur is unmistakable. On clear days, the view from the rim is one of the most stunning panoramas in Italy: Naples below you, Sorrento to the south, Capri across the bay, the island of Ischia to the west, and on a really clear day, the long curve of the Amalfi coast.

A few practical notes. The summit can be twenty degrees cooler than the bottom of the mountain. Bring a layer. There is essentially no shade at the rim. Visits are weather-dependent — the park closes the trail in high winds or storm conditions. Tickets are timed-entry and can be booked online; same-day walk-ups are usually possible but may involve waiting.

A small bar at the parking lot sells coffee, water, and souvenirs at reasonable prices. There are bathrooms.

Stand at the rim and look down into the crater. The volcano is sleeping. It has been sleeping for eighty years. It will not sleep forever.
