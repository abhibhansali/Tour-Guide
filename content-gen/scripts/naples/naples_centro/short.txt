You're walking through the historic center of Naples — Spaccanapoli, "the splitter of Naples." The name refers to a single long straight street that cuts through the medieval old town in a line so geometrically perfect that it visibly divides the surrounding maze of crooked alleys into two halves. The street itself isn't called Spaccanapoli on any official sign. It's actually three contiguous streets — Via Pasquale Scura, Via Benedetto Croce, and Via San Biagio dei Librai — joined end to end. The whole length runs for about a mile and a half through the densest part of old Naples.

The reason for the perfect straight line is that this is one of the ancient streets of the Greek and Roman city. Naples was founded by Greek colonists from the island of Rhodes around six hundred B.C., and they laid the city out on a grid. The Romans, when they took over, kept the grid. The streets that survived from the Greek era are still recognizable today by their dead-straight runs through the otherwise tangled medieval city. Spaccanapoli is the most famous of these. The street you're walking on is, in essence, the same street a Greek colonist walked twenty-five centuries ago.

The old town around it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The buildings on either side are six, seven, eight stories tall, packed wall to wall, with laundry hanging from every balcony and motorbikes wedged into every alcove. There is no pretense of museum-piece preservation here. This is a living working-class neighborhood, dense and loud and chaotic in a way that no other major Italian historic center quite manages. Naples has resisted gentrification in ways that Florence and Rome have not. The result is more alive and more uncomfortable.

A few specific stops to make along Spaccanapoli.

San Gregorio Armeno — the side street known as the "via dei presepi," the street of nativity scenes. For centuries, the Neapolitan craftsmen on this street have specialized in making elaborate ceramic figures for nativity scenes — not just the holy family but entire miniature villages, with butchers and bakers and shoemakers, plus increasingly satirical modern characters: Italian politicians, soccer stars, popes, all rendered as tiny clay figures. The shops are open year-round, and the craft has been an unbroken tradition since the seventeen hundreds.

The Sansevero Chapel, on a side street north of Spaccanapoli. A small private chapel of an aristocratic family, the di Sangro, containing what may be the most extraordinary marble sculpture in Italy: Giuseppe Sanmartino's Veiled Christ, finished in seventeen fifty-three. It depicts a recumbent dead Christ covered with a marble veil so thin and translucent that you can see the wounds and features through it. The illusion is uncanny. People used to believe Sanmartino had somehow chemically dissolved real cloth onto the marble. He hadn't; it's all stone. The chapel also contains the strange "anatomical machines" of Prince Raimondo di Sangro — two human skeletons with their entire circulatory systems preserved as wax-and-wire models, made under mysterious circumstances in the eighteenth century. The chapel charges admission. It is worth every cent.

Pizza. The Margherita pizza — tomato, mozzarella, basil, the three colors of the Italian flag — was invented in Naples, in eighteen eighty-nine, for Queen Margherita of Savoy by a pizzaiolo named Raffaele Esposito. The original pizzeria, Pizzeria Brandi, still operates a few blocks west of Spaccanapoli on Via Chiaia. There are arguably better pizzerias in modern Naples — Da Michele, on Via Cesare Sersale, has a longer queue and a more cult following — but Brandi is the historic one. Either is a religious experience.

Naples is loud. It's chaotic. It will pickpocket you if you're not careful. It will also serve you the best coffee, the best pizza, and the best mozzarella di bufala you have ever tasted. Walk slowly, watch your bag, eat constantly, and don't try to make it make sense.
