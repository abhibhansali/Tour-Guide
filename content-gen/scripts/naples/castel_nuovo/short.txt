You're walking up to Castel Nuovo — the "New Castle" — a massive medieval fortress on the Naples waterfront, built in the twelve seventies and rebuilt in the fourteen forties. Despite the name, this castle is now eight hundred years old. It got the name "new" relative to two older Norman castles in the city — the Castel dell'Ovo on the bay and the Castel Capuano in the old center. Compared to those, this was the new one. The name stuck.

The castle was built by Charles the First of Anjou, who became king of Naples in twelve sixty-six after defeating the previous Hohenstaufen ruler. Charles wanted a defensible royal residence near the harbor, and the castle was designed by the French architect Pierre de Chaule between twelve seventy-nine and twelve eighty-two. The original design was a square plan with four cylindrical corner towers, surrounded by a moat. The royal apartments were inside.

The castle's most famous feature is the entrance arch — the Arco di Trionfo, designed in the fourteen forties by various sculptors including Francesco Laurana, Pietro di Martino, and Domenico Gagini. The arch was commissioned by Alfonso the First of Aragon, who had conquered Naples in fourteen forty-two and wanted to celebrate his victory. The marble arch is set into the entrance between two of the castle's original towers, and the contrast — Renaissance white marble framed by medieval grey stone towers — is one of the most distinctive sights in Naples. The arch depicts Alfonso's triumphal entry into the city in great detail, with horses, soldiers, retainers, and the king himself in procession.

The castle has had a checkered history. After Alfonso, it served various Aragonese, Spanish, and Austrian Habsburg rulers across three centuries. The Bourbon kings, after seventeen thirty-four, mostly used it for administration rather than royal residence — they preferred newer palaces nearby. By the eighteen hundreds, the castle was largely a military barracks. In the early twentieth century, the Italian state began converting it back to a public monument. It now houses several museums and is used for occasional cultural events.

Inside, the standout space is the Sala dei Baroni — the Hall of the Barons. A massive vaulted reception hall, roughly twenty-five meters square, with a complex stellated ribbed ceiling that converges at a central oculus. The room is named for a particularly grim moment in fourteen eighty-six: King Ferdinand the First of Aragon invited a group of conspiring barons to the room for a celebration and then had them all arrested. Many were executed. The story is one of the legendary acts of Aragonese-era political violence in Naples.

The Cappella Palatina, on the ground floor, was the medieval royal chapel. It contains a small but important early-Renaissance fresco cycle by Maso di Banco and others, depicting biblical scenes. Most of the frescoes were damaged over the centuries; what survives is fragmentary but evocative.

The castle's main floor museums include a collection of paintings — mostly fourteenth to nineteenth-century Neapolitan works — and a small section of medieval and Renaissance sculpture.

Plan one to two hours. Entry is about six euros. The exterior view, with the white marble triumphal arch set in dark medieval stone, is one of the most photographed sights in Naples and worth seeing even if you don't go inside.

The castle is right on the Naples harbor — you can walk from here to the ferry terminal in ten minutes if you're catching a boat to Capri or Sorrento, or to the Royal Palace next door in five minutes.
