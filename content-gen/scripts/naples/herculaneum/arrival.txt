You're walking into Herculaneum — Pompeii's smaller, better-preserved sister city. Both were destroyed by the same eruption in seventy-nine A.D. But Herculaneum is, in some specific ways, more useful as a window into Roman life than Pompeii is. The volcanic conditions here preserved things that decomposed elsewhere.

Some context. Herculaneum was a wealthier, smaller seaside town — about four thousand inhabitants compared to Pompeii's eleven thousand or more. The geography was different too. Pompeii sat ten miles east of the volcano, on a slope; the eruption rained ash and pumice on it for about twelve hours before the deadly phase. Herculaneum sat just four miles southwest of Vesuvius, on the coast; it was hit by something different. The pyroclastic surge — a wave of superheated gas and ash moving at hurricane speed — rolled over Herculaneum first, in a single instant. Temperatures reached approximately five hundred degrees Celsius. The town was vaporized, then encased in flowing mud that hardened into volcanic rock.

The encasement was a paradox. The instantaneous heat killed everyone outright. But the lack of oxygen in the mud preserved organic materials that would normally rot — wooden beams, doors, furniture, bread, papyrus scrolls, even rope and cloth. Pompeii's slow rain of pumice burned and dehydrated organic material; Herculaneum's mud sealed it.

So when you walk through Herculaneum today, you see wooden balconies still standing. You see wooden beds still in place. You see carbonized but recognizable bread on shop counters. You see fishing nets, ropes, and wood-paneled rooms with intact furniture. None of this survives anywhere else.

The town is much smaller than Pompeii. The excavated area is about eleven hectares — about a third the size of excavated Pompeii — and most of it is densely built. The site can be walked through in two to three hours. The crowds are typically a quarter or less of Pompeii's.

A note about the entry. You arrive at the site through a modern visitor center, then walk down a long ramp to reach the excavation. The ancient ground level was about sixty feet below the modern surface — that's how deep the volcanic rock layered over the town. Walking down the ramp, you're walking back in time and physically below the modern world.

The entrance into the ancient town is through what was once the seafront. You'll see a row of arched chambers, called the boat sheds, that opened onto the beach. In nineteen eighty-two, archaeologists found roughly three hundred human skeletons crowded into these sheds — the people of Herculaneum who had fled to the waterfront hoping for boat rescue, only to be caught by the pyroclastic surge. Many of the bones are still there. They are gradually being studied.
