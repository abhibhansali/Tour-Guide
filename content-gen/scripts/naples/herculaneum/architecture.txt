Herculaneum's preserved architecture gives you a closer look at Roman residential life than Pompeii does, because more of the upper-story construction has survived.

The town's street grid is regular. Two main streets, called the Decumanus Maximus and the Cardo, cross at right angles in the town center. Around them are smaller streets — Cardo III, Cardo IV, Cardo V — running roughly perpendicular to the coast. The streets are paved with the same dark basalt as Pompeii, with stepping stones at intersections and curbs designed to drain water.

The houses are densely packed. Most of them have a standard Roman atrium plan — a small entrance corridor opening into a central reception space with a rectangular roof opening above. But Herculaneum's homes also routinely have upper stories preserved, with wooden floors and stairs, and ceiling beams still in place. This is the unique feature.

Look at the House of the Wooden Partition. It's an early Roman house, with a small atrium and an old-style timber partition wall — a folding wooden screen — preserved from the first century A.D. The partition is essentially intact. You can see the wood, the bronze fittings, the original construction technique. Wooden partitions like this were common in Roman houses but have decomposed everywhere except here.

The House of Neptune and Amphitrite. The standout for mosaic work. The triclinium — the dining room — has a wall fountain decorated with a large mosaic of Neptune and his consort Amphitrite, made in vivid blue and gold tesserae. The mosaic is preserved with its original colors. Look at the floor near the fountain — also mosaic, with marine motifs. This is what a wealthy Roman dining room actually looked like.

The Villa of the Papyri. The largest house at Herculaneum, located outside the main excavated area. The villa is famous for its library of carbonized papyrus scrolls — approximately eighteen hundred Roman scrolls, the only ancient library that has survived intact. The villa was discovered through tunneling in the seventeen forties. The bronzes from it are in the Naples Archaeological Museum. The villa itself has been only partially excavated; most of it remains under modern Ercolano.

The College of the Augustales. A large public building decorated with frescoes depicting Hercules — the town's mythological founder — being received among the gods. The frescoes are extensive and well-preserved. This was a meeting hall for the local priesthood of the imperial cult.

The thermal baths. The Forum Baths and the Suburban Baths are well-preserved. The Suburban Baths, near the harbor, are notable because they have intact wooden doors and seats — making them probably the only Roman bath complex anywhere where you can see exactly how the wooden furnishings looked.

The shop fronts. Many of Herculaneum's streets are lined with shops, each typically with a wide opening onto the street, a counter with built-in jars or amphorae, and a small room behind. You can see the goods that were being sold on the day of the eruption — sometimes carbonized, but recognizable. A fruit shop with a basket of carbonized walnuts. A garum shop — fish sauce — with rows of preserved amphorae.

The boat sheds at the seafront. Arched chambers facing what was once the beach. The site preserves perhaps the most affecting tableau of any Roman ruin: about three hundred people, all dead from the pyroclastic surge, crowded into these sheds. The skeletons are still there. Some are in their final positions, holding each other, sheltering children. The excavators have chosen to leave them. They are studied in place rather than removed.
