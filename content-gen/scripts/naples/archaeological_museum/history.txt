The museum is the result of two converging collecting traditions.

The Farnese collection. The Farnese were a major Italian aristocratic family who, in the early fifteen hundreds, became wealthy through papal connections. Alessandro Farnese became Pope Paul the Third in fifteen thirty-four and used his office to enrich the family. The Farnese began collecting classical sculpture aggressively, mostly through purchases and excavations on family-owned land in Rome. By the sixteen hundreds, the Farnese collection was one of the largest private holdings of ancient sculpture in Europe.

The collection passed through inheritance to Elisabetta Farnese, who married King Philip the Fifth of Spain in seventeen-fourteen. Their son, Charles, became king of Naples in seventeen thirty-four. Charles inherited the Farnese sculpture from his mother and brought it to Naples around seventeen sixty-five. Some of the major Farnese pieces — the Hercules, the Bull, the Atlas, several emperors — became the foundation of the new Bourbon museum in Naples.

The Vesuvian collection. Charles the Third also initiated, in seventeen forty-eight, the systematic excavation of Pompeii and the resumption of work at Herculaneum. The discoveries were sensational. Bronzes from the Villa of the Papyri at Herculaneum came to light first — life-sized bronze sculptures of dancing girls, satyrs, philosophers, emperors. Then frescoes from Pompeiian houses. Then mosaics, ceramics, gold jewelry, household objects. All of it came to Naples, to a single museum building, where the king's archaeologists cataloged and displayed it.

The museum became the central institution of Bourbon-era Neapolitan culture. Foreign visitors — Goethe, Mozart, Stendhal, others — came to Naples specifically to see it. Goethe wrote that the collection had given him a "different idea of antiquity" than he'd had from books. The bronzes from the Villa of the Papyri, in particular, were considered the highest standard of classical art for the next century. Sculptors and painters all over Europe traveled to Naples to study them.

The collection survived multiple political upheavals. When the Bourbons were temporarily ousted by Napoleon's forces in eighteen-oh-six, Joachim Murat became king of Naples and continued the museum's operation. When Bourbons returned in eighteen-fifteen, the collection remained. When Italian unification absorbed Naples in eighteen sixty-one, the new Italian state took ownership but didn't move the collection. The museum has stayed in the same building since seventeen seventy-seven.

In nineteen eighty, an earthquake damaged parts of the museum and forced extensive restoration. The frescoes and mosaics had to be moved temporarily and have been gradually returned. The Secret Cabinet — the room of erotic artifacts that had been hidden from public view since the eighteen sixties — was reopened in two thousand, after long debate.

Recent additions include the contents of the so-called Imperial Cult Building at Herculaneum, excavated in the nineteen ninetes, and updated displays of the Pompeii frescoes with modern lighting and conservation. The museum's reputation has been resurging in the past twenty years.
