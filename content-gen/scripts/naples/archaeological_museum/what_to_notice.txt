The collection is dense. Here are the specific works to find.

The Farnese Hercules, on the ground floor. A ten-foot-tall Roman copy of a Greek bronze original by the sculptor Lysippos, who worked in the late fourth century B.C. Hercules is depicted resting after his labors — leaning on his club, exhausted, with the apples of the Hesperides in his right hand. The work was the most-admired ancient sculpture of the eighteenth century. Every European nobleman wanted a copy in his garden. The original is here, and you can see the chisel marks on the back where Roman copyists adjusted the figure.

The Farnese Bull, in the same gallery. A vast multi-figure marble group from the early third century A.D. The subject: Zethus and Amphion tying Dirce to the horns of a bull as punishment for tormenting their mother. The sculpture is the largest single piece of sculpture surviving from antiquity — about twenty-three feet tall, carved from a single block of marble. The composition is intricate: figures, animals, draperies, all carved with detail at three different scales. The work was found in the Baths of Caracalla in Rome in the early fifteen hundreds and acquired by the Farnese.

The bronzes from the Villa of the Papyri, on the ground floor. The Drunken Faun, a bronze sculpture of a satyr collapsed against a wineskin, leg crossed, eyes shut. The Resting Hermes, a small bronze of the god seated on a rock, exhausted. The Dancing Faun, bronze, mid-step. All from a single buried Roman villa near Herculaneum. The bronzes survived because they were buried in volcanic mud that excluded oxygen. The patina is unusually well-preserved.

The Alexander Mosaic. On the mezzanine. A floor mosaic depicting Alexander the Great defeating King Darius the Third of Persia at the Battle of Issus in three thirty-three B.C. The mosaic is made of about a million and a half tesserae — individual stones — each smaller than a grain of rice. The composition is dynamic: Alexander on the left, charging on his horse; Darius on the right, retreating in a chariot, his face turned in horror. The mosaic was excavated from the House of the Faun in Pompeii in eighteen thirty-one. The original house also had a small bronze faun — also in this museum.

The Pompeii frescoes, first floor. Entire walls have been lifted, transported, and reassembled here, with their painted decorations intact. The famous Sappho fresco, depicting a young woman with a stylus and writing tablet, possibly representing the Greek poet. The Three Graces fresco. The frescoes from the Villa of Diomedes. Several rooms of red-walled and yellow-walled Pompeiian Fourth Style decorations.

The Beware of the Dog mosaic — Cave Canem — from the entrance of the House of the Tragic Poet in Pompeii. A small black-and-white floor mosaic of a chained dog, with the Latin phrase below. The original is here. Pompeii has only a copy.

The Secret Cabinet, on the mezzanine. A small set of rooms containing the erotic Roman art discovered in Pompeii — phallic objects, sexually explicit frescoes, garden statuettes of Priapus. The Bourbon kings had this material locked away because they considered it inappropriate for public viewing. It was hidden for over a century. Adults are welcome; children may need permission. The content is matter-of-fact, sometimes humorous, never sensational. Romans had a very different attitude toward sex than modern Europeans do. The room is one way to learn that.

The carbonized loaves of bread, in the first-floor galleries. Found in bakery ovens in Pompeii, baked the morning of the eruption, never delivered. Each loaf has the baker's stamp on top. Some bear identifying marks — names of customers, household accounts.
