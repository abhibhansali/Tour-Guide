You're walking into one of the most important archaeological collections in the world — and the essential complement to any visit to Pompeii. The National Archaeological Museum of Naples, the MANN, houses the bulk of the original artifacts excavated from Pompeii, Herculaneum, Oplontis, and the other Vesuvian sites. If you've already been to Pompeii, much of what you saw there was either replicas or buildings without their contents. The contents are here.

The collection is huge. About fifteen thousand works total, across three floors, organized roughly by source. The ground floor has classical sculpture, including the famous Farnese collection — a group of monumental Roman copies of Greek originals inherited from the Farnese family in the eighteenth century. The mezzanine has mosaics from Pompeii. The first floor has frescoes from the Vesuvian sites, organized by house and theme.

This is not a Roman collection. Naples held it for a specific historical reason. When the king of Spain, Charles the Third of Bourbon, became king of Naples in the seventeen thirties, he inherited the Farnese collection from his mother. Charles was also the patron who initiated the systematic excavations of Pompeii and Herculaneum in seventeen forty-eight. As artifacts came out of the volcanic ash, Charles directed them to a building in Naples specifically renovated to display them. The museum opened in seventeen seventy-seven, with an unprecedented combination of classical antiquities and freshly-excavated Roman material. It has been adding to that collection ever since.

The museum is, frankly, much less polished than you'd expect for a collection of this importance. The lighting is uneven. The wall labels are inconsistent. Some rooms feel beautifully curated; others feel half-finished. Climate control is hit-or-miss. Italian funding for archaeological museums has been thin for decades, and the MANN shows it. But the collection is what justifies the visit. Even with poor presentation, this is one of the most concentrated displays of Roman everyday life anywhere.

Plan three hours. Longer if you have stamina. The order I'd recommend: ground floor sculpture first, while you're fresh. Then up to the first floor for the frescoes, and finish at the mezzanine mosaics. The Secret Cabinet — the room of erotic Roman artifacts — is on the mezzanine and is gated; some visitors love it, some find it uncomfortable. We'll talk about it.

A few things you'll see that you can't see anywhere else: the bronze sculptures from the Villa of the Papyri at Herculaneum, including the Drunken Faun and the Resting Hermes; the gigantic Farnese Hercules; the gigantic Farnese Bull, the largest single piece of sculpture surviving from antiquity; the mosaic floor of the Alexander Mosaic from the House of the Faun in Pompeii; the Beware of the Dog mosaic; the carbonized loaves of Pompeiian bread; the gold jewelry from victims of the eruption; and several entire rooms of frescoes lifted from Pompeiian walls.

If you're doing Pompeii on this trip, do this museum afterward, ideally the next day, while the site is still fresh. The pairing makes both make sense.
