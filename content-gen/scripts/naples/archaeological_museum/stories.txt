A few stories from the collection.

The discovery of the Villa of the Papyri. In seventeen fifty, workmen digging a well near the town of Resina, in the buried zone of ancient Herculaneum, broke through volcanic rock into a Roman library. They had stumbled into one of the most important archaeological finds in history. The building, called the Villa of the Papyri because of its vast library of papyrus scrolls, belonged to a wealthy Roman family — possibly that of Lucius Calpurnius Piso, Julius Caesar's father-in-law. The library contained approximately eighteen hundred scrolls. The bronzes — the Drunken Faun, the Dancing Faun, the philosophers — were also found here. The scrolls had been carbonized by the heat of the pyroclastic surge but not destroyed. They were like cigars: round, black, brittle. For three centuries scholars have been trying to read them. In the past five years, using advanced X-ray imaging and AI text recognition, researchers have started actually reading them — the first Roman scrolls anyone has read in two thousand years. The carbonized scrolls are now in the National Library in Naples. The bronzes are here at the MANN.

The Alexander Mosaic and Roman propaganda. The mosaic depicting Alexander defeating Darius is dramatically pro-Alexander. He is the hero; the Persian king is portrayed as cowardly. The mosaic was made for a Pompeiian house in the late second century B.C. — almost two hundred years after the actual battle. The Romans, who had absorbed Alexander's empire after his death, used the image of Alexander defeating Darius as a way of legitimizing their own imperial conquests. The original Greek bronze that inspired the mosaic has been lost. The mosaic is the closest we can get to what it looked like.

The Secret Cabinet and Bourbon prudishness. After the explicit Pompeiian artifacts emerged in the late seventeen hundreds, the Bourbon court was scandalized. Pompeii had been considered a serious archaeological site, not a salacious one. Roman erotic art forced a re-evaluation. King Francis the First of Naples ordered all explicit material removed from public display in eighteen nineteen, locked in a secret room, accessible only to "people of mature age and proven morality" — meaning male aristocrats with personal references. The room stayed locked for almost two centuries. Women, children, and most foreign visitors could not enter. The Secret Cabinet finally reopened in two thousand, with the explicit condition that visitors signed an acknowledgment of the content. The signage policy has since been relaxed.

The Farnese Bull and its sculpture problem. The Farnese Bull is the largest ancient sculpture in existence. It is also a sculpture mostly assembled from fragments. When it was excavated in fifteen forty-six from the Baths of Caracalla, it was broken into hundreds of pieces. Reassembly took decades. The current form is heavily restored. Modern art historians estimate that perhaps sixty percent of what you're looking at is ancient marble; the rest is later restoration. The hands, the lower legs of several figures, parts of the bull's body are sixteenth-century additions. This is true of many famous ancient sculptures — they look more whole than they actually are. The Bull is one of the most extreme examples.
