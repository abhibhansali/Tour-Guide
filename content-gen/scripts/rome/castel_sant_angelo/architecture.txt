The architecture of the castle is layered in a way few buildings are.

Walk in through the main entrance. The first thing you'll see is the long spiral ramp that climbs through the core of the original Hadrian mausoleum. The ramp is in the original Roman brickwork. It runs in a single shallow corkscrew from ground level to the top of the cylinder, about ninety meters of climbing. The width is enough for a small cart. The Romans built it this way because the funeral processions, transporting the imperial ashes up the building, had to travel by chariot or cart. The ramp goes around the cylinder almost twice. The walls beside it are made of opus reticulatum — a Roman concrete-and-stone facing technique with small square stones set diagonally in a grid pattern. The pattern is unchanged from Hadrian's time.

Halfway up the ramp, you'll pass a vestibule that was originally the entrance to the tomb chamber. The tomb itself — where Hadrian's ashes lay — was behind that wall. The chamber was looted in late antiquity. Nothing remains of the original imperial burial except the room's bare walls.

The papal apartments occupy the medieval and Renaissance additions on top of the Roman core. You'll walk through several frescoed chambers — Pope Paul the Third's apartments, finished in the fifteen forties; Pope Clement the Seventh's library; the Sala Paolina, a vast frescoed reception hall — that show the papal court's living conditions when they were holding state in the castle. The frescoes are by minor masters of the late Renaissance, but the quality is high and the rooms are atmospheric.

The most striking interior is the Sala Paolina — Paul the Third's audience hall — with frescoes by Perino del Vaga and Pellegrino Tibaldi from around fifteen forty-five. The walls are painted in elaborate trompe-l'oeil architecture, with classical statues in painted niches, scenes from Greek mythology, and a few historical narratives. The ceiling has gilded stucco. The whole effect is operatic. Paul the Third entertained ambassadors here.

Up another level, you reach the prison cells. They're at the upper interior of the building, deliberately separated from the apartments. Cellini's cell — where he was held in fifteen thirty-eight — is one of them, marked with a plaque. The cells are small, dark, with iron-grated windows.

At the top, the terrace runs around the building's circumference, with the great bronze archangel Michael standing in the center on his pedestal. The view from up here covers most of Rome — Saint Peter's directly across the river, the Pantheon and the Vatican axis, the seven hills of the city laid out behind. On a clear day you can see all the way to the Alban Hills.

The Ponte Sant'Angelo bridge in front of the castle is itself a Bernini-era monument. The original Roman bridge — built by Hadrian — was on this site. The current bridge dates from the late seventeenth century. The ten angel statues along the railings were designed by Bernini, who carved two of them himself; the others were executed by his pupils. Each angel holds a different instrument of the Passion: nails, a crown of thorns, a column, a sponge, a cross.
