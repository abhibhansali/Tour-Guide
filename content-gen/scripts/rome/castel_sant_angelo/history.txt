The name Castel Sant'Angelo — the Castle of the Holy Angel — comes from a specific moment. In the year five ninety A.D., Rome was in the grip of a plague that had been killing thousands per week. Pope Gregory the Great led a procession through the city, praying for the plague to end. As the procession reached the top of the Mausoleum of Hadrian, Gregory reportedly saw a vision: the archangel Michael standing on top of the building, sheathing his sword as a sign that the plague was over. The plague did indeed end shortly after, according to the chronicles. Gregory ordered a statue of Michael placed on the roof of the building, and the name Castel Sant'Angelo stuck. The current statue, in bronze, is from seventeen fifty-three.

For most of the medieval period, the castle served the popes as a defensive position. Rome's old Roman walls were too long to defend with the populations that remained, so concentrated strongholds — fortresses inside the city — became the practical defense. The mausoleum, with its massive walls and elevated position, was a natural choice. By the eleven hundreds, the popes had built a covered passageway — the Passetto di Borgo — connecting the Vatican Palace to Castel Sant'Angelo, allowing the pope to flee from the Vatican to the castle without crossing exposed streets. The passageway is about a thousand feet long, runs along the top of an old Roman wall, and is still standing. Parts of it can sometimes be visited.

The passageway saved Pope Clement the Seventh's life in fifteen-twenty-seven, during the Sack of Rome. The Holy Roman Emperor Charles the Fifth's mercenary army — unpaid, mutinous, mixed German Lutheran and Spanish Catholic — descended on Rome in May of that year. The city was looted for eight days. Tens of thousands of Romans were killed, raped, or driven from their homes. Clement the Seventh, the Medici pope, fled across the Passetto to Castel Sant'Angelo at the very last moment. He survived the siege by hiding in the castle's interior chambers for seven months while the imperial troops gradually withdrew. He paid an enormous ransom to be released. The Sack of Rome was the worst single disaster the papacy had suffered in centuries.

The castle has been the site of multiple notorious imprisonments. The Renaissance goldsmith Benvenuto Cellini was held here in fifteen thirty-eight after being accused of murder. He escaped by lowering himself out a window on a knotted bedsheet, broke his leg in the fall, and recounted the whole episode in his autobiography — one of the great Renaissance memoirs. The astronomer Giordano Bruno spent time in the castle's dungeons in the fifteen nineties before being burned alive in Campo de' Fiori for heresy.

The castle's most famous fictional moment is in Puccini's opera Tosca, premiered in nineteen-hundred. The opera ends with the soprano leaping from the castle's upper rampart, choosing death over surrender to the corrupt police chief Scarpia. The actual rampart from which she leaps is on the top terrace — you can stand there now. Puccini chose the location for its theatricality. Generations of sopranos have ended the opera in this real building, in a real Rome, with a fictional jump.

After Italian unification in eighteen seventy, the Italian state took the castle from the papacy. It served as an army barracks and prison for several decades. In nineteen-oh-one, it became a public museum. It has been continuously open since.
