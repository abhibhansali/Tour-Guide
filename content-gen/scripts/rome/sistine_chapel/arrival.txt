You're entering one of the most concentrated spaces of human creative effort in existence. It's also one of the most crowded. Before you go in, a few things to know.

The Sistine Chapel is, first, a chapel — a working religious space. The pope is elected here. Mass is said here. The Latin Mass and the burning of black or white smoke after each ballot of the papal conclave all happen in this room. You are walking into an active place of worship that just happens to be wallpapered with masterpieces.

The chapel was built between fourteen-seventy-three and fourteen-eighty-one for Pope Sixtus the Fourth, who gave it his name — Sistine comes from Sisto. It replaced an older, more austere chapel called the Capella Magna. The new building was the same size — about a hundred and thirty feet long, forty-four feet wide — but designed to be the great ceremonial space of the papacy.

When the walls were first finished, Sixtus brought in the best painters in Italy — Botticelli, Ghirlandaio, Perugino, Pinturicchio, and Rosselli — and gave them the side walls. The frescoes you'll see at eye level, depicting scenes from the lives of Moses and Christ, were painted in fourteen-eighty-one to fourteen-eighty-three. They are, on their own, an extraordinary collection. In any other room, they'd be the headliner.

But the room has a ceiling. Pope Julius the Second, who succeeded Sixtus, decided in fifteen-oh-eight that the original blue starry ceiling was too modest. He commissioned a thirty-three-year-old sculptor — Michelangelo — to repaint it. Michelangelo had never frescoed a ceiling before, didn't want the job, and tried to refuse. Julius made him take it. Four years later, in fifteen-twelve, he was done.

Then, twenty-three years after that, Pope Paul the Third brought Michelangelo — now sixty years old — back to paint the wall behind the altar. The Last Judgment was finished in fifteen-forty-one. It took five years.

So when you walk in, you're looking at three separate moments of genius stacked on top of each other: the side walls from the fourteen eighties, the ceiling from fifteen-twelve, and the altar wall from fifteen-forty-one. The room covers about sixty years of Renaissance art history all in one place.

The room is silent — or supposed to be. The guards say "silenzio" constantly. Try to honor it. You'll get more from the room if you do.
