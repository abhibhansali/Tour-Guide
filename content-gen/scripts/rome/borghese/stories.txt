A few stories about the works and the people who made them.

Bernini and the impossible Daphne. Bernini was twenty-four when he started carving Apollo and Daphne in sixteen twenty-two. The commission was from Cardinal Scipione, who wanted a sculpture that depicted the climactic moment of Ovid's story — the moment when the nymph Daphne, fleeing the god Apollo, calls on her father to save her and is transformed into a laurel tree. Apollo catches her just as the metamorphosis begins. Daphne's fingers are turning into leaves; her hair is turning into bark; her toes are growing into roots in the ground.

The technical challenge was enormous. Bernini had to carve, in white marble, the texture of human skin, smooth hair, twisting bark, and individual leaves — all in the same piece. He had to depict bodies in mid-motion, captured in stone. The figures are connected at the back, which provides structural support. The leaves are so thin that they ring when tapped. The transition between Daphne's skin and the bark forming on her body is gradual — actual marble, gradually changing texture from polished smoothness to rough tree surface. Bernini took four years on the piece. It was finished in sixteen twenty-five. Scipione had it placed in the room where you'll see it, raised on a low pedestal, designed to be circled.

The Caravaggio David. There's a small Caravaggio painting upstairs called David with the Head of Goliath. It depicts the young David holding a severed head — Goliath's, dripping blood. The face of Goliath in the painting is unmistakably Caravaggio's own self-portrait. Caravaggio painted the work in sixteen-oh-five or six, around the time he killed a man in a duel in Rome and fled the city. The self-portrait as severed head is, by most accounts, an act of contrition — Caravaggio depicting himself as the defeated giant, his own face as the trophy of justice. The painting was sent to Cardinal Scipione, possibly as a request for Vatican intercession with the pope. The pardon was eventually granted, but too late — Caravaggio died on a beach in Tuscany in sixteen-ten, en route back to Rome.

The Canova Pauline. In one of the larger ground-floor rooms, there's a striking statue of a half-nude reclining woman, sculpted by Antonio Canova between eighteen-oh-four and eighteen-oh-eight. The subject is Pauline Bonaparte — Napoleon's sister and at the time the wife of Camillo Borghese. The sculpture depicts her as Venus Victrix, the victorious Venus, lounging on a divan with one hand holding the golden apple of judgment. The work was scandalous in its time. Pauline was a real woman, alive and well-known in Roman society, and she had posed essentially nude for the sculptor. When asked whether she had really posed unclothed for Canova, Pauline reportedly said: "Yes, but the studio was warm." She had a reputation for not pretending. The sculpture is one of the great works of neoclassical sculpture and it's a real portrait of a real Italian socialite.

The Bernini David. Bernini's David, also in the ground floor, is unlike Michelangelo's. Michelangelo's David is in the moment before the throw — calm, contemplating. Bernini's David is in the throw — body torquing, fabric flying, lips compressed, brow furrowed. The face is Bernini's own self-portrait — he sculpted himself, by all accounts because Cardinal Scipione held up a mirror so the young Bernini could see his own facial expression while carving. The sculpture is full of motion. Look at the toes gripping the rock. Look at the muscles in the back.
