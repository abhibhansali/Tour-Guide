You're standing at the bottom of the Spanish Steps — the Scalinata di Trinità dei Monti — one hundred and thirty-five steps connecting Piazza di Spagna at the bottom to the small piazza in front of the church Trinità dei Monti at the top. The whole staircase is a single eighteenth-century architectural composition, deliberately ornamental, deliberately theatrical. It was built between seventeen twenty-three and seventeen twenty-five.

The funding for the steps came from a French diplomat. Étienne Gueffier, who'd lived in Rome and become attached to the city, left a bequest in his will for "the construction of a fitting approach" to the French-owned church on the hill above. The bequest sat unused for decades because the popes and the French embassy couldn't agree on who would design and supervise the project. The deadlock was finally broken in seventeen twenty-three. The architect chosen was Francesco de Sanctis, who designed the steps as a sweeping S-curve cascading down the hillside in five interconnected tiers.

The name comes from the bottom piazza. The Spanish Embassy to the Holy See had its headquarters there since the sixteen hundreds — at the time, the only diplomatic ground in Rome that wasn't directly Roman or papal. Spaniards considered the whole piazza, and by extension the staircase rising from it, as informally Spanish territory. The Italians called the piazza Piazza di Spagna, and the steps inherited the name. Confusingly, the steps themselves are paid for by the French and lead to a French church, but they're called Spanish.

The fountain at the base of the steps — the Fontana della Barcaccia, the "Ugly Boat Fountain" — was designed by Pietro Bernini in sixteen twenty-seven, the father of the more famous Gian Lorenzo Bernini. Pietro had a problem: the water pressure at this spot was very low because of the elevation. He couldn't build a tall fountain like those elsewhere in the city. He solved the problem by designing the fountain as a half-sunken boat, with water gently overflowing the sides. The story is that he was inspired by an actual boat that had been stranded here during a Tiber flood. The fountain still flows from a low-pressure spring. The water is drinkable — Roman public fountains use Acqua Vergine, the same source that feeds the Trevi.

The church at the top — Trinità dei Monti — is owned by the French government. It was originally built in the early fifteen hundreds for King Louis the Twelfth of France. The two distinctive bell towers, the white-and-cream facade, the small Egyptian obelisk in the piazza in front of it — all have been part of the composition since the sixteenth century. The obelisk was brought from the Gardens of Sallust in Rome and re-erected in front of the church in seventeen eighty-nine.

A few cultural notes. Romantic-era poets famously gathered on the steps in the eighteen hundreds. Keats lived in the small house on the right at the base of the steps and died there in eighteen twenty-one, age twenty-five, of tuberculosis. The house is now the Keats-Shelley Memorial House and is open as a museum. It's a quiet, melancholy spot that few tourists visit, despite being three meters from the most-photographed staircase in Rome.

A practical note. Since twenty-nineteen, sitting on the steps is forbidden, and Roman police will fine you about four hundred euros. The policy is enforced more loosely at quiet times but firmly during busy hours. Find a bench in the surrounding streets if you want to rest. The Piazza Mignanelli, just south of the steps, has a column dedicated to the Immaculate Conception — the pope blesses the statue from atop a fire ladder every December eighth.

In two thousand and fifteen, the steps were extensively restored by the luxury brand Bulgari, which paid roughly one and a half million euros for the work. Bulgari's flagship is just down the street on Via Condotti.
