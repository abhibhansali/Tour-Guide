The history of this building is the history of papal ambition and papal compromise, in roughly equal measures.

The first basilica on this site was built by the Emperor Constantine starting around three-twenty A.D., right after Christianity became legal in the Roman Empire. Constantine built it directly over what was already believed to be Peter's grave, leveling part of a Roman cemetery to do it. That first church stood for more than a thousand years. By the fourteen hundreds, it was crumbling — the foundation was sinking, the walls were leaning, the roof was failing.

In fifteen-oh-six, Pope Julius the Second made a radical decision: tear it down and build something entirely new. The architect Donato Bramante began with a vast Greek-cross plan — four equal arms, a single great central dome. Bramante was famously dismissive of the old basilica. His nickname became "Maestro Ruinante" — Master Demolisher. He died in fifteen-fourteen with the church barely begun.

The job passed to Raphael, who died young in fifteen-twenty. Then to Antonio da Sangallo, who died in fifteen-forty-six. By that point construction had been going for forty years with no end in sight. Pope Paul the Third turned to Michelangelo, then seventy-one years old.

Michelangelo accepted on the condition that he would take no salary. He simplified the plan, strengthened the piers, and designed the great dome — a hemisphere with a slightly elongated profile, taller than wide, derived from the Pantheon and from Brunelleschi's dome in Florence. Michelangelo himself never saw it finished. He died in fifteen-sixty-four with the dome only at the base of its drum. Giacomo della Porta finished it from Michelangelo's drawings between fifteen-eighty-eight and fifteen-ninety. It went up in twenty-two months.

But the building wasn't done. In sixteen-oh-six, Pope Paul the Fifth made a controversial decision: he ordered the architect Carlo Maderno to extend the nave forward, turning the original Greek cross into a Latin cross. The longer nave gave more space for processions and crowds, but it pushed the dome back from view. From the front of the church now, you can barely see the dome at all unless you stand far back in the square. That's why Bernini's piazza is so deep — partly to give the dome room to be visible again.

The interior decoration was Bernini's life work. He spent fifty years on it. The bronze baldacchino over the altar, the throne of Saint Peter behind it, the colonnade outside — all Bernini. He died in sixteen-eighty. By then the church was, more or less, finished.
