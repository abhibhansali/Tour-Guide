You're standing in front of the largest church in the world. Whether you find that meaningful or not, the scale of the place is a fact: about fifteen thousand square meters of interior floor, a dome that rises four hundred and forty-eight feet from the ground, a piazza outside designed to hold three hundred thousand people. It's bigger inside than it looks from out here, and it looks plenty big from out here.

You're also standing on what is, in the Catholic understanding, the holiest piece of ground in Christianity. The apostle Peter — the fisherman who led Christ's followers after the crucifixion — is believed to have been killed on this hill, sometime in the seventh decade A.D., during the persecutions under the Emperor Nero. He was, according to tradition, crucified upside down on his own request, in a public arena that stood near where the basilica is now. His followers buried him secretly nearby. Two thousand years later, you can stand directly above his grave.

The square you're in was designed by Gianlorenzo Bernini between sixteen fifty-six and sixteen sixty-seven. The two great curving colonnades, each four columns deep, are intended to feel like welcoming arms reaching out from the basilica. Stand near either of the two round stone markers in the pavement — there's one on each side of the central obelisk — and look at the columns. From those exact spots, the four columns of each colonnade line up so precisely that they look like one. It's a deliberate optical trick.

The obelisk in the middle is older than the church by fifteen hundred years. It was carried from Egypt by the Emperor Caligula in the year forty A.D., to decorate his racetrack on this hill — the very racetrack where Peter was killed. The obelisk witnessed the martyrdom. When the modern square was being designed, the architects kept it.

The basilica itself took a hundred and twenty years to build. Five architects worked on it across the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, each one rebuilding parts of his predecessor's work. The result is somehow coherent. Michelangelo's dome rises behind a façade Michelangelo never designed, over a nave Michelangelo never planned. We'll get into all of that.
