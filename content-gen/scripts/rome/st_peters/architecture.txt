Walk into the central nave and stop for a second. The proportions are deliberately disorienting. The interior is so big that you lose your sense of scale; the cherubs holding the holy water fonts near the entrance look child-sized from a distance, but they're actually six feet tall. There are markings on the floor of the nave showing the lengths of other famous cathedrals — Cologne, Notre Dame, Saint Paul's — for comparison. None of them reaches halfway up Saint Peter's.

The first thing to find is Michelangelo's Pietà — on the right, in the first chapel as you walk in. It's the only major sculpture Michelangelo ever signed; he carved his name on the strap across Mary's chest. He was twenty-four years old when he finished it, in fourteen ninety-nine. The figure of Christ is anatomically precise — Michelangelo had spent years dissecting cadavers — and the proportions of Mary are deliberately altered, her body larger than it would naturally be, so that Christ's adult corpse can rest convincingly across her lap. It's behind bullet-proof glass since nineteen-seventy-two, when a man attacked it with a hammer. He damaged Mary's face. It was repaired, almost invisibly.

Now walk forward to the high altar, under the dome. Bernini's baldacchino rises above it — a bronze canopy ninety-five feet tall, with four twisted columns that imitate the columns from the temple of Solomon as the Romans had described them. The bronze for it came from the porch of the Pantheon, melted down by Pope Urban the Eighth Barberini. That's the line about "What the barbarians did not do, the Barberini did" you may have heard at the Pantheon.

Look down, in front of the altar. There's a sunken oval area called the Confessio, lit by ninety-five oil lamps. Below it, at a deeper level, is the actual tomb of Saint Peter. The papal altar above is positioned so that the pope, when celebrating mass, is standing directly over the apostle's grave.

Now look up into the dome. The mosaic letters around the base, six feet tall, read in Latin: "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church." This is the founding charter, from the Gospel of Matthew, of papal authority. The whole building is essentially an argument for that sentence.

At the far end, behind the altar, is the Cathedra Petri — the throne of Saint Peter — another Bernini work. It's a bronze sculpture of an old wooden chair, supposedly Peter's, surrounded by clouds and angels and lit by an alabaster window depicting the Holy Spirit as a dove. The window is the only natural light in that part of the church, and Bernini designed everything around it to glow.
