You're entering what was, for almost a thousand years, the political and commercial center of the western world. Today it looks like a field of half-standing columns and broken pavement. That can be hard to make sense of without a map. Let me orient you.

This long, low valley between two hills — the Palatine on one side, the Capitoline on the other — was originally a marshy floodplain. The earliest Romans lived up on the hills, and the valley was their cemetery. In the sixth century B.C., the Etruscan kings drained the marsh with a giant covered sewer called the Cloaca Maxima. Once the ground was dry, the Romans paved it and put public life on top of it. The Forum was born.

For roughly a thousand years, this is where Romans did everything that wasn't private. The Senate met here. Trials were held here. Public speeches were given here. Money was changed here. Triumphs ended here. Religious processions started here. Caesar was cremated here. The space evolved continuously — every emperor wanted to add a temple or a monument — until by the late empire it was a dense forest of marble and bronze.

Then Rome fell, and the Forum did too. Over the next thousand years, it sank under layers of mud and rubble until the columns you see today were buried to the shoulders. By the seventeen hundreds, it was a pasture called the Campo Vaccino — the Cow Field. Local farmers grazed cattle among the column tops sticking out of the dirt. The serious excavations didn't begin until the early eighteen hundreds and continued for a hundred and fifty years.

What you're seeing now is layered. Different buildings, different centuries, different rulers, all mixed together. A temple from the time of the early republic stands next to a triumphal arch from the late empire. Some of the columns are five hundred years older than others.

A few orienting points. Looking from the entrance: the Palatine Hill — where the emperors lived — is on your right. The Capitoline Hill — with the modern museums on top — is dead ahead. The Via Sacra, the Sacred Way, is the main paved street running through it all. We'll walk along it. Watch your footing. The basalt cobbles are the same ones that triumphs marched over.
