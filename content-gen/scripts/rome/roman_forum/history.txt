The Forum's history is essentially the history of Rome itself, compressed into a single piece of real estate. Let me give you the broad shape.

The earliest period — the seventh and sixth centuries B.C. — is the time of the kings. Romulus is the founder of legend. The historical kings include Etruscan rulers like Tarquinius, who drained the marsh and built the first major temples. The Temple of Saturn, whose columns you'll see, dates from this era — built around five-oh-one B.C., right at the founding of the Republic. It was the public treasury for the next thousand years.

Then comes the Roman Republic, from five-oh-nine B.C. to the late first century B.C. This is when the Forum becomes the political stage of a self-governing city. The Senate met in a building called the Curia, which you can still see — the brick rectangle near the Arch of Septimius Severus. The Rostra — the speakers' platform — got its name from the bronze rams of captured warships nailed to its front. Cicero spoke from it. So did Mark Antony, when he delivered the eulogy over Caesar's body.

The Republic ended with civil war and the rise of Augustus, the first emperor, around twenty-seven B.C. The imperial period turned the Forum into a stage for emperors. Each one tried to leave a building behind. The Arch of Titus celebrates the conquest of Jerusalem in seventy A.D. The Arch of Septimius Severus celebrates campaigns in Parthia a hundred and twenty years later. The Temple of Antoninus and Faustina, whose tall front you'll notice, became a church in the seventh century, which is why its columns are still standing while everything around it isn't.

The end came gradually. Rome was sacked by the Visigoths in four-ten and the Vandals in four-fifty-five. The empire collapsed in the west by four-seventy-six. The Forum kept partially functioning through the Byzantine period, but by the eighth century it had effectively gone silent.

Then a thousand-year intermission. Earthquakes brought down columns. Floods buried foundations. Medieval builders quarried what was left. The Forum became a swamp again, then a pasture.

Excavations began under the Napoleonic occupation in the early eighteen hundreds and continued through the unification of Italy. They've never really stopped. The Forum you're walking through is still actively being dug.
