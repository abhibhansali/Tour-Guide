Michelangelo's redesign of the Capitoline is one of the great urban architectural compositions of the Renaissance. Let me walk you through it.

Start at the bottom of the Cordonata — the wide stepped ramp leading up to the piazza. Look up. The ramp is shallow enough that you can ride a horse up it. The width is generous, creating a slow approach. The two reclining classical river-god statues at the bottom were placed by Michelangelo to mark the entry. The whole approach is designed to make the piazza appear gradually, as a culminating destination.

Reaching the top, you arrive in the piazza. The first thing to notice is the geometry. The piazza is a trapezoid, not a rectangle. The two flanking palaces — the Palazzo Nuovo on the left, the Palazzo dei Conservatori on the right — are not parallel. They angle outward. The Palazzo Senatorio at the back is wider than the Cordonata at the front. Michelangelo did this deliberately. The trapezoidal shape exaggerates the perspective, making the piazza look larger than it actually is, and drawing the eye toward the Palazzo Senatorio as the focal point.

The pavement is the most distinctive feature. Twelve interlocking diamonds, set within an oval, set within the trapezoid. The pattern is geometrically complex — based on a star with twelve rays — and the design is original to Michelangelo. The pavement was completed only in nineteen forty, four hundred years after Michelangelo's drawings, because earlier popes had thought the pattern looked too pagan. Mussolini, ironically, had it laid as a piece of Roman civic restoration.

The central statue of Marcus Aurelius — emperor from one sixty-one to one eighty A.D. — is a sixteen-foot bronze of the emperor on horseback, his right arm raised in the gesture of a public address. The work is the only fully intact ancient Roman bronze equestrian statue. It survived because, in the Middle Ages, it was misidentified as Constantine the Great — the first Christian emperor — and therefore considered too important to melt down. Most other bronze equestrian statues from antiquity were melted down for their metal. Marcus Aurelius survived through mistaken identity. The figure on top is a copy. The original is inside the Palazzo dei Conservatori.

The three palace facades all show Michelangelo's mature architectural style. The proportions are clear but unconventional: the windows are taller than wide, the entablatures more elaborate than classical rules required, the corners articulated by giant pilasters that span two stories. The whole composition violates strict classical proportions while remaining within recognizable classical vocabulary. This is mature Renaissance architecture moving toward Mannerism.

The Palazzo Senatorio at the back was the seat of medieval Rome's city government and is still Rome's city hall. The clock tower above was added in seventeen eighty-four. The central double staircase, climbing toward the entrance, makes the building feel monumental despite its actual modest size. The two classical river gods at the base of the staircase are repurposed from a former villa.

Inside the museums, the architecture continues. The Palazzo dei Conservatori has a famous courtyard containing fragments of the colossal statue of the Emperor Constantine — pieces of a sixty-foot statue that once stood in the Basilica of Maxentius and Constantine in the Forum. The head, a hand, an arm, and a foot are arranged around the courtyard. They are the surviving fragments of one of the largest ancient sculptures ever made.
