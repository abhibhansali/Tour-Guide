You're walking up to one of the most historically important hills in human civilization. The Capitoline Hill — Campidoglio in Italian — is the smallest of the seven hills of Rome and was, for over a thousand years, the religious and political center of the ancient Roman world. The Capitol in Washington is named after this hill. The word "capital" itself comes from this hill. Every cathedral that calls itself a capitoline is referencing this place. Today, the hill houses Rome's city hall, three of the world's oldest art museums, and an open piazza designed by Michelangelo. It is one of the most concentrated cultural sites in the city.

The hill rises steeply from the Roman Forum side. The walk up follows a wide stepped ramp called the Cordonata, designed by Michelangelo in the fifteen thirties. The Cordonata is wider than a staircase and shallow enough to ride a horse up. At the top, you emerge into the Piazza del Campidoglio — Michelangelo's famous trapezoidal piazza. The piazza has a distinctive star pavement, a central equestrian statue (a copy of an original Marcus Aurelius bronze that's now inside one of the museums), and three palaces forming the three closed sides.

The Capitoline Museums are housed in two of those three palaces — the Palazzo dei Conservatori on your right as you face the piazza, and the Palazzo Nuovo on your left. The third palace, the Palazzo Senatorio in the center, is Rome's city hall and not open to the public. The two museum palaces are connected underground by a tunnel that runs beneath the piazza.

The collections together form the oldest public art museum in the world. They were established in fourteen seventy-one by Pope Sixtus the Fourth, who donated a group of ancient bronzes to "the Roman people." The donation was both a religious gesture and a political one — Sixtus was claiming that the popes were the proper inheritors of ancient Roman civic life. The collection has grown for five and a half centuries since.

Plan three hours for both palaces; ninety minutes for one. The Palazzo Nuovo, on the left, contains the densest concentration of classical sculpture. The Palazzo dei Conservatori, on the right, mixes ancient sculpture with Renaissance and Baroque painting, plus the Capitoline Wolf and the original Marcus Aurelius.

A note on the foundation beneath. The Tabularium — the ancient Roman state archive, from seventy-eight B.C. — is visible from the lower levels of the Palazzo Senatorio. You can look out from inside the museum down into the Roman Forum, and you can also look up at the Tabularium's massive arched supports from the Forum side. The whole hill is a Roman foundation with medieval and Renaissance buildings stacked on top.
