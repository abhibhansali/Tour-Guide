Here's what to find inside the museums.

In the Palazzo dei Conservatori courtyard: the giant fragments of Constantine. The marble head of the emperor is taller than you are. The eyes are eight inches across. The original statue stood sixty feet tall and sat enthroned in the Basilica of Maxentius and Constantine in the Roman Forum — you can still see the basilica's foundations from the outside. The body was probably wooden, dressed in metal. Only the marble head, hands, and parts of the limbs survive. The eyes look upward, beyond the viewer; that was Constantine's idea of imperial dignity.

Upstairs, the original Marcus Aurelius bronze. Mounted in a glass-enclosed chamber. Walk around it slowly. The horse's anatomy, the emperor's gesture, the surface gilding — most of which has worn off but still visible in patches — are details that the copy outside doesn't quite capture.

The Capitoline Wolf. The most famous single image in Rome. A bronze sculpture of a she-wolf nursing the infant twins Romulus and Remus, the legendary founders of Rome. The wolf is medieval — probably eleventh or twelfth century — though for centuries it was claimed to be Etruscan, from around five hundred B.C. The twins are Renaissance additions, by Antonio Pollaiolo, around fourteen seventy. The sculpture together is the symbol of the city. Every Roman knows it. The image is on flags, manhole covers, official seals, sports uniforms.

The Dying Gaul. A Roman marble copy of a Greek bronze original from around two thirty B.C. A wounded Celtic warrior, supporting himself on his right arm, his face contorted in pain. The work was a Roman copy commissioned for a triumphal monument celebrating victory over Celtic tribes. The figure has dignity in defeat — the Greeks were unusual in giving sympathetic portraits of their enemies. The sculpture is one of the most affecting ancient marbles in the world.

The Capitoline Venus. A second-century A.D. Roman copy of a Greek bronze original by the sculptor Praxiteles, probably fourth century B.C. The Venus stands nude after bathing, modestly attempting to cover herself with her hands. The sculpture was the model for the entire tradition of Venus pudica — modest Venus — in Western art. Botticelli's Venus, in Florence, is a direct descendant.

Caravaggio's Saint John the Baptist. Painted in sixteen-oh-two. A young John seated on a stone, looking out at the viewer, holding the head of a ram. The painting is one of Caravaggio's more controversial works — the figure is sensual, possibly erotic, and the symbolism is unsettling. The painting was rejected for several public commissions. Cardinal Mattei kept it privately.

The Pinacoteca on the upper floor of the Conservatori. A painting collection that includes Caravaggios, a Veronese, a Tintoretto, and a major collection of Roman late-baroque painters. Worth at least forty-five minutes if you have the time.

Two views worth pausing for. The terrace looking over the Roman Forum, from the Palazzo Nuovo's upper floor — gives you the best free view of the Forum from above. And the underground passage between the two palaces, which lets you look down into the Tabularium, the Roman state archive from seventy-eight B.C., which forms the foundation of the Palazzo Senatorio. You're standing in twentieth-century Italian government infrastructure looking down at first-century-B.C. Roman government infrastructure.
