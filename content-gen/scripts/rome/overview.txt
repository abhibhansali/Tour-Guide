Rome's history is too long. There is genuinely too much. The city has been continuously inhabited for almost three thousand years, and at various points it has been the capital of the largest empire in the ancient world, the headquarters of the largest religious institution in human history, the seat of a unified Italian state, and the cultural center of Western Europe. To compress this into ten minutes is unfair to Rome and probably unfair to you. But here is a try.

The name is older than the recorded history of the city itself. The traditional founding date is April twenty-first, seven hundred and fifty-three B.C., the day when Romulus — according to legend — killed his twin brother Remus and laid out the boundaries of a new city on the Palatine Hill. The legend is too neat to be literally true, but archaeology has found Iron Age settlements on the Palatine going back to about the eighth century B.C. — so the legend exists for a reason. The actual etymology of "Roma" is debated. It may come from an Etruscan word for a teat — a reference to the she-wolf who supposedly nursed the twin founders — or from a much older root meaning river, the Tiber having shaped the city from the beginning.

The early Romans, in the seven hundreds to five hundreds B.C., were not yet a great power. They were a small Latin-speaking tribe at the southern edge of Etruscan civilization. Their last king, Tarquin the Proud, was overthrown around five hundred and nine B.C. The Romans then formed a republic — government by elected magistrates and a senate, not by hereditary kings. The republican experiment lasted, in various forms, for almost five centuries. It is, alongside the Greek democracies, the political model that all modern republics consciously imitate. The American founders read Cicero before they wrote the Constitution.

Over the next four hundred years, Republican Rome conquered everything around it. The Punic Wars, fought against Carthage between two hundred and sixty-four and one hundred and forty-six B.C., made Rome the master of the western Mediterranean. By the first century B.C., Rome controlled most of the modern Mediterranean basin from Spain to the Levant.

The Republic ended in civil war. Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon River in forty-nine B.C., effectively staging a coup. He was assassinated in the Senate on March fifteenth, forty-four B.C. His adoptive son Octavian — the future Augustus — became sole ruler by twenty-seven B.C. The Roman Empire was born, and it would last another five centuries in the west and almost fifteen hundred years in the east.

Imperial Rome's contributions to engineering and material science are still studied. Roman concrete — opus caementicium, a mix of volcanic ash, lime, and aggregate — is stronger underwater than modern Portland cement. Recent research at M.I.T., published in twenty twenty-three, identified the mechanism: tiny "lime clasts" embedded in the concrete react with water entering through cracks, automatically filling and resealing those cracks with calcium carbonate. Roman concrete is, in effect, self-healing. We have only begun to reverse-engineer it. The Pantheon's dome, the largest unreinforced concrete dome in the world even today, is made of this material. It has stood for almost nineteen hundred years.

The Romans also built fifty thousand miles of paved roads, some still drivable. They built aqueducts that delivered fresh water across hundreds of kilometers. The Aqua Vergine, built by Marcus Agrippa in nineteen B.C., still feeds the Trevi Fountain. They invented apartment-block housing — the insula — which solved urban density problems that European cities wouldn't face again for fifteen hundred years.

They also reformed time itself. In forty-six B.C., Julius Caesar overhauled the Roman calendar based on a recommendation from the Alexandrian astronomer Sosigenes. The result, the Julian calendar, was adopted across the empire and remained the standard for the next sixteen hundred years. In fifteen eighty-two, Pope Gregory the Thirteenth corrected the Julian calendar's small annual drift, producing the Gregorian calendar — which is now the international civil calendar used by essentially every modern government. Both calendar reforms originated in Rome.

The Western Roman Empire fell in steps across the fourth and fifth centuries A.D. The emperor Constantine moved the capital to Byzantium — modern Istanbul — in three hundred and thirty A.D., effectively abandoning Rome as the empire's political center. The Visigoths sacked the city in four hundred and ten A.D. The Vandals did it again in four fifty-five. The last Western Roman emperor was deposed in four seventy-six. Rome's population, which had peaked above one million during the high empire, collapsed to perhaps thirty thousand by the early Middle Ages.

What kept Rome from disappearing entirely was the Catholic Church. The bishop of Rome — the pope — claimed succession from the apostle Peter, and over centuries that claim became the institutional spine of Western Christianity. The Papal States, a sovereign territory ruled by the pope, controlled most of central Italy from the eighth century until eighteen seventy.

The Renaissance hit Rome in the early fifteen hundreds. Pope Julius the Second, an aggressive patron and builder, commissioned Bramante to start a new Saint Peter's Basilica in fifteen-oh-six, hired Michelangelo to paint the Sistine Chapel in fifteen-oh-eight, and Raphael to paint his apartments at the same time. Rome became, for the next century, the artistic center of Europe.

The popes also founded the Vatican Observatory in fifteen eighty-two, partly to support the Gregorian calendar reform's astronomical research. The observatory — Specola Vaticana — is still operating today, with a research facility at Castel Gandolfo south of Rome and an additional observatory in Arizona. It is the oldest continuously-operating astronomical institution in the world.

A darker scientific moment also happened in Rome. In sixteen thirty-three, the seventy-year-old Galileo Galilei was tried by the Roman Inquisition for arguing that the Earth orbits the Sun. He was forced to recant under threat of execution. The Church formally rehabilitated him in nineteen ninety-two — three hundred and fifty-nine years later. The building where he was tried still stands, near Piazza Navona.

Modern science in the Rome region is mostly out of view to tourists but world-class. The Frascati National Laboratories, about twenty kilometers south, are one of Europe's major particle physics centers. The Italian National Institute of Nuclear Physics, the INFN, was effectively founded by Enrico Fermi and his Roman colleagues — the "Via Panisperna boys" — in the nineteen thirties, before most of them fled fascist Italy. Their early work on slow neutrons led directly to the development of nuclear reactors. The European Space Agency has major facilities at Frascati as well.

The political history closes with unification. Italy became a single state in eighteen sixty-one, with Turin and then Florence as its temporary capitals. Italian troops took Rome from the papacy by force in eighteen seventy. Rome has been the Italian capital since. The pope retreated to the Vatican — about a hundred and ten acres in the city's northwest — and remained essentially a prisoner of the new Italian state until the Lateran Treaty of nineteen twenty-nine recognized Vatican City as an independent sovereign state. It still is — the smallest sovereign state in the world.

Modern Rome has about two point eight million inhabitants. The city is the headquarters of the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization. It hosts a major international film industry at Cinecittà, the studio complex on the eastern edge of the city that produced the great Italian films of the nineteen forties through seventies. The Treaty of Rome, signed in nineteen fifty-seven, founded the European Economic Community — what eventually became the European Union. Rome's role in the European project is more than ceremonial.

Three thousand years on, Rome is still the city that defined what cities should look like. The grid plan, the public square, the elevated aqueduct, the apartment block, the triumphal arch, the church basilica — all of these architectural types were invented or refined here. Walking through any modern Western city, you are walking through dim reflections of Roman ideas. The original is here.
