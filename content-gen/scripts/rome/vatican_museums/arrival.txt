You're about to walk into one of the largest, oldest, and most concentrated art collections in human history. Let me give you the lay of the land before you're swept along in the crowd.

The Vatican Museums are not one museum. They're a sequence of more than twenty galleries strung together inside the Vatican Palace, the building where the pope lives. There are roughly seventy thousand objects in the collection. Of those, about twenty thousand are on display. The full route — the standard tourist circuit — covers more than four miles of corridors.

The collection began in fifteen-oh-six, when a farmer plowing a vineyard nearby uncovered a marble sculpture of a man and his two sons being strangled by snakes. It was the Laocoön — a Greek masterpiece from the first century B.C., described by ancient writers but never actually seen by any living person until that day. Pope Julius the Second sent Michelangelo to authenticate it. Michelangelo identified it instantly. Julius bought it and put it on display in the Belvedere Courtyard, and the popes have been adding to it ever since.

What followed was five centuries of papal collecting — Egyptian antiquities, Etruscan tombs, Greek and Roman sculpture, Renaissance painting, baroque ceiling frescoes, modern religious art, and an unbroken sequence of rooms decorated by Raphael, Pinturicchio, Botticelli, Ghirlandaio, and finally Michelangelo himself.

The route is essentially one-way. You'll be funneled along a corridor that ends at the Sistine Chapel. Once inside the Sistine, you can either exit toward Saint Peter's or back to the main entrance. Most days, there's no going back to revisit a room you missed.

So a quick strategic note. You'll see the famous things — the long Gallery of Maps, the Raphael Rooms, the spiral staircase on the way out. But the side rooms reward whoever takes a moment off the main artery. The Egyptian museum near the start has full mummified bodies, recovered by nineteenth-century papal scholars. The Pinacoteca, just off the main route, has a Caravaggio and a Leonardo. We'll point out the ones we'd particularly notice.

For now: be patient with the crowd. Walk at your own pace. The art was already old when most modern museums hadn't been invented yet, and it isn't going anywhere.
