In a museum this dense, the rule is to choose what to ignore. Here are the things I'd point at specifically.

The Laocoön, in the Octagonal Court. It's behind a small crowd, always. Get close enough to see the muscles, the veins, the actual expression of pain on the father's face. This is the foundational sculpture of Western art — the first piece Julius the Second bought, the standard for everything that came after. Michelangelo studied it before painting the Sistine Chapel. You can see its influence in everything he did after.

The Belvedere Torso, also in the Octagonal Court. A headless, limbless fragment of a male figure, signed by a Greek sculptor named Apollonius. Michelangelo loved it. The story is that he refused to "restore" it when popes asked him to, because he thought the fragment was perfect as it was. The Sistine ceiling figures — the muscular nude prophets — are direct echoes of this torso.

The Gallery of Maps. A two-hundred-foot corridor with frescoed maps of every region of Italy as they were understood in fifteen eighty. The ceiling is gilded. The maps are surprisingly accurate. The little ships and sea monsters in the Mediterranean are worth slowing down for.

In the Raphael Rooms, the School of Athens. It's on the wall to the right as you enter the room. Plato pointing up, Aristotle pointing down — idealism versus empiricism, in a single hand gesture. Find Heraclitus brooding on the steps in the foreground — Raphael painted him as a portrait of Michelangelo, who was working on the Sistine ceiling next door at the time. Raphael did it as a private tribute.

In the Pinacoteca — if you can find it, it's off the main route — the Caravaggio Deposition and Leonardo's unfinished Saint Jerome. Leonardo never finished much. This painting is bare-canvas in places, ghostly. You can see his drawing underneath. It's a rare chance to see how Leonardo actually built a picture.

The Egyptian Museum near the start. Most people walk through it fast. Slow down. There are real mummies, real coffins, and a granite statue of Tuya, the mother of Ramses the Second, three thousand years old. The room is quiet because most tourists have already rushed ahead.

And on your way out — the Momo staircase. Look up the central well. It's a double helix, like DNA, designed in nineteen-thirty-two. One of the most photographed staircases in the world. Most tourists are too busy descending to look back up.
