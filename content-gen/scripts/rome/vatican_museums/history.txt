The collection's history is the history of how the popes thought about the past.

For most of the Middle Ages, classical antiquity was a problem the church wanted to avoid. Pagan sculpture was associated with idolatry; classical literature was suspect. Marble statues of Greek gods were burned for lime, used for foundations, ground up for road fill. The Roman Forum became a quarry. The Pantheon was an exception because it had been consecrated as a church.

In the early fourteen hundreds, that began to change. The Renaissance popes — Nicholas the Fifth, then Pius the Second, then Julius the Second — started seeing classical antiquity not as a threat but as an inheritance. The argument was that ancient civilization had reached its highest point with Christianity, and the great pagan art was the soil that Christian art had grown out of. By that logic, the church should preserve it.

Julius the Second is the founding figure. When the Laocoön was dug up in fifteen-oh-six, he bought it and put it in the Belvedere Courtyard, on display for cardinals and visiting ambassadors. That courtyard, just past the Egyptian wing, is still the heart of the museum. Most of the most famous classical sculptures in the collection still sit around its edges.

The next two hundred years brought a steady accumulation: Egyptian obelisks, Etruscan jewelry from tombs in northern Italy, Greek bronzes pulled from shipwrecks, Roman copies of Greek originals collected from across the former empire. Each pope tended to add a wing or build a new gallery for his particular passion.

Then came Napoleon. In seventeen ninety-seven, French troops occupied the Papal States, and Napoleon shipped a hundred crates of art — including the Laocoön and the Apollo Belvedere — back to Paris. They sat in the Louvre for almost twenty years. After Waterloo, in eighteen-fifteen, the papacy got them back. The Italian sculptor Antonio Canova personally negotiated the return.

The collection grew in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries with archaeological discoveries — including major Etruscan tomb finds — and with donated modern religious art. The latest major addition is a wing for contemporary art opened by Pope Paul the Sixth in the nineteen seventies. There's a Picasso in there. There's a Francis Bacon.

The point is that the Vatican collection is alive. It's still acquiring, still arranging, still negotiating with archaeological sites worldwide. It is the longest continuously curated collection on Earth.
