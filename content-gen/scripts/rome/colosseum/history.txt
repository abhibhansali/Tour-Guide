Construction began under the Emperor Vespasian around seventy A.D. and finished a decade later under his son Titus. The inaugural games in eighty A.D. lasted a hundred days. The historian Cassius Dio claims that nine thousand animals were killed during those games. That figure is probably high, but not absurdly so.

The Colosseum was used for three main types of event. In the morning came the venationes — staged animal hunts, often spectacular and exotic: lions, leopards, bears, elephants, rhinoceroses, brought from across the empire. At midday came the executions of criminals, often theatrically staged using mythological themes. And in the afternoon came the main event: the munera, the gladiatorial combats.

Most gladiators were slaves, prisoners of war, or condemned criminals. A few were volunteers — Romans who, for money or fame or because they'd lost everything else, took the oath to fight. They trained in specialized schools just east of here, on what's now Via San Giovanni in Laterano. The most famous school, the Ludus Magnus, was connected to the Colosseum by an underground tunnel. You can still see its excavated foundations beside the metro stop.

Not every fight ended in death. Gladiators were expensive — the equivalent of professional athletes — and their owners had a financial interest in keeping them alive. A defeated gladiator could request mercy. Whether he got it depended on the crowd and on the man hosting the games.

The thumbs gesture you've seen in films — the thumb up or down to decide a man's fate — is mostly Hollywood. The Romans gestured with a closed fist or a pointed thumb, and we still don't really know which direction meant which.

The games went on, in some form, for almost four hundred years. The last recorded gladiatorial combats happened in the early fourth century. Animal hunts continued into the sixth.

After the games stopped, the building had a long second life. It became a fortress in the Middle Ages, a quarry in the Renaissance, a Christian shrine in the seventeen hundreds when Pope Benedict the Fourteenth declared it consecrated ground. That declaration — even more than the building's beauty — is what stopped further quarrying. The popes saved the Colosseum because Christians had died inside it.

We're not entirely sure that's true, by the way. The historical evidence for Christian martyrdoms at the Colosseum specifically is thin. But the legend protected the building, and the building is still here.
