You're walking into Santa Maria Maggiore — Saint Mary Major — one of the four great papal basilicas of Rome and the largest church in the city dedicated to the Virgin Mary. It is also the only one of the major basilicas that has preserved its early-Christian architectural plan essentially intact for almost sixteen hundred years.

The basilica was founded around four thirty-two A.D. by Pope Sixtus the Third, immediately after the Council of Ephesus in four thirty-one had declared Mary to be Theotokos — "God-bearer" — and effectively elevated her to a central position in Christian theology. Building a major Roman basilica dedicated to her was a political statement: the western papacy embracing the new doctrine. Sixtus built the church on a site that already had Marian traditions. Legend has it that a wealthy Roman couple had dreamed of the Virgin asking them to build a church wherever snow fell on the morning of August fifth — and that miraculous snow did fall on this hill the next day. Every August fifth, the church re-enacts the miracle by dropping white rose petals from the ceiling during mass.

The basilica's central nave is fifth-century work. The mosaics along the upper walls and around the triumphal arch are fifth-century work. The forty marble columns supporting the nave are even older, salvaged from older Roman buildings. Walking into the nave today, you're seeing essentially what a fifth-century Roman would have seen — give or take a few baroque additions, a Renaissance ceiling, and centuries of cleaning.

The mosaics are the artistic centerpiece. Twenty-seven panels along the upper walls of the nave depict scenes from the Old Testament — the books of Genesis, Exodus, Joshua. They're done in a vivid late-Roman style, almost cinematic in their narrative compression. The mosaic over the triumphal arch above the altar depicts scenes from the life of Christ. The apse mosaic, in the curved space behind the altar, is later — completed by Jacopo Torriti in twelve ninety-six, depicting the Coronation of the Virgin. The mosaic is one of the great medieval works in Rome.

The ceiling of the nave was paid for in fifteen hundred. According to tradition, the gold leaf gilding it was made from the first shipment of gold brought back from the New World by Christopher Columbus, presented to Pope Alexander the Sixth (a Borgia), who used it to gild this ceiling. Whether the story is literally true is uncertain — it depends on contemporary records that aren't entirely reliable — but the gold leaf is indeed early sixteenth century, and the church has maintained the legend for centuries.

A few specific things to look at:

The Borghese Chapel, on the left side. A baroque chapel commissioned by Pope Paul the Fifth Borghese in sixteen-oh-five — the same family as Cardinal Scipione of the Galleria Borghese. The chapel houses a small Byzantine icon, the Salus Populi Romani, "Salvation of the Roman People," that's been venerated since at least the eleventh century. The icon is one of the most important Marian images in Catholicism. Pope Francis stops here to pray before and after major international trips.

The Sistine Chapel — not the famous one. This one is in Santa Maria Maggiore, built for Pope Sixtus the Fifth in fifteen eighty-five (the other Sistine Chapel was built for a different Sixtus). The Sistine Chapel here is on the right side of the nave. It contains the tomb of Pope Sixtus himself and several other popes.

Bernini's tomb. The great baroque sculptor is buried in this basilica, in a simple slab on the floor in front of the high altar. He had requested this resting place. The slab is marked with a plain inscription: "Here rests Gian Lorenzo Bernini, in honor of the Bernini family." Most visitors walk over it without realizing.

Entry to the basilica is free. The museum and Sistine Chapel inside have a small fee. Plan an hour. Modest dress required.
