You're standing in one of the most distinctive pieces of urban design in Rome, and most people don't realize why it has its peculiar shape. Piazza Navona is a perfect elongated oval, with rounded ends — not a square at all in any rectangular sense. The reason: it's built directly on top of a Roman athletic stadium.

The Emperor Domitian, around eighty-six A.D., constructed a stadium on this spot for foot races and athletic competitions. It seated about thirty thousand spectators. The Romans called it the Stadium of Domitian, or sometimes the Circus Agonalis — agonalis meaning "of the games." Over the centuries, "agonalis" got slurred into "n'agone," and then "navona." The piazza's name is a thirteen-hundred-year-old slur of the Latin word for games.

When Rome collapsed and the stadium fell into disuse, medieval Romans built houses directly on top of its outer arcade. The stadium's seating rows became foundations. The track became a market square. Today, if you look at any modern map of central Rome, you can spot the piazza by its shape — it's a fossil of a stadium two thousand years old. You can even visit the original Roman ruins just below the surface, in a small archaeological site at the north end of the piazza.

For centuries the piazza was actually flooded each August. The drains were sealed, the water from the surrounding fountains flowed in, and a few inches of standing water covered the whole oval. Romans waded through it; carriages drove through it; people threw parties in it. They called it the Lago di Piazza Navona, the lake of Piazza Navona. The custom ended only in the late eighteen hundreds.

The piazza has three major fountains. In the center is the Fountain of the Four Rivers — a vast and dramatic work by Gianlorenzo Bernini, finished in sixteen fifty-one for Pope Innocent the Tenth. Bernini designed the whole thing as a pyramid of rock supporting a real Egyptian obelisk on top, with four giant marble figures around the base, each representing one of the four major rivers known at the time: the Nile, the Ganges, the Danube, and the Rio de la Plata. Each figure has surrounding animals and plants from its part of the world. The Nile figure covers his face with a cloth — the official explanation is that the source of the Nile was, at the time, unknown.

There's a famous legend that Bernini sculpted the Nile that way to avoid looking at the church directly opposite — Sant'Agnese in Agone, designed by Bernini's rival Francesco Borromini. The two were locked in a public feud for decades. The story is delicious but probably false: the church's façade was actually built after Bernini's fountain. But the legend has stuck around Rome for three centuries because it's the kind of thing Bernini would have done.

At the south end of the piazza is the Fountain of the Moor, also originally by Giacomo della Porta in fifteen seventy-five, with a central figure added later by Bernini. At the north end is the Fountain of Neptune, designed by della Porta but left without statuary for three hundred years until figures were finally added in eighteen seventy-eight.

The church on the side of the piazza is Sant'Agnese in Agone, built where the young martyr Agnes was, according to tradition, executed in the Roman stadium itself in the year three hundred and four. The façade is Borromini's masterpiece — concave, swooping, gracefully curved. Step inside if it's open. The interior is small and intense, built on a Greek cross plan, and the dome catches afternoon light beautifully.

A practical note. Piazza Navona is one of the busiest spots in Rome. The cafés around the edges are expensive — about double the price of equivalent spots two blocks away. The restaurants are tourist traps in every sense. Take in the fountains, walk through, and then go eat in the smaller streets to the east or south.

Come back at night. The fountains are lit, the crowds thin out, and the whole oval feels like a stage with the lights still on.
