The building you're standing in is the third Pantheon. Marcus Agrippa built the first one around twenty-seven B.C. — a more conventional rectangular temple, dedicated to all the gods. The name means exactly that: pan-theon, all the gods. It burned in a fire in eighty A.D. and was rebuilt, and then burned again under the Emperor Trajan.

What you see now is the Emperor Hadrian's rebuild, finished around one hundred and twenty-five A.D. Hadrian was an architect himself — a hands-on emperor with strong opinions about buildings. The Pantheon was his statement. He left Agrippa's old inscription on the front for political cover, but every brick and every bag of concrete inside is his.

How did this dome survive when nothing else like it did? Three reasons.

First, the concrete. The Romans had a recipe — volcanic ash from the Bay of Naples mixed with lime — that hardens under seawater and only gets stronger with age. We've lost the exact formula. Modern concrete cracks within a century or two. Roman concrete in salt water is still bonding.

Second, the engineering. The dome is heaviest at the bottom and lightest at the top. The aggregate gradually changes as you go up — from dense basalt near the base, to lighter limestone in the middle, to pumice near the oculus. The whole dome weighs about five thousand tons, but that weight is carried by walls below that are twenty feet thick.

Third, sheer luck. In the year six hundred and nine A.D., Pope Boniface the Fourth took the building and consecrated it as a Christian church — Santa Maria ad Martyres, Saint Mary and the Martyrs. That conversion is the only reason the Pantheon is still here. Every other major Roman building in the city was quarried for its stone. The Pantheon was protected because Catholics were saying mass inside it.

It wasn't completely spared. In the sixteen hundreds, Pope Urban the Eighth — a Barberini — stripped the bronze off the porch ceiling and melted it down. Some of that bronze became cannons for Castel Sant'Angelo. Some became Bernini's giant canopy in Saint Peter's Basilica. A Roman wit at the time wrote, in Latin: "What the barbarians did not do, the Barberini did."

The dome itself, though, was never touched. Almost two thousand years on, it has never been rebuilt, never reinforced, never repaired.
