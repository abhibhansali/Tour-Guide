You're standing on or near the Ponte Vecchio — the Old Bridge — and it earned that name a long time ago. There has been a bridge across the Arno at this exact spot since Roman times. The current stone bridge dates from thirteen forty-five, when an earlier wooden version was destroyed by flooding. It is the oldest surviving bridge in Florence, and it is the oldest bridge in Europe with shops still operating on top of it.

The shops are the strange part. Most medieval bridges were just bridges. Ponte Vecchio became something more — a covered marketplace, with permanent stalls built into the bridge structure itself, hanging out over the water in wooden boxes called sporti. The original tenants were butchers, fishmongers, and tanners. They used the river underneath as a dumping ground for their waste. The smell, by all accounts, was tremendous.

The trades you see today — jewelers and goldsmiths, almost exclusively — date from a specific decree. In fifteen ninety-three, the Grand Duke Ferdinando the First de' Medici walked across the bridge on his way between the family's two palaces and decided he couldn't take the smell anymore. He issued an order: no more butchers, no more tanners, only goldsmiths and jewelers were permitted to operate shops on the bridge. The order has, more or less, held for four hundred and thirty years.

Look up. Above the eastern row of shops you can see a closed-in covered corridor running along the top of the bridge. That's the Vasari Corridor. The same Grand Duke, Cosimo the First, had asked the architect Giorgio Vasari in fifteen sixty-five to build him a private passageway from his official offices in the Palazzo Vecchio to his new private residence at the Palazzo Pitti, on the south side of the river. The corridor crosses about a kilometer of central Florence, passing over the bridge, running through buildings, even cutting through a church — so that the duke could move between his two seats of power without ever stepping into the street. The corridor is closed to the public most of the time but has occasionally been opened for restoration tours.

There's a wartime story you may have heard. In nineteen forty-four, retreating German troops blew up every bridge across the Arno except this one. The Ponte Vecchio survived because, according to legend, Hitler had personally ordered it spared. The German commanders instead blew up the buildings at either end of the bridge to make it inaccessible. The buildings were rebuilt after the war; the bridge itself is the original stone.

The Allied forces, when they arrived a few days later, had to cross the Arno on temporary pontoons. The Ponte Vecchio was standing but cut off, surrounded by rubble. The photograph of it isolated in the destruction became one of the iconic images of the Italian campaign.

A few things to notice while you're here. The shops are very small — each one is essentially a single-room box. Many of them have been operated by the same families for generations. The jewelry is mostly real and mostly expensive, though touristy items have crept in. The bust on the eastern side, near the middle, is of Benvenuto Cellini, the great Florentine goldsmith — a tribute placed by the modern jewelers' guild.

For about a decade in the two thousands, lovers attached padlocks to the railings of the bridge to symbolize their relationships. The weight of the locks started threatening the railings, and the city banned the practice in two thousand six. Most of the locks have been removed, but you can still find a few attached to less-visible spots.

Stand in the middle of the bridge, lean on the railing, and look down at the river. The view of the next bridge upstream — the Santa Trinita — is one of the best photographs in Florence. The Ponte Vecchio in the foreground or background of any picture you take will look exactly the same as it did to a Florentine in fifteen sixty-five.
