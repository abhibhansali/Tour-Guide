The tombs and the frescoes are the two main attractions. Let me point at the specific ones to find.

Michelangelo's tomb. First major tomb on the right as you walk in. Designed by Vasari in fifteen seventy. The tomb is a baroque concoction: an arched niche with three female allegorical figures representing Painting, Sculpture, and Architecture, all mourning. Above them, a bust of Michelangelo. Below, the inscription. The tomb is theatrical and slightly overdone — Vasari was honoring his master, but his master had asked to be buried simply in Rome. The Florentines wanted him here. They got him.

Galileo's tomb. On the right side of the nave, in a special niche. The tomb is from seventeen thirty-seven, when the Catholic church finally permitted Galileo to be moved out of the closet where he'd been kept for ninety-five years after his heresy conviction. The tomb is a baroque ceremonial monument. Galileo is depicted on a pedestal, surrounded by figures of Geometry and Astronomy. The placement directly opposite Michelangelo's tomb is deliberate — Florence chose to put its two greatest figures of art and science face to face across the nave.

Machiavelli's tomb. Further down on the right. Smaller, plainer, with a Latin inscription that begins: "No epitaph adequate to this name." The placement is appropriate — Machiavelli was a political theorist, not a religious figure, and the marble is more sober than the others.

Dante's cenotaph. On the right side, between Michelangelo and Galileo. A large carved monument, but the tomb is empty — Dante is buried in Ravenna, where he died in exile from Florence in thirteen twenty-one. Ravenna has consistently refused to return his remains. Florence has consistently maintained a cenotaph in his honor since the eighteen thirties.

The Bardi Chapel. In the right transept, off the apse. Frescoes by Giotto from around thirteen twenty-five, depicting the life of Saint Francis. The Trial by Fire scene — Saint Francis offering to walk into a fire with a Muslim sultan to prove his faith — is one of Giotto's best. The figures have weight, the composition is balanced, the space is convincing. This is what made Giotto the first modern painter.

The Peruzzi Chapel. Next to the Bardi. Frescoes by Giotto from a slightly later period, around thirteen thirty. Life of John the Baptist and John the Evangelist. Painted more dramatically than the Bardi cycle, with elongated figures and more emotional gesture. Together the two chapels are arguably the most concentrated Giotto exposure available anywhere.

Donatello's Cavalcanti Annunciation. On a pillar in the right aisle, easy to miss. A sandstone relief sculpture from around fourteen thirty-five, depicting the angel Gabriel kneeling before Mary. The Madonna's downturned face and the angel's gentle pose are unlike Florentine painted Annunciations of the same period. Donatello working in low relief.

The Pazzi Chapel. Through a side door, at the back of the cloister. Built by Brunelleschi between fourteen forty-three and fourteen sixty-one. A small Renaissance chapel for the Pazzi family — the same family that conspired to assassinate the Medici brothers in fourteen seventy-eight. The interior is one of Brunelleschi's purest geometric compositions: a square plan, a hemispherical dome, gray pietra serena stone framing white-plastered surfaces. The simplicity is the lesson. Brunelleschi was teaching Florence what classical proportions looked like.
