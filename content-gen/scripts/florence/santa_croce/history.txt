Santa Croce was begun in twelve ninety-four, the same year construction started on the Florence Cathedral. Arnolfo di Cambio, the architect of the Cathedral, designed both. The Franciscans were given the site by the city. They had been preaching in Florence for nearly a century by then, in smaller chapels around the eastern part of the city. The new basilica was meant to be the order's flagship in central Italy.

Construction took roughly a century — typical for a major medieval church. The basic structure was complete by the late thirteen hundreds. The bell tower was added in the fifteen sixties, the white-and-green marble facade in eighteen sixty-three. The famous statue of Dante outside the facade was added in eighteen sixty-five for the six hundredth anniversary of his birth.

The Franciscans wanted Santa Croce to be a teaching church. Franciscan friars preached publicly to large crowds. They needed open interior space, sight lines from anywhere in the nave to the pulpits, and wall surfaces big enough for narrative fresco cycles that could illustrate sermons. Santa Croce was designed with all of that in mind. Sixteen side chapels along the walls of the apse and transept were funded by wealthy Florentine families, who commissioned frescoes for them. The Bardi family funded the Bardi Chapel. The Peruzzi family funded the Peruzzi Chapel. The Baroncelli family funded another. The painters they hired were the best in Florence at the time. Most of those painters were students or imitators of Giotto.

Giotto himself painted the Bardi Chapel and the Peruzzi Chapel personally, probably between thirteen-fifteen and thirteen-thirty. The frescoes were partly whitewashed in the seventeen hundreds during a baroque renovation, then partially recovered in the eighteen-fifties. What you see today is a complicated mix of original Giotto, what survived the centuries, and modern restoration. They are still among the most important early Renaissance frescoes anywhere.

The transformation of Santa Croce into a national pantheon happened slowly. Michelangelo was the first major artist buried here, in fifteen sixty-four. His tomb was designed by Vasari and executed by a team of Florentine sculptors. The tomb's presence reinforced Santa Croce's reputation as a special place for the burial of cultural figures.

Galileo was buried here in sixteen forty-two — but quietly, in a closet at the back, because he had been convicted of heresy by the Inquisition. His remains stayed there for ninety-five years. In seventeen thirty-seven, the church was finally permitted to move him to a proper tomb along the nave wall. That tomb, designed in baroque style, is what you see now. The translation of his remains was a political event — a sign that the new Habsburg-Lorraine rulers were less worried about Inquisition opinions than the previous Medici grand dukes had been.

Machiavelli was buried here in fifteen twenty-seven, but his tomb is from much later — added in seventeen eighty-seven as part of the same political reframing.

The flood of November nineteen sixty-six damaged the church badly. The Arno overflowed, and Santa Croce was hit by twenty feet of water. Cimabue's giant Crucifix, one of the great thirteenth-century paintings in Florence, was partially destroyed. Restoration of the building and its art continued for thirty years.
