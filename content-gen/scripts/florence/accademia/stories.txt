A few stories around the David and around the museum.

The flawed block. The marble that became the David had been considered ruined for almost forty years before Michelangelo touched it. The flaw in the marble was real — a vein of weakness running through the middle of the block. Earlier sculptors who'd examined it had concluded the stone would crack if worked. Michelangelo worked around the flaw. The visible joint in the David's neck and chest area corresponds to that vein. He chose to carve the body in such a pose that the vein ran through a structural part of the sculpture — between the shoulder and the neck — where compression would be most resistant. The technical achievement was as important as the artistic one. He extracted a heroic figure from a block that everyone else had given up on.

The arm break. In fifteen twenty-seven, the Medici were briefly expelled from Florence in a riot. A mob attacked the Palazzo Vecchio. During the fighting, a bench was thrown from a high window and struck the David's left arm, breaking it into three pieces. The pieces fell into the street and lay there. A bystander — the painter Giorgio Vasari, who'd later write the foundational history of Renaissance art — picked them up and stored them in his house. Years later, when the political situation stabilized, the pieces were retrieved and the arm was reattached. You can see the joint lines if you look carefully at the left arm above the elbow.

The Carrara quarries. The marble for the David came from Carrara, the famous quarries in the northern Apennines. The Romans had quarried there since the first century B.C. Michelangelo personally visited Carrara to select his blocks. He had specific preferences — fine grain, no visible iron deposits, white rather than blue-tinged. He spent months at a time at the quarries throughout his life. Marble from Carrara still moves through the same quarries today, and the workmen still pull blocks out using techniques essentially unchanged from Michelangelo's time.

The unfinished Slaves and the doctrine of liberazione. Michelangelo wrote, in a sonnet, that the sculptor's job is to liberate a figure already trapped in the stone. The Prisoners are the most direct example of this idea in his work — figures half-released from the block. Whether they're unfinished or intentionally left in this state has been debated. Most art historians believe Michelangelo simply abandoned them as the tomb project shrank. Some argue he developed the idea of the deliberately unfinished — the non finito — as a stylistic choice, especially in his late work. Either way, the figures are extraordinary, and they let you see Michelangelo's process in a way his finished works never do.

The musicaland the medici. Up on the second floor, the museum of historical musical instruments is one of the most under-visited rooms in the building. It contains instruments from the Medici grand-ducal collection — including violins by Stradivarius and Amati, harpsichords, and the first known piano-forte, built by Bartolomeo Cristofori in seventeen-twenty. Cristofori was the inventor of the modern piano. He worked in Florence. The first piano in the world is upstairs, and almost no one visits it.
