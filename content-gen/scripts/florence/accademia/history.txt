The David has the strangest origin story of any major Renaissance sculpture.

In fourteen sixty-four, the cathedral works committee — the Opera del Duomo — commissioned a series of giant statues of Old Testament figures for the upper buttresses of the Florence Cathedral. The committee bought a massive block of Carrara marble for one of them, intended to be a figure of David. The block was huge — over seventeen feet tall — but flawed. Several years later, the sculptor Agostino di Duccio began work on it. He carved roughly between the legs, shaping the rough outline, and then abandoned the project. The block sat in the cathedral workshop for the next thirty-six years, half-worked, exposed to weather, considered ruined.

In fifteen-oh-one, the committee tried again. They offered the project to a number of sculptors. Most refused — the block was too damaged. They eventually offered it to Michelangelo, who was twenty-six years old, freshly returned to Florence after his early Pietà in Rome had made him famous. Michelangelo accepted. He worked for two and a half years, inside a wooden shed built around the block to protect it from weather and from public view. He worked alone, refusing help. He slept in his clothes, ate at his workbench. His brother delivered food.

When the statue was finished in fifteen-oh-four, the committee changed plans. The David was too important, too big, too well-carved to be hoisted onto a buttress hundreds of feet above the piazza. A committee of artists — including Leonardo da Vinci, Botticelli, and Filippino Lippi — was convened to decide where to place it. They debated for weeks. Leonardo argued for putting it in the Loggia dei Lanzi, partially hidden. Botticelli argued for the cathedral facade. Michelangelo wanted Piazza della Signoria, the public square in front of the Palazzo Vecchio. He won.

The David was hauled through the streets of Florence on rollers, taking four days to move the half-mile from the cathedral workshop to the piazza. Crowds gathered. Some pro-Medici Florentines, opposed to the city's republican government, threw stones at the statue during the move. The David, an image of a small republican defeating a tyrannical giant, was politically loaded from day one. He was meant to be a Florentine warning to outside powers.

He stood in Piazza della Signoria for three hundred and sixty-nine years. He was struck by lightning in fifteen twelve. His left arm was broken off in a fifteen twenty-seven riot and pieced back together. He weathered, eroded, was patched repeatedly. In eighteen seventy-three, finally, the Italian state moved him indoors.

The move itself was theatrical. A wooden cradle was built. The David was rolled on iron rails from Piazza della Signoria to the Accademia, a journey that took several days. The route is now marked with small plaques in the pavement. The cradle, by tradition, is in the museum's archives.
