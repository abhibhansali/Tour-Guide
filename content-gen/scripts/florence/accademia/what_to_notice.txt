Now stand in front of the David and let me point at some things.

The proportions are wrong, deliberately. The head and right hand are too large for the body. The shoulders are slightly broader than they would be on a real seventeen-foot man. Michelangelo did this because the statue was meant to be viewed from below — from the piazza, looking up at a figure on a pedestal. Enlarged head and hands counteract the optical foreshortening that would have made them seem too small from down there. Now that you're looking at him at roughly his own eye level, the proportions look slightly off. The hands look enormous. The head sits forward, almost too heavy. That's deliberate.

His pose. He's not yet fighting Goliath. He's about to. The sling is over his left shoulder, the stone is in his right hand, his weight has shifted onto his right leg as he begins to turn. The body is in the moment of decision, not action. Michelangelo's David is contemplating violence rather than committing it. Other Renaissance sculptors — Donatello, Verrocchio — depicted David after the victory, standing on Goliath's head. Michelangelo painted the instant before.

His face. Look at it from below if you can. The expression is calm but watchful. The brows are slightly furrowed. The mouth is tense. He's preparing himself.

The veins in the right hand. Look at the hand holding the stone. Michelangelo carved each vein, each tendon, the subtle bone structure. He had dissected cadavers as a teenager to learn anatomy. The hand is one of the most anatomically detailed pieces of sculpture in Western art.

Now look at his pubic hair. This is something most visitors don't realize: Michelangelo carved the pubic hair almost obsessively in detail. The proportions of David's body suggest a young teenager — David in the biblical story was a youth — but the body is also clearly a mature man's. Michelangelo was working out the conflict between heroic adult body and youthful biblical character. The genitals are deliberately understated; the body is heroic; the face is young.

The unfinished works behind you. Turn around and walk slowly back through the corridor. The four Prisoners — sometimes called Slaves — are male figures emerging from rough blocks. Michelangelo carved them in the fifteen twenties for the tomb of Pope Julius the Second. He never finished them. The tomb project was repeatedly downsized over forty years; these figures were always going to be left behind. They're some of the most arresting unfinished sculptures in the world. Michelangelo talked about sculpting as the act of liberating a figure already imprisoned in the stone. These figures, half-emerged, are the doctrine made literal.

The Saint Matthew, in a similar room. Also unfinished. Carved around fifteen-oh-six. The figure twists in marble, half-trapped. You can see the chisel marks. The work shows you Michelangelo's actual technique — the marks he made before refining, the rough planes he chiseled out before smoothing. This is process exposed.
