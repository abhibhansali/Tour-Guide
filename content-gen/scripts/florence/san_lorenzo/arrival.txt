You're walking up to the Basilica of San Lorenzo, one of the oldest churches in Florence and the family parish of the Medici. The exterior is famously plain — the front of the church is bare rough stone, never finished. Michelangelo designed a Carrara marble facade for it in fifteen sixteen, but it was never built. The Medici ran out of money, ran out of attention, or got distracted by other projects. The church has stood with its unfinished face for five centuries. The contrast between the rough exterior and the elegantly Renaissance interior is part of the experience.

A few orienting points. The whole San Lorenzo complex includes four distinct things:

The Basilica itself, the main church. Brunelleschi designed the interior starting in fourteen-twenty, financed by Giovanni de' Medici. The space is the first major Renaissance church interior in the world — flat ceiling, repeated columns, mathematical proportions. We'll talk about why this mattered in a minute.

The Old Sacristy, also by Brunelleschi, completed in fourteen twenty-eight. A small chapel off the left transept, designed as a Medici family tomb. The first space anywhere built in pure Renaissance architectural language. Donatello did the bronze doors and the decorative reliefs.

The New Sacristy, by Michelangelo, started in fifteen twenty-one and worked on intermittently for the next twelve years. Another Medici tomb chapel, on the opposite side from the Old Sacristy. The tombs of Lorenzo and Giuliano de' Medici are here, with Michelangelo's famous allegorical figures of Day, Night, Dawn, and Dusk. This room is arguably Michelangelo's greatest architectural composition.

The Chapel of the Princes, attached to the back of the basilica complex. A vast Baroque mausoleum begun in sixteen-oh-four and never quite finished — the Medici grand dukes' lavish memorial to themselves, with walls inlaid in colored stone and semi-precious marble. The contrast with Michelangelo's adjacent New Sacristy is stark and instructive.

The Laurentian Library, on a separate ticket. Michelangelo's most radical architectural design — a small reading room with a monumental staircase that breaks all the classical rules. Architects argue about it.

Plan two hours for everything. Three if you want to slow down. The complex includes one of the densest concentrations of major Renaissance art and architecture in any single city block in Italy. You're inside the family church of the people who paid for the Renaissance.
