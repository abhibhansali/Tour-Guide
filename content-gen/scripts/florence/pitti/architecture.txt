Let me walk you through the building's architecture, because the Pitti's massive rusticated facade tells you what kind of statement Luca Pitti was making.

Look at the front of the building from the piazza. The lower walls are made of huge rusticated stone blocks — irregular, rough-hewn surfaces with prominent grooves between them. This rustication is unusual on a residence. Roman palaces used smoother, more refined stonework. Florentine palaces, including the Medici and Strozzi houses, used flat stone above a rusticated base. The Pitti's lower stories are rusticated all the way up. The effect is deliberately heavy, almost military — a private fortress instead of a refined townhouse. Luca Pitti wanted his palace to look like it could withstand a siege. Architecturally, it's a statement of bourgeois ambition trying to look aristocratic.

The proportions are imposing. The current facade is two hundred and fifty feet long and one hundred and fifteen feet tall — nine windows wide on the original section, with extended wings on either side. The window heights gradate down as the floors rise — taller windows on the lower level, shorter ones on the third. The visual effect is to make the building look more horizontal and grounded than its actual proportions would suggest.

Inside, the Medici-era courtyard at the back, by Bartolomeo Ammannati, is the architectural heart of the palace. It's a three-sided enclosure with a fourth side facing the Boboli Gardens, a deliberate transition from the formal palace into the more theatrical garden landscape. Ammannati's design includes three orders of pilasters — Doric below, Ionic in the middle, Corinthian on top — strictly following the classical formula laid down by Vitruvius and refined by Italian Renaissance theorists.

The grottoes and fountains at the back of the courtyard, where the palace meets the gardens, were designed by Bernardo Buontalenti in the late fifteen seventies. The Buontalenti Grotto is one of the strangest pieces of Renaissance landscape architecture anywhere. The exterior looks like a normal grotto built into a hillside. Inside, the walls and ceiling drip with carved stalactites, painted shepherds, statues that emerge from rocks, and figures whose limbs blur into the surrounding stone. The grotto was meant to evoke a primitive natural cave, a place of pagan ritual, designed for Cosimo's son Francesco the First. Buontalenti was working at the edge of the Renaissance, anticipating Mannerism. The grotto is a small masterpiece of artificial nature.

Inside the main palace, the Palatine Gallery occupies a sequence of state rooms on the second floor. The rooms are arranged enfilade — one opening into the next, in a long axial line — so visitors walk through them as a single processional space. The decoration is gilded, frescoed, hung with paintings two or three deep on the walls in the eighteenth-century manner. The rooms are not arranged chronologically. Paintings are placed for visual coherence — a Madonna near another Madonna, a Caravaggio near another Caravaggio — rather than by date or school. This kind of hanging was standard before the late nineteenth century. The Pitti has kept it.
