You're walking up to the Pitti Palace, the largest single building in Florence and one of the most overwhelming combinations of art, architecture, and garden in any single complex in Italy. The palace was the residence of three Italian dynasties — the Pitti family, then the Medici, then the Habsburg-Lorraine, then briefly the Savoy royal family — across more than five centuries. Today it houses several museums, an enormous formal garden behind it, and roughly eight hundred works of major Italian and European painting.

The first thing to know is that this is going to take a while. A complete visit to all the Pitti museums plus the Boboli Gardens is a full day. A focused visit to the Palatine Gallery — the main painting collection — and the gardens is half a day. We'd recommend planning at least four hours.

The palace was built starting in fourteen fifty-eight by Luca Pitti, a Florentine banker who wanted his house to be more impressive than the Medici palace across the river. He spent vast sums and ended up bankrupt. His descendants sold the unfinished building to Eleonora di Toledo, the wife of Cosimo the First de' Medici, in fifteen forty-nine. The Medici turned it into their official residence, doubling the palace's size and adding the formal gardens behind. After the Medici died out in seventeen thirty-seven, the Habsburg-Lorraine dynasty inherited it. After Italian unification in eighteen sixty-five, the new Italian royal family — the House of Savoy — used it as a temporary residence when Florence was briefly the capital of the new Italy.

The collection inside is one of the densest in Europe. The Palatine Gallery, on the second floor, displays the paintings the Medici and their successors collected — Raphael, Titian, Caravaggio, Rubens, Andrea del Sarto, Botticelli, Velázquez, and many others. The Royal Apartments, in another wing, are preserved as the rooms looked when the Savoy family lived in them, complete with original furniture and tapestries.

There are also four smaller museums: a Gallery of Modern Art, a Costume Gallery, the Treasury of the Grand Dukes (jewelry and decorative arts), and the Porcelain Museum. Most visitors skip these unless they have a special interest. The Palatine Gallery is the headliner.

Behind the palace, the Boboli Gardens climb the hill of San Giorgio in formal eighteenth-century terraces. They're huge — almost eleven hectares — and full of statues, fountains, grottoes, and viewpoints. The Egyptian obelisk in the upper amphitheater is older than the gardens by a thousand years. The Buontalenti Grotto, near the lower entrance, is one of the strangest Renaissance landscape designs in Italy.

A note on strategy. Decide before you go in whether you're doing the Palatine Gallery, the Boboli Gardens, or both. The Pitti tickets are arranged in confusing combinations; the most useful is the "Palatine plus Gardens" combo. Buy that one.
