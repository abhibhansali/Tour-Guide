The Pitti Palace history is a story of one family's overreach and another family's takeover.

Luca Pitti was a wealthy Florentine banker who, in the fourteen fifties, decided he would build a house larger and more impressive than the Medici Palace. The Medici were the unofficial rulers of Florence, and Pitti was a political rival who wanted to make a statement in stone. He commissioned a design from the architect Filippo Brunelleschi — or possibly from Luca Fancelli, working from Brunelleschi's drawings; the attribution is disputed. The palace was begun in fourteen fifty-eight. It was deliberately positioned across the river from the Medici Palace, on a slight rise, dominating the view.

Pitti spent extravagantly on it. The original design was huge by Florentine standards: seven windows wide, three stories tall. But Pitti's financial empire collapsed in fourteen sixty-six, the same year his palace was reaching completion. He couldn't afford to finish the interior decorations. He died in fourteen seventy-two, broke, in his unfinished house. His descendants struggled to maintain it for the next eighty years.

In fifteen forty-nine, Eleonora di Toledo, the Spanish-born wife of Cosimo the First de' Medici, bought the palace from the Pitti family. The price was reportedly nine thousand florins — a fraction of what it had cost to build. The Medici renamed it the Pitti out of mock-respect for the bankrupt family they'd replaced.

The Medici expansion took decades. Cosimo's architect Bartolomeo Ammannati was put in charge. He doubled the palace's size by extending it sideways, added a courtyard at the back, and broke through the rear walls to create access to the hill behind, which Eleonora wanted as a private garden. Cosimo's grandson Ferdinand the First de' Medici added two further wings in the early sixteen hundreds. By the late seventeenth century, the palace was the size you see today.

Cosimo built the Vasari Corridor at the same time — the elevated walkway that connects the Uffizi to the Pitti by way of the Ponte Vecchio. The corridor was Cosimo's private route between his offices and his home, allowing him to move between the two without descending into the streets where assassins might find him.

The Medici dynasty died out in seventeen thirty-seven. The Pitti passed to the Habsburg-Lorraine, who used it as their Italian residence. Some of the most aggressive expansion of the painting collection happened under the Habsburg-Lorraine in the seventeen hundreds. They acquired Raphaels, Titians, Rubenses, deepening what was already a major collection.

After Italian unification, when Florence was briefly the capital of the new Italy from eighteen sixty-five to eighteen seventy-one, King Vittorio Emanuele the Second used the Pitti as his Florentine palace. The royal apartments preserved today are from this period.

The Italian state took ownership of the palace in nineteen-nineteen, after the monarchy fell. It became a public museum complex. It has been operating in that capacity ever since.
