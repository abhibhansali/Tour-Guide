In the Palatine Gallery, the standout works are densely clustered. Here's what I'd point at.

Raphael's portraits. The Palatine has eleven Raphael paintings, including some of his greatest. Look for the Donna Velata — a portrait of a veiled woman, around fifteen sixteen, probably his lover Margherita Luti. Look for the Madonna of the Chair, painted around fifteen fourteen, a circular composition of mother and child with John the Baptist crowding into the frame. Look for the portrait of Pope Julius the Second, the warrior pope, painted in fifteen-eleven. The portrait depicts Julius seated, looking exhausted, lost in his own thoughts. It was a revelation when Raphael painted it: nobody had shown a pope as an old man before. It set a template for papal portraiture that lasted three centuries.

Titian's portraits and the late paintings. Titian has six paintings here, including the Concert — a sensual painting of three musicians, one of them looking out at the viewer, attribution still debated between Titian and his contemporary Giorgione. Also Titian's late portrait of Pope Paul the Third with his grandsons, finished in fifteen forty-six. The painting shows the elderly pope seated, surrounded by two scheming relatives whose body language indicates conspiracy. The pope is supposed to be the subject; the dynamic between him and his grandsons is the actual painting.

Andrea del Sarto. The Palatine has the densest collection of his work anywhere. Andrea del Sarto was Vasari's teacher, an early sixteenth-century Florentine, and probably the most refined draftsman of his generation. His Madonna of the Harpies, painted in fifteen seventeen, is a small marvel. Look at the way the Madonna stands on a low pedestal supported by tiny harpies, with two saints flanking her in profile. The composition is geometric, the colors muted, the faces deeply expressive.

Caravaggio's Sleeping Cupid. A small painting, around sixteen-oh-eight, of a child sleeping with his head resting on his arm. Caravaggio painted it in Malta, where he'd fled while on the run from a murder charge in Rome. The figure is essentially a still life — the boy is asleep, motionless, isolated against a dark background. It's an unusual Caravaggio: quiet, intimate, with none of his usual dramatic violence.

Rubens's late landscape and group portrait. The Palatine has Rubens's Consequences of War, painted in sixteen thirty-eight, a vast allegorical scene of Mars being restrained by Venus while war goes on around them. It was painted right after the Thirty Years War devastated Northern Europe. The painting is large, dynamic, full of figures, and personally felt — Rubens had lost friends and patrons to the war. It's one of his most direct political statements.

The Royal Apartments. After the Palatine, walk through the Royal Apartments — preserved rooms from when the Savoy royal family used the palace. The furniture is original. The tapestries are original. The bedrooms are arranged exactly as they would have been used. It's a glimpse of how royalty actually lived in the late nineteenth century: not luxurious by Versailles standards, but elegant and personal.

In the gardens, find the Egyptian obelisk in the upper amphitheater. It's a real Egyptian obelisk, brought from Luxor in seventeen ninety, three thousand years old. The Medici grand dukes collected antiquities. The garden's most prized object is hidden in plain sight, in a place most visitors walk past without looking up.
