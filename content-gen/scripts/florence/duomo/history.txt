Florence in the late twelve hundreds was a rich city — wool, banking, dye trades — and like every rich Italian city, it wanted a cathedral that proved the point. The old church on this site, Santa Reparata, was too small and outdated. In twelve ninety-six, the city commissioned a new cathedral from the architect Arnolfo di Cambio, with instructions to make it grander than anything in Tuscany. Arnolfo designed the basic plan: a long nave, three apses at the east end, and an enormous octagonal crossing.

Construction started fast and then slowed down. Arnolfo died in thirteen-oh-two. Giotto took over and built the bell tower, the Campanile, until his death in thirteen thirty-seven. The Black Death hit in thirteen forty-eight and killed roughly half the city. The cathedral kept getting wider and longer, but progress on the structure was glacial. By the early fourteen hundreds, after a hundred years of work, the nave was finished, the apses were finished, the bell tower was finished — and the octagonal hole at the crossing was still uncovered. They had built a cathedral with an open roof at the center. Florence couldn't figure out how to dome it.

The hole was a hundred and forty-three feet across. The Romans had built the Pantheon, which was wider, but that used a hemisphere of concrete poured on giant wooden centering. Nothing similar had been attempted since. Florence didn't have enough timber in all of Tuscany to build scaffolding that big.

In fourteen-eighteen, the wool merchants' guild — which financed the cathedral — opened a public competition for the dome. The two leading entries came from a goldsmith named Lorenzo Ghiberti, who'd just spent twenty years on the Baptistery doors, and an architect named Filippo Brunelleschi, who'd been studying Roman ruins obsessively and had ideas about double-shell construction.

Brunelleschi won, with help from a famous demonstration. The story is that he proposed solving the engineering problem and refused to explain how. When the commission insisted on details, Brunelleschi asked them to stand an egg on a marble slab. They couldn't. He cracked the egg's bottom on the slab and stood it up. They said any of them could have done that if they'd known how. Brunelleschi said exactly the same about the dome.

He spent the next sixteen years building it. The dome went up without internal scaffolding, layer by layer, supporting itself as it rose. Brunelleschi invented a hoist for the bricks, a herringbone bonding pattern for stability, and methods of measuring the dome's slope that nobody had seen before. When he died in fourteen forty-six, it had been finished for ten years. The lantern on top was completed by his pupil from drawings he'd left.

Brunelleschi is buried inside the cathedral, in the crypt. His tomb is simple. The dome is his epitaph.
