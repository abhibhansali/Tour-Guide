A few stories that bring this building to life.

Brunelleschi versus Ghiberti. The competition for the dome in fourteen-eighteen pitted two Florentine geniuses against each other, and the rivalry lasted decades. Initially the commission tried to make them work together. Brunelleschi pretended to be sick whenever Ghiberti showed up at the construction site, then resumed work as soon as he left. The strategy was deliberate: Brunelleschi knew Ghiberti was a craftsman, not an engineer, and that the dome's technical problems would expose him. Eventually the commission realized Brunelleschi was the one actually solving the problems and quietly reduced Ghiberti's role. The two men barely spoke for the rest of their lives.

The Pazzi Conspiracy. On April twenty-sixth, fourteen seventy-eight, during High Mass in this cathedral, a rival banking family called the Pazzi tried to assassinate the Medici brothers, Lorenzo and Giuliano. Two priests stabbed Giuliano to death in front of the altar. Lorenzo escaped, wounded, into the sacristy and barricaded the bronze doors. The conspirators were caught the same week. Lorenzo took revenge: the Pazzi were executed publicly, hanged from the windows of the Palazzo Vecchio, and Botticelli was hired to paint frescoes of the dead conspirators on the building's walls. The blood on the cathedral floor was real and recent, and the city was reminded of it for decades. Lorenzo de' Medici, the surviving brother, became known as Lorenzo the Magnificent.

Galileo's lamp. There's a story, told by Galileo's biographer, that the young Galileo was sitting in the cathedral as a student watching a bronze lamp swing on a long chain from the ceiling. He noticed that the lamp's swing took the same amount of time regardless of how widely it swung — a small swing took as long as a large one. This observation became the foundation for his work on pendulums and on the measurement of time. The lamp he supposedly watched is still in the cathedral. Whether he actually had the insight here or invented the story later — biographers are split — is uncertain. But the lamp is real, and you can see it.

The unmarked tomb. Until nineteen seventy-two, no one knew exactly where Brunelleschi was buried. His tomb plaque was set in the floor, but the actual burial site had been lost. In that year, excavations under the cathedral revealed a small unmarked vault containing his skeleton, identified by surrounding objects and by skeletal analysis matching contemporary portraits. The discovery quietly settled four hundred years of speculation. He's now displayed in the crypt, which you can visit for a small fee. His remains rest, more or less directly, under the dome he built.
