You've just walked into one of the most surprising visual moments in Europe. The Florence Cathedral — properly called Santa Maria del Fiore, Saint Mary of the Flower — rises out of a tight medieval square that doesn't really have room for it. The building is too big for its piazza. That's part of the experience: you turn a corner and the whole pink, green, and white façade is suddenly five feet from your face.

The cathedral is the fourth-largest church in the world. The full structure includes three separate buildings: the cathedral itself, the bell tower beside it called the Campanile, and the smaller octagonal building across from the entrance, which is the Baptistery. They were built across two and a half centuries and don't entirely match. That's fine. Florence has never been about coherence.

The façade you're looking at, the dense decoration of marble panels, is younger than it looks. Most of the cathedral was built between twelve ninety-six and fourteen thirty-six, but the front was left rough and unfinished for centuries. Several Renaissance facades were proposed and rejected. The pink, white, and green Gothic façade you see now was actually designed by an architect named Emilio De Fabris and finished in eighteen eighty-seven. It's only about a hundred and forty years old. So your first impression of Florence Cathedral is, in a real sense, a Victorian impression of what a medieval cathedral should look like.

Now look up. The dome — red-tiled, vast, off-center because it sits above the back third of the building — is the original. Filippo Brunelleschi designed and built it between fourteen-twenty and fourteen thirty-six. It is the largest masonry dome ever built, anywhere, before or since. No one had built a dome that wide since the Pantheon, fourteen centuries earlier. Brunelleschi figured out how. We'll talk about exactly how soon.

Across the piazza, the Baptistery is older than everything else — its core is from the eleventh century, the marble facing from the twelfth. Every Florentine for seven hundred years has been baptized in it, including Dante in twelve sixty-six. The east-facing bronze doors, with ten Old Testament scenes in gilded relief, are by Lorenzo Ghiberti — what you see is a high-quality copy. Michelangelo called them "fit to be the Gates of Paradise."

Three buildings, three different eras, all sitting in a square that can barely hold them. Welcome to the center of Florence.
