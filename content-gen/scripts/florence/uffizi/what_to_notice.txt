In a museum this dense, you have to be selective. Here are the works I would not skip.

The three Madonnas — Cimabue, Duccio, Giotto. The very first room. Three enormous panel paintings of the Virgin enthroned, each about thirteen feet tall, painted within thirty years of each other in the late twelve hundreds. They mark the transition from Byzantine icon painting to recognizably Italian painting. Cimabue's Madonna is still essentially Byzantine — flat, gilded, stylized. Giotto's, painted just a generation later, has actual three-dimensional space, real weight, a Madonna who could exist in physical reality. It's the beginning of Western painting as we now understand it.

Botticelli's Birth of Venus and Primavera. They hang in adjacent positions in the central gallery. The Birth of Venus shows Venus emerging from a shell, blown by Zephyr toward the shore where a nymph waits with a robe. The Primavera shows a complex mythological allegory — Mercury, the three Graces, Venus, Flora, Chloris, Zephyr — set in a flowered orange grove. The Primavera is the harder painting; nobody fully agrees on what it means. Look at the faces. Look at the colors. Both paintings are tempera on wood, which is why the colors are so soft and matte. Linger.

Leonardo's Annunciation. A serene early work by Leonardo, painted around fourteen seventy-five when he was in his twenties. The angel Gabriel approaches Mary in a garden. Look at the angel's wings — anatomically careful, almost a bird's. Leonardo was already studying birds in flight. The landscape in the background is Leonardo's signature blue-tinted distance.

Michelangelo's Doni Tondo. The only major Michelangelo painting on a flat surface (he hated painting and preferred sculpture). It's a circular composition, the Holy Family with John the Baptist, with five nude male figures behind in unclear classical poses. Painted around fifteen-oh-six. The colors are vivid — pink, yellow, blue — and the painting is preserved in its original frame, which Michelangelo helped design.

Raphael's Madonna of the Goldfinch. A late work, around fifteen-oh-six, where the Madonna and the young John the Baptist watch a young Christ holding a goldfinch. The painting was badly damaged in an earthquake in fifteen forty-seven, broken into seventeen pieces, and reassembled. You can still see the joins if you look carefully.

Caravaggio's Medusa, Bacchus, and Sacrifice of Isaac. Three Caravaggios in the same room. The Bacchus is the most decadent — a beautiful young man offering a glass of wine, with fruit going bad in the foreground, suggesting that all this beauty is already decaying. The Sacrifice of Isaac is the most dramatic — God's angel grabs Abraham's wrist mid-strike. Caravaggio's lighting is the lesson here: a single source, harsh, theatrical, picking out exactly what he wants you to see.

In the long Vasari corridor, even if it's closed, walk to the windows on the western side. The view down the Ponte Vecchio at sunset is one of the best in Florence. The river goes amber. The bridge goes amber. You're inside one of the great architectural projects of the Renaissance, looking out at another.
