The history of the Uffizi is the history of how Italian art got organized — and saved.

The Medici family had been collecting art since the early fourteen hundreds. Lorenzo the Magnificent, in the late fourteen seventies, kept a sculpture garden in the family palace and let his protégés — including a teenage Michelangelo — study from his collection. When Lorenzo died in fourteen ninety-two, the collection was already substantial. Subsequent generations added to it through patronage, marriages, and inheritance.

Cosimo the First, who came to power in fifteen thirty-seven and consolidated Florentine power, turned art collecting into a state activity. He hired Vasari as his court painter, art critic, and architect. The Uffizi building, begun in fifteen sixty, was Vasari's design for the dukal administration. The galleries upstairs were Cosimo's private gallery.

The crucial moment came in seventeen thirty-seven, when the last Medici grand duke — Gian Gastone — died without an heir. The Medici line ended after three hundred years. Florence passed to the Habsburg-Lorraine dynasty. Most observers expected the new rulers to take the collection back to Vienna.

Instead, the last Medici, Anna Maria Luisa, signed a document called the Patto di Famiglia — the Family Pact — with the incoming Habsburg-Lorraine ruler. The terms: the entire Medici art collection would remain in Florence permanently, inalienable, the property of the city forever. Anna Maria Luisa was sixty-five years old, unmarried, and the last of her line. She used her final negotiating leverage to bind the next dynasty to keep the art where it was.

The pact has held for nearly three hundred years. The Uffizi is the result.

The Habsburg-Lorraine rulers actually expanded the public access — opening it to selected visitors in the seventeen forties, formally to the public in seventeen sixty-nine. Then came Napoleon. French troops occupied Florence in seventeen ninety-six and shipped about sixty paintings to Paris. The Lorenzo Medici Pact prevented worse pillaging; the legal claim of inalienable city ownership held up even under French rule. After Waterloo in eighteen-fifteen, the paintings came back.

The next great threat was World War Two. As Germany retreated through Italy in nineteen forty-four, the Uffizi's curators moved much of the collection to country villas in Tuscany. German troops occupied the building. Allied bombers targeted the railway lines nearby. The collection survived. A few works were stolen by German officers and recovered by the Monuments Men team after the war.

The most recent damage was a Mafia car bomb in May nineteen ninety-three, set near the back of the Uffizi to intimidate the Italian state. Five people died. Several paintings were damaged. The collection has since been steadily restored.

What you're looking at is, in essence, the world's oldest functioning public museum, sustained by a Medici woman's last act and three centuries of careful preservation.
