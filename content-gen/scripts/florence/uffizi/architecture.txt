A few notes on the building itself, because Vasari's architecture is more cleverly designed than most visitors notice.

The Uffizi is a U-shape opening toward the Arno river. The two long parallel wings face each other across a narrow piazza, and the short bottom of the U closes the piazza near the Palazzo Vecchio. From the river, looking up the piazza, you see a long perspective view straight to the tower of the Palazzo Vecchio. That view was deliberate. Vasari wanted the offices to feel architecturally subordinate to the seat of government, but elegant enough to demand their own space. The result is one of the most theatrical urban spaces in Florence.

The two wings each have three stories. The ground floor and first floor were originally the working offices. The top floor — what's now the gallery — was supposed to be a roof terrace and loggia. Cosimo's son Francesco enclosed it and converted it to a private gallery in the fifteen eighties.

Walk along the gallery's long river-facing corridor. The ceiling is decorated with grotesques — small painted figures, masks, mythological creatures, framed in elaborate scrollwork — painted in the late fifteen hundreds. The style is called grottesche because the painters had been studying Roman wall paintings excavated from underground "grottoes" in the late fifteenth century. The decorative tradition was new at the time. The Uffizi corridor is one of the great examples.

Look at the windows. Each one has a marble bust over it — Florentine and Tuscan worthies, mostly from Cosimo's time. The busts are nearly all by sixteenth-century sculptors. Together they form a who's-who of Tuscan history.

The Tribuna — a small octagonal room near the middle of the gallery — was Francesco's private cabinet of curiosities. The walls are lined in red velvet. The cupola is studded with mother-of-pearl. Inside, an ornate collection of sculptures and small paintings is arranged around the perimeter. The Tribuna was the original "museum within the museum." For three hundred years, visitors who reached this room felt they had seen the gallery's heart.

The Vasari Corridor — the elevated walkway connecting the Uffizi to the Pitti Palace across the Arno — was added in fifteen sixty-five. Cosimo wanted to be able to move between his offices and his private residence at Pitti without descending to the street. The corridor runs along the top of the Ponte Vecchio, then through a few buildings, and over a church. About a kilometer total. Lined with portraits and self-portraits of artists, donated over centuries. Closed to the public for the past several years pending restoration; reopening expected. Check before your visit.

The recent renovations of the Uffizi — additions of new galleries on the lower floors, restorations of ceilings, new climate control — have continued for the past two decades. The building you're walking through is in some sense a permanent construction project. Italy has decided the Uffizi can never be finished.
