You're entering the Uffizi Gallery — one of the oldest art museums in the world and, by most reasonable measures, the most concentrated collection of Italian Renaissance painting anywhere. The collection covers roughly four hundred years of Italian art, from the late Middle Ages through the early baroque, and it includes works by Botticelli, Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael, Titian, Caravaggio, and dozens of others whose names you would recognize.

The building wasn't built to be a museum. It was originally the administrative offices of the grand-ducal government — uffizi means "offices" in Italian — designed by Giorgio Vasari in fifteen sixty for Cosimo the First de' Medici. The Medici had centralized Florentine government and needed a single building to house the magistrates, accountants, and clerks who ran it. Vasari's building is a long U-shape with two parallel wings facing each other across a narrow piazza that opens to the Arno. The Medici collected art aggressively across the fifteen hundreds, and as the family vault filled, they began hanging the best pieces on the upper floor of the offices. By fifteen-eighty-one, Cosimo's son Francesco the First had turned the entire top floor into a private gallery. By the seventeen sixties, the gallery was opening to selected scholars and visitors. In seventeen sixty-nine it was opened to the general public — making it the oldest continuously-operating public art museum in the world.

The route through the museum is essentially a long corridor running around the U-shape, with rooms branching off each side. You'll be funneled along this corridor in roughly chronological order, starting with thirteenth-century altarpieces by Cimabue and Giotto, moving through the early Renaissance, peaking at the Botticellis in the central rooms, and emerging into Caravaggio and the seventeenth century by the end.

Plan three hours for a careful visit; longer if you stop in front of every major work. The crowds are heaviest from mid-morning to mid-afternoon. The room with Botticelli's Birth of Venus is the most crowded; the rooms with Renaissance maps and Vasari's frescoes are often nearly empty.

A few orienting notes. The corridor running along the river — the long arm that faces the Arno — is one of the great viewing spaces of any museum, with windows opening onto the Ponte Vecchio. Don't miss the views. The Vasari Corridor, the famous private passageway connecting the Uffizi to the Pitti Palace, runs over the Ponte Vecchio. It's been closed for restoration for years; check whether it's open during your visit.

The first room of altarpieces sets the tone. Three enormous Madonnas by Cimabue, Duccio, and Giotto, painted within thirty years of each other in the late twelve hundreds. They are the beginning of Italian painting as we know it. We'll point at the rest as we go.
